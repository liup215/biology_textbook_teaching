Vegetative Growth and
Organogenesis: Primary
Growth of the Plant Axis

As we discussed in Chapter 17, developmental programming

in the seedling ensures functional establishment of a
photosynthesizing plant capable of taking up nutrients and

water from the soil. In the next phase of growth, undifferentiated
cells in juvenile plants adopt new fates to generate specialized cell
types or organs that enhance competition for resources under
variable environmental conditions. The pattern of differentiation
and growth results in a huge diversity of organ shapes and
functions.

In this chapter we discuss the developmental processes by
which plants transition from the seedling state, expand on their
primary axis, and begin production of determinate lateral
organs such as leaves. We begin by considering the basic features
of cell division and initiation of processes that lead to differentia-
tion in the root and shoot apical meristems, and then discuss leaf
development. We examine blade formation in simple leaves, which
involves the marginal expansion of leaf tissues, differentiation
into adaxial and abaxial domains, and morphogenesis along the
proximal–distal axis. We also discuss how compound leaves are
produced by variations of these developmental pathways. Finally,
we discuss the gene networks and hormonal signals that control
development of the specialized cells of the epidermis and
vascular tissue.

The regulated formation and outgrowth of indeterminate
branch systems, as well as secondary growth in perennial plants,
will be discussed in Chapter 19.

 18.1  Meristematic Tissues: Foundations

for Indeterminate Growth

Plant development exhibits a remarkable degree of plasticity,
which can be largely attributed to specialized tissues called
meristems. A meristem can be broadly defined as a region that
contains cells that divide to both replicate themselves and produce
founder cells for the differentiated plant body. Several types of
meristems contribute to the vegetative development of plants.

The root and shoot apical meristems
use similar strategies to enable
indeterminate growth
Although it might seem difficult to imagine two parts
of a plant more different than a shoot and a root, the
RAM and SAM function in similar ways. Both contain
a spatially defined cluster of cells, termed initials, that
are distinguished by their slow rate of division and un-
determined fate. As polarized patterns of cell division
displace some initials to the meristem edge, they take on
various differentiated fates that contribute to the radial
and longitudinal organization of the root or shoot and
to the development of lateral organs.

Both the RAM and the SAM must balance the produc-
tion of new cells with the ongoing recruitment of cells
into differentiated tissues to maintain a constant size. Is it
possible that common aspects of RAM and SAM behavior
can be traced to similar underlying mechanisms? How are
these mechanisms regulated to maintain the characteristic
organizations of the shoot and root and to enable adaptive
growth responses to a range of different environments? Do
the distinct patterns of growth and organogenesis in root
and shoot impose special requirements on RAM and SAM
function? To address these questions, Sections 18.2 and 18.3
discuss basic features of the RAM and the SAM as well as
examples of genetically defined signaling pathways that
contribute to their establishment and maintenance.

 18.2 The Root Apical Meristem

Many aspects of root growth reflect adaptations to a de-
manding environment. Roots, which anchor the plant
and absorb water and mineral nutrients from the soil,
display complex patterns of growth and tropisms that
allow them to explore and exploit a heterogeneous envi-
ronment laden with obstacles. Although cells produced
by the RAM divide, differentiate, and elongate as they
are displaced away from the tip, lateral outgrowths such
as root hairs (see Chapter 17) and lateral branches (see
Chapter 19) emerge farther away from the root tip in
regions where cell elongation is complete. This spatial
separation, which helps prevent damage to lateral or-
gans from shearing forces, affords a useful opportunity
to focus on just those processes at the root tip that serve
to maintain a set of initials and regulate their division

The root tip has four developmental zones
The basic features of root development can best be de-
scribed by first distinguishing zones within the root with
distinct cellular behaviors. Although it is impossible to
define their boundaries with absolute precision, the divi-
sion of the root into the following zones provides a use-
ful spatial framework that is relevant to our discussion
of the underlying mechanisms (Figure 18.1).

 ● The root cap occupies the most distal part of the root.
It represents a unique set of initial derivatives that are
displaced distally away from the meristematic zone.
The differentiated products of these divisions cover
the apical meristem and protect it from mechanical
injury as the root tip is pushed through the soil.
Other functions of the root cap include perception of
gravity, to enable gravitropism (see Chapter 17), and
the secretion of compounds that help the root pene-
trate the soil and mobilize mineral nutrients.

 ● The meristematic zone lies just under the root

cap. It contains a cluster of cells that act as initials,
dividing with characteristic polarities to produce
cells that divide further and differentiate into the
various mature tissues that make up the root. Cells
surrounding these initials have small vacuoles and
expand and divide rapidly.

 ● The elongation zone is the site of rapid and exten-
sive cell elongation. Although some cells continue
to divide while they elongate within this zone, the
rate of division decreases progressively to zero with
increasing distance from the meristem.

 ● The maturation zone is the region in which cells ac-
quire their differentiated characteristics. Cells enter
the maturation zone after division and elongation
have ceased, and in this region cell outgrowths
called root hairs and lateral organs such as lateral
roots may begin to form. Differentiation may begin
much earlier, but cells do not achieve their mature
state until they reach this zone. Lateral root devel-
opment is described in detail in Chapter 19.

Depending on the species, these four developmental
zones can occupy little more than the first millimeter
of the root or extend over a longer portion of the distal
regions of the root.

 18.3 The Shoot Apical Meristem

Like the root apical meristem, the shoot apical meristem
(SAM) first forms during embryogenesis and maintains
a group of undetermined cells. However, there are sig-
nificant differences between the two meristem types in
how the descendants of those cells become incorporated
into organs. Whereas lateral root initiation occurs well
back of the root tip (see Chapter 19), leaves and associ-
ated axillary branches form close to apical initials in the
shoot. In place of the root cap that protects the apical
initials of the root, young leaf primordia overlap and
enclose the shoot tip (Figure 18.4).

The term shoot apical meristem refers specifically to
the initial cells and their undifferentiated derivatives,
but excludes adjacent regions that contain cells fully
committed  to  particular  developmental  fates.1  The
moreinclusive term shoot apex (plural apices) refers
to the apical meristem plus the most recently formed
leaf primordia. The size, shape, and organization of the
SAMvary according to plant species, developmental
stage, and growth conditions. Cycads have the largest
SAM among vascular plants, measuring over 3 mm in
diameter; at the other extreme, the SAM of Arabidopsis
is less than 50 µm in diameter and contains only a few
dozen cells. Within a given species, significant varia-
tions in SAM size can also occur over time, and SAM
shapes may range from flat to mounded. Some of these

We have discussed maintenance mechanisms in the SAM
that are similar to those in the RAM, but some unique
mechanisms are present in the SAM. For example, the pro-
duction of lateral organs close to the more pluripotent cells
of the meristem appears to require an additional level of
regulation, which is mediated by members of the KNOX
(KNOTTED 1 homeobox) family of transcription factors.
The name KNOTTED is derived from the knotlike pro-
tuberances observed in maize (corn; Zea mays) with mu-
tations in the genes encoding these transcription factors.
Subsequently, studies of the Arabidopsis shoot meristemless
(stm) mutant showed that the mutation resulted from loss
of a KNOX transcription factor required for SAM forma-
tion and maintenance. STM is present throughout almost
the entire body of the meristem, but not in the groups of
cells in flanking positions that are committed to become
leaf primordia (labeled P0 in Figure 18.7).

KNOX proteins function in part by modulating the
concentrations of the plant hormones cytokinin and
gibberellin (GA) (Figure 18.7). Applying cytokinin to
the meristem rescues meristem collapse in stm mutants,
and STM expression in the SAM activates the transcrip-
tion of genes encoding isopentenyl transferases that
are involved in the biosynthesis of cytokinin. These
observations support the idea that STM up-regulates
cytokinin-mediated stabilization of meristems.

As well as up-regulating cytokinin synthesis, KNOX
genes also stabilize the meristem by suppressing the
accumulation of GA in the SAM. Genetic experiments
have shown that artificial activation of GA signaling
in the SAM destabilizes the meristem. In a variety of
species, KNOX gene expression directly represses tran-
scription of GA 20-OXIDASE1, an enzyme that catalyzes
the rate-limiting step in biosynthesis of the active form
of GA (see Chapter 4).

Axillary meristems form in the axils
of leaf primordia
Axillary meristems are located where the adaxial (upper)
leaf surface meets the stem. They form at the same time
as leaf primordia and remain dormant until they grow
out later in development to form branches. Initial sup-
pression of axillary meristems not only allows plants to
pattern branches in response to the environment, but
also reduces the accumulation of mutations that are
due to DNA replication. This may allow large perennial
plants such as trees to live for many years without accu-
mulating deleterious mutations.

Axillary meristem initiation involves three main
steps: correct positioning of initial cells, delineation of
the meristem boundaries, and establishment of the mer-
istem proper. Many of the same genes that control leaf
initiation and lamina outgrowth also regulate axillary
meristem initiation. Auxin biosynthesis, transport, and
signaling are all required for the initiation of axillary
meristems, and mutants defective in these pathways fail
to form new axillary meristems.

Morphologically, the leaf is the most variable of all the
plant organs. The collective term for any type of leaf on

Growth determines leaf shape
Despite the many different shapes, all leaves begin as
dome-shaped primordia on the flanks of the SAM (see
Figure 18.9A). In Chapter 17 we discussed the aniso-
tropic (directional) growth of seedling hypocotyls and
roots in nutational growth and in their responses to
environmental stimuli. Nutational growth results from
differing rates of anisotropic expansion between cell
files constrained by cell wall interactions, as described
in Chapter 2. Hypocotyl tropisms are also based on
anisotropic cellular elongation. Tropic responses in
the root involve anisotropic growth of the organ, but
also involve inhibition of anisotropic growth to achieve
organ bending. More complex combinations of regu-
lated isotropic and anisotropic growth determine the
shape of leaves (Figure 18.13).

The patterns of differential growth in leaves are de-
termined by regions of gene expression associated with
a particular identity—for example, the adaxial and ab-
axial side of leaves or different zones of the meristem.
Local cell wall alterations within these regions can then
alter growth rates or directions and create new shapes
through development. The complexity of mechanical
constraints on programmed cellular expansion in the
leaf can be further elaborated by functional differentia-
tion of cells for metabolic or reproductive specialization
and environmental interactions.

Dynamic imaging of leaves during development pro-
vides the basis for detailed models of the contributions
of cells in specific domains to leaf shape and growth
(see Figure 18.13B). Sectors of leaves that are associated
with expression of particular genes or clusters of genes
can then be measured to determine their contributions
to leaf shape and rates of growth during a particular
interval (see Figure 18.13C and D). Comparisons of ac-
tual measurements to computational models are used
to refine growth models. These are then compared to
observations of growth in mutants lacking key regula-
tory genes, as well as to imaging of gene expression pat-
terns using reporters (see Chapter 3), to understand gene
function at high resolution. This approach has become
the standard for studying growth of all plant organs.

 18.5  The Establishment of

Leaf Polarity

Leaves are patterned along three axes: from base to tip
(proximal–distal), from midline to margin (medial–
lateral), and from top to bottom (adaxial–abaxial) (see

Figure 18.12). The amount of growth along each of
these axes determines the final shape of the leaf. For
example, flat leaves grow preferentially along the me-
dial–lateral and proximal–distal axes. Cell types form
patterns along these axes: The relatively simple leaves
in Arabidopsis  contain an adaxial epidermis and a
single layer of adaxial palisade mesophyll cells, and
three abaxial layers of spongy mesophyll and an ab-
axial epidermis. But how are these patterns set up and
maintained during development? As we will see, the
patterning of these axes is interlinked and involves
shared molecular regulators.

A signal from the SAM initiates
adaxial–abaxial polarity
Because leaf primordia develop from a group of cells on
the flanks of the SAM, leaves possess inherent positional
relationships with the SAM: The adaxial side of a leaf
primordium is derived from cells adjacent to the SAM,
while the abaxial side is derived from cells farther away.
Microsurgical studies in the 1950s demonstrated that
communication between the SAM and the leaf primor-
dium is required to establish leaf adaxial–abaxial polar-
ity. For example, a transverse incision dividing the SAM
from the primordium initial (I) caused the initial to devel-
op radially without forming any adaxial tissue (Figure
18.14A). The resulting “leaf” was cylindrical and con-
tained only abaxial tissues (it was abaxialized). However,
two marginal incisions that allowed unimpeded commu-
nication between the SAM and the primordium did not
disrupt adaxial–abaxial symmetry (Figure 18.14B).

Antagonism between sets of transcription
factors determines adaxial–abaxial
leaf polarity
The first insights into the molecular basis of adaxial
and abaxial identity came from analysis of the loss-of-
function mutant phantastica (phan) in snapdragon (An-
tirrhinum majus) (Figure 18.15A). phan mutants produce
leaves with altered adaxial–abaxial symmetry, ranging
from abaxialized needlelike leaves that fail to produce
lamina, to leaves with blades with a mosaic of adaxial
and abaxial characters (Figure 18.15B). Molecular genet-
ic experiments in Arabidopsis and other species using
mutants such as phan have elucidated the mechanisms
that regulate adaxial–abaxial polarity.

A complex gene expression network specifies adaxial
and abaxial fate and keeps these two identities spatially
separate (Figure 18.16). Our current understanding is
that genes specifying adaxial fate repress genes speci-
fying abaxial fate and vice versa, producing mutually
exclusive domains on each side of the leaf. KNOX1

expression in leaf primordia is initially induced by auxin
and prevents precocious leaf differentiation, but is then
repressed in both adaxial and abaxial zones in most plant
species. Detailed gene expression studies suggest that
alongside a signal between leaf and SAM, adaxial and
abaxial patterning of the leaf is inherited from the SAM,
where Class III Homeodomain-Leucine Zipper (HD-ZIP III)
genes are expressed in the center and genes encoding
KANADI (Malayalam for “mirror”) transcription factors
are expressed at the perimeter. Leaf primordia form at
the boundary between the two and inherit the pattern.

MYB transcription factors, HD-ZIP III
proteins, and KNOX1 repression
promote adaxial identity

Arabidopsis encode similar proteins. Arabidopsis AS1
and AS2 repress KNOX1 genes on the adaxial side
of the leaf indirectly by activating genes that encode
HD-ZIP III transcription factors to, in turn, activate
WOX3 repression of KNOX1 (see Figure 18.16A). The
HD-ZIP III transcription factors derive their name
from a characteristic structure: a DNA-binding home-
odomain, a sterol-binding domain, and a leucine zip-
per protein dimerization domain. Expression of the
HD-ZIP III adaxial identity genes is normally limited
to the adaxial domains of the leaf primordia (see Figure

tissues take on adaxial characteristics such as produc-

adaxial identity is demonstrated by an additional level

 18.6  Differentiation of Epidermal

Cell Types

The epidermis is the outermost layer of cells on the prima-
ry plant body, including both vegetative and reproductive
structures. The epidermis usually consists of a single layer
of cells derived from the L1 layer, or protoderm. In some
plants, such as certain species of the Moraceae, Begonia-
ceae, and Piperaceae, the epidermis has two or more cell
layers derived from periclinal divisions of the protoderm.
There are three main types of epidermal cells found in all
angiosperms: pavement cells, trichomes, and guard cells.
Pavement cells are relatively unspecialized epidermal

cells that can be regarded as the default developmental
fate of the protoderm (see Chapter 22). Trichomes are
unicellular or multicellular extensions of the shoot epi-
dermis that have diverse forms, structures, and functions,
including protection against insect and pathogen attack,
reduction of water loss, and increased tolerance of abiotic
stress conditions. Trichome development and function are
discussed in WEB TOPIC 18.6. Guard cells are pairs of
cells that surround the stomata, or pores, that are present
in the photosynthetic parts of the shoot. Guard cells regu-
late gas exchange between the leaf and the atmosphere by
undergoing tightly regulated turgor changes in response
to light and other factors (see Chapters 6, 8, and 16). Here
we describe the development of stomata as a model sys-
tem for pattern formation and cytodifferentiation.

lineages, but really began to advance after the identi-
fication of Arabidopsis mutants with more, fewer, or
distorted stomata (Figure 18.19). The names of many of
these mutants allude to the mouthlike shape of stomata.

In eudicots, such as Arabidopsis, a population of mer-
istemoid mother cells (MMCs) is established in the de-
veloping protoderm (which will give rise to the leaf epi-
dermis). Each MMC divides asymmetrically to give rise
to two morphologically distinct daughter cells—a larger
stomatal lineage ground cell (SLGC) and a smaller mer-
istemoid (Figure 18.20). The meristemoid can undergo a
variable number of asymmetric amplifying divisions giv-
ing rise to as many as three SLGCs, with the meristemoid
ultimately differentiating into a guard mother cell (GMC),
which is recognizable because of its rounded morphology.
The GMC then undergoes one symmetric division, form-
ing a pair of guard cells surrounding a pore—the stomate
(from the Greek word meaning “mouth”). Following am-
plifying divisions of the meristemoid, the resulting SLGCs
can differentiate into pavement cells, which are the most
abundant cell type in the epidermis of a mature leaf, or
they can divide asymmetrically (spacing divisions) to
give rise to a secondary meristemoid. Although this lin-
eage is called the stomatal lineage, the ability of meriste-
moids and SLGCs to undergo repeated divisions means
that this lineage is actually responsible for generating the
majority of the epidermal cells in the leaves.

Two groups of bHLH transcription factors
govern stomatal cell identity transitions
In Arabidopsis stomatal lineage, the transition from
one cell identity to another is associated with, and re-
quires the specific expression of, one of three basic
helix-loop-helix (bHLH) transcription factors: SPEECH-
LESS  (SPCH),  MUTE,  and  FAMA  (named  after  the
Roman goddess of rumor) (see Figures 18.20 and 18.21.
Transcriptional activity regulated by SPCH drives MMC
formation and leads to the entry into asymmetric di-
vision of these cells. SPCH also regulates subsequent
asymmetric amplifying and spacing divisions. MUTE
promotes the differentiation of meristemoids into GMCs
and promotes the terminal cell division of the GMC. In
parallel, MUTE induces FAMA, which promotes differ-
entiation of stomatal guard cells and halts further divi-
sions. Arabidopsis mute mutants have amplifying divi-
sions but no stomata, while fama mutants form clusters
of thin unpaired guard cells. In addition, two related
bHLH leucine zipper (bHLH-LZ) proteins, SCREAM
(SCRM) and SCRM2, have been identified as the part-
ners of SPCH, MUTE, and FAMA. In scrm mutants, the
entire epidermis becomes stomata.

Cell-to-cell peptide signals regulate
stomatal patterning
Stomata are spaced apart by at least one cell length
to maximize gas exchange between the leaf and the
atmosphere. The spacing is regulated by cell-to-cell
communication and intrinsic factors that orient the cell
divisions (Figure 18.21A). The secreted peptides from
the EPIDERMAL PATTERNING FACTOR (EPF) fami-
ly are expressed by stomatal lineage cells. EPF2 is ex-
pressed  by MMCs and early meristemoids, while EPF1
is expressed by late meristemoids, GMCs and young
guard cells. Binding of EPF peptides to the receptor-like
kinase (RLK) heterodimer formed by ERECTA (or an
ERECTA-like protein) and TOO MANY MOUTHS re-
sults in recruitment of a BAK1/SERKs component to
activate kinase activity (see Chapter 4). EPF2 is detect-
ed by receptors in protodermal cells, while EPF1 is de-
tected in SLGCs and in GMCs. EPF1 prevents stomata
from forming in clusters or pairs by providing positional
information. EPF2 inhibits adjacent cells from entering
the stomatal lineage and promotes the formation of

pavement cells. Mutations in these pathways result in
changes in stomatal density or clustering of stomata. The
mesophyll also contributes to stomatal patterning. One
of the EPF-like peptides, EPFL9, a positive regulator of
stomatal density, is produced by the underlying meso-
phyll and released to the epidermis where it binds to an
RLK complex. Experiments have shown that the deple-
tion of EPFL9 results in a decrease in stomatal numbers,
indicating that its function is important for normal sto-
matal development.

 18.7 Venation Patterns in Leaves

The leaf vascular system is a complex network of inter-
connecting veins consisting of two main conducting tis-
sue types, xylem and phloem, as well as nonconducting
cells, such as parenchyma, sclerenchyma, and fibers. The
spatial organization of the leaf vascular system—its ve-
nation pattern—is both species- and organ-specific. Ve-
nation patterns fall into two broad categories: reticulate
venation, found in most eudicots, and parallel venation,
typical of many monocots (Figure 18.23).

Despite the diversity of leaf venation patterns, they
all share a hierarchical organization. Veins are orga-
nized into distinct size classes—primary, secondary,
tertiary, and so on—based on their width at the point
of attachment to the parent vein (Figure 18.24). The
smallest veinlets end blindly in the mesophyll. The hi-
erarchical structure of the leaf vascular system reflects
the hierarchical functions of different-sized veins, with
larger-diameter veins functioning in the bulk transport
of water, minerals, sugars, and other metabolites, and
smaller-diameter veins functioning in phloem loading
(see Chapter 11).

The question of how leaf venation patterns develop
has long intrigued plant biologists. For the leaf vascu-
lar system to carry out its long-distance transport func-
tions effectively, veins must be efficiently distributed
throughout photosynthetic tissues and a vein’s many
cell types must be arranged properly within the radial

The primary leaf vein is initiated
discontinuously from the preexisting
vascular system
In the mid-nineteenth century, the Swiss plant anatomist
Carl Wilhelm von Nägeli made a surprising discovery
while tracing the source of vascular bundles in the prima-
ry shoot. In the mature part of the stem of seed plants, the
longitudinal vascular bundles form a continuous conduct-
ing system that begins at the root–shoot juncture and ends
near the growing tips. Nägeli had assumed that the vascu-
lar system must grow upward (acropetally) from the pre-
existing vascular system to the growing tips of the shoot.
Instead, he discovered that the leaf vascular bundles,
arising from vascular precursor cells called the procam-
bium, were initiated discontinuously in association with
the emerging leaf primordia in the SAM (Figure 18.25).
From there the vascular bundles differentiated downward
(basipetally) toward the node directly below the leaf and
formed a connection to the older vascular bundle. The
portion of the vascular bundle that enters the leaf was
later called the leaf trace (see Figure 18.25).

What Nägeli had discovered was that the continu-
ous longitudinal vascular bundles in the stem are ac-
tually composed of individual leaf traces. Species may
differ in the exact course of leaf trace development, but
the basic interpretation of the seed plant shoot primary
vascular system as a sympodium of leaf traces appears
to be universal.

Auxin canalization initiates development
of the leaf trace
Several lines of evidence indicate that auxin stimulates
formation of vascular tissues. An example is the role of
auxin in regeneration of vascular tissue after wounding
(Figure 18.26A). Vascular regeneration is prevented by
removal of the leaf and shoot above the wound but can
be restored by the application of auxin to the cut pet-
iole above the wound, suggesting that auxin from the
leaf is required for vascular regeneration. As shown in
Figure 18.26B, the files of regenerating xylem elements
originate at the source of auxin at the upper cut end of
the vascular bundle, and progress basipetally until they
reconnect with the cut end of the vascular bundle below,
matching the presumed direction of the flow of auxin.
The upper end of the cut vascular bundle thus acts as
the auxin source and the lower cut end as the auxin sink.

The existing vasculature guides the growth
of the leaf trace
Microsurgical experiments have shown that the existing
vascular bundle in the stem is required for the direc-
tional development of the leaf trace procambium. Figure
18.28A shows PIN1 distribution in the apex of a tomato
plant expressing Arabidopsis PIN1 fused to GFP. The
leaf trace emerging from the leaf primordium initial
(P0) has connected to the existing leaf trace of the leaf
primordium below it (P3), as shown diagrammatically
in Figure 18.28B. However, if P3 is surgically removed,
the leaf trace from P0 connects instead to the vascular
bundle of the leaf primordium on the other side of the
stem (P2) (Figure 18.28C and D). These results suggest
that either the existing vascular bundle is serving as an
auxin sink and thus facilitating auxin canalization, or
that it is producing a different signal that guides the de-
velopment of the leaf trace.

Vascular development proceeds from
procambium differentiation
During  vein  formation,  ground  meristem  cells  dif-
ferentiate into pre-procambium cells—a stable inter-
mediate state between ground cells and procambium
cells. Pre-procambium cells are isodiametric in shape
(approximately  cube-shaped)  and  are  anatomically

indistinguishable from ground meristem cells. Cell di-
visions of the pre-procambium are parallel to the direc-
tion of growth of the vascular strand, resulting in the
elongated cells characteristic of the procambium (Figure
18.29A). This differentiation of the procambium pro-
ceeds in the basipetal direction.

Protophloem is the first vascular tissue to form from
procambium cells, and its differentiation begins at the
vascular bundle below and proceeds acropetally into the
leaf primordium. In contrast, primary xylem differentia-
tion lags behind primary phloem, is discontinuous, and
proceeds both acropetally into the leaf primordium and
basipetally toward the vascular bundle below.

Higher-order leaf veins differentiate in
a predictable hierarchical order
The hierarchical order of leaf vascularization has been
best studied in Arabidopsis. In general, vein develop-
ment and patterning progress in the basipetal direction
(Figure 18.29B). In other words, venation is generally
at a more advanced stage of development at the tip of a
developing leaf than it is at the base.

veins (orange arrows in Figure 18.29B) develops out
from the midvein and then loops acropetally toward the

This chapter has illustrated that the great variety of plant
cell types and organ shapes are controlled by common
themes. Mobile signals such as auxin and peptide hor-
mones integrate environmental and spatial cues to pat-
tern growth and differentiation. Interlocking signaling
pathways involving transcription factors, signaling pep-
tides, and hormones balance environmental inputs to
maintain characters such as meristem size and epidermal
cell spacing. As we will see in the next chapter, this inte-
gration of long distance signaling with conserved local
signaling pathways allows plants to make decentralized
decisions that conserve basic plant architecture while en-
hancing prospects for survival and reproduction.
