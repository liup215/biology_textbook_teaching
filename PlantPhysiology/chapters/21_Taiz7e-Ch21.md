Sexual Reproduction:
From Gametes to Fruits

Prior to the discovery of sexual reproduction in plants in

the late seventeenth century, seeds were thought to be
produced by an asexual, vegetative process similar to bud

formation. In the mid-eighteenth century the role of pollen in
fertilization was experimentally demonstrated, and by the late
nineteenth century the major features of the plant life cycle had
been established. The most profound difference between sexual
reproduction in plants and animals is the presence in the plant
life cycle of two entirely separate haploid individuals called the
male and female gametophytes. Strictly speaking, the stamens
and carpels of flowers are spore-producing structures rather than
sexual structures. The spores produced in flowers develop into
the male and female gametophytes, the true sexual phases of the
plant life cycle (see Chapter 1).

We begin our discussion with the development of the male
and female gametophytes, which produce the gametes. As sessile
organisms, plants depend on gravity and vectors such as wind or
insects to bring about pollination and fertilization. As we will see,
plants are not entirely passive in this process: They have evolved
complex mechanisms, both anatomical and biochemical, that
ensure outcrossing. As in the case of animals, Darwinian evolution
involves both natural selection and sexual selection. Sexual selec-
tion in plants occurs not by visual displays by males as in animals,
but by competition between male gametophytes to reach the
female gametophyte and fertilize the egg within the ovule. The
final step in the process is the development of the seed and fruit—
the structures that protect and nourish the embryo, and deliver it
to a suitable substrate on which to germinate and establish itself
as a new seedling.

Because of alternation of generations, there is a crucial
difference between plant and animal life cycles in the out-
come of meiosis. In animals, the haploid cells produced
by meiosis differentiate directly into the gametes—
sperm or egg. In contrast, the haploid cells produced by
meiosis in plants differentiate into spores—microspores
(male) or megaspores (female) (Figure 21.1) (see Chapter
3 for a review of meiosis). Spores are cells formed by
meiosis that develop into a separate multicellular gener-
ation called the gametophyte generation, rather than dif-
ferentiating directly into gametes as in animals. Specifi-
cally, the microspores and megaspores undergo mitotic
divisions to produce haploid individuals called male (or
micro-) gametophytes, and female (or mega-) gametophytes,
respectively. The male gametophytes form in the anther
of the stamen, while the female gametophyte develops
inside the ovule, or immature seed, which is contained
within the hollow base of the carpel, called the ovary (see
Figure 21.1). The presence of the haploid multicellular
gametophyte generation in the plant life cycle means
that gametes in plants are produced by mitosis rather
than by meiosis. That is, specialized cells within the ma-
ture male and female gametophytes divide mitotically

to produce the 1N gametes—the sperm and egg cells.
The male and female gametes eventually participate in
syngamy, in which one sperm cell fuses with one egg cell.
Upon being fertilized by the sperm cell, the egg cell is
transformed into the 2N zygote, the first cell of the future
embryo (see Figure 21.1 and Chapter 22).

In addition to syngamy between the male and female
gametes, a unique type of gametophytic cell fusion oc-
curs within the embryo sacs of angiosperms: A second
sperm cell fuses with the diploid central cell of the female
gametophyte (itself the result of the fusion of two hap-
loid polar nuclei) to produce the triploid primary endo-
sperm cell, which goes on to form the nutritive endosperm
tissue of the seed. The participation of two sperm cells
during fertilization, unique to flowering plants, is called
double fertilization. These two processes of cell fusion are
only possible because the cells involved (sperm, egg, and
central cell), unlike all other cells in the plant body, lack
true cell walls, which would prevent membrane fusion.

the Fertile Crescent of the Middle East, played a pivotal

 21.2  Formation of Male

Gametophytes in the Stamen

The male gametophyte is formed in the stamen of the
flower. Typically, the stamen is made up of a delicate fil-
ament attached to an anther composed of four microspo-
rangia (or pollen sacs) arranged in opposite pairs (Figure
21.2A). Each pair of microsporangia is separated from
the other by a central region of sterile tissue surrounding
a vascular bundle.

The precise sequence of microsporangium develop-
ment varies from species to species. In Arabidopsis, the
mature anther contains pollen mother cells, the cells that
undergo meiosis, surrounded by four somatic layers: the
epidermis, endothecium, middle layer, and tapetum, a
nutrient-transfer tissue that supports the development
of the sporogenic tissue. These layers are originally

derived from the three layers of the floral meristem (L1,
L2, and L3) (see Chapter 20). The L1 layer becomes the
epidermis, and the L2 layer gives rise to the archesporial
cells as well as the inner surrounding layers, as shown in
Figure 21.2B. The central region containing the arches-
porial cells is called the locule.

Pollen grain formation occurs in two
successive stages
The pollen grain is the male gametophyte of flowering
plants. Its development is temporally divided into two
sequential phases: microsporogenesis and microgameto-
genesis. During microsporogenesis, sporogenous cells
within the anther locules differentiate into pollen mother
cells (also referred to as microsporocytes), which are
the diploid cells that undergo meiosis to produce the
microspores (Figure 21.3A). The end result of the meiot-
ic process is a tetrad of haploid microspores joined to-
gether at their cell walls, which are composed largely of
callose, a (1,3)-β-glucan polysaccharide (see Chapter 2).

The second stage is microgametogenesis, the forma-

haploid microspore develops mitotically into the ma-
ture male gametophyte, composed of the vegetative (or
tube) cell and two sperm cells (Figure 21.3B). Prior to
the first mitotic division, the microspore expands sub-

large vacuole. In parallel, the microspore nucleus mi-
grates away from the vacuole to the other side of the cell,
resulting in a polarized microspore. The polarized micro-
spore then undergoes a highly asymmetric cell division

(pollen mitosis I), giving rise to a large vegetative cell
and a small generative cell (or male germ cell). In many
species, this asymmetric mitosis leads to a dramatic di-
vision of the cytoplasm, excluding all plastids from the
generative cell. Consequently the plastid genome is usu-
ally inherited through the maternal lineage.

side facing the cell wall is shared with the outer mem-
brane of the microgametophyte. As a result, the genera-
tive cell initially remains attached to the microspore cell
wall and is enclosed by a hemispherical wall of callose
that separates the generative cell from the vegetative
cell. Eventually, however, the callose layer breaks down
and the generative cell pinches off from the outer mi-
crogametophyte plasma membrane. The generative cell
then migrates to the center of the pollen grain, resulting
in a unique anatomical structure: a cell within a cell (bi-

The multilayered pollen cell wall is
surprisingly complex
The outer surfaces of pollen grain cell walls exhibit a
remarkable  variety  of  sculptural  features  that  play

important ecological roles in the transfer of pollen from
flower to flower (Figure 21.4A). Equally complex, how-
ever, are the multiple subsurface wall layers that provide
a labyrinth of internal spaces where lipids and proteins
may be deposited (Figure 21.4B).

Initiation of pollen cell wall formation begins in the
microspores immediately following meiosis. An ephem-
eral callose wall is the first of several layers to be de-
posited by the microspore onto the cell surface. This is
followed by the primexine (a precursor of the sexine), the
nexine, and finally the intine. (Note that because the mi-
crospore is the source of these layers, the innermost layer
is the last to be deposited.)

The primexine, composed largely of polysaccharide,
acts as a template that guides the accumulation of spo-
ropollenin, the main structural component of the exine,
or outer layer, which includes both the nexine and sex-
ine. While the microspores are still in a tetrad, the early
exine is assembled from sporopollenin precursors syn-
thesized and secreted by the microspores themselves.
However, once the outermost, callose walls are dis-
solved and the microspores are released from the tetrad,
most of the sporopollenin precursors are supplied by
the tapetum. The intine, or inner layer, consists primar-
ily of cellulose and pectins.

Recent studies in Arabidopsis suggest that the spo-
ropollenin polymer consists of phenolic and fatty acid–
derived constituents that are covalently coupled, similar
to lignin and suberin. In addition, most pollen grain walls
include zones where the exine is either thin or missing
(Figure 21.5A and B). These gaps in the exine where the
pollen wall is weakened, called apertures or pores, enable
the pollen tube to emerge from the pollen grain when it
germinates on a compatible stigma. Instead of exine, these
apertures are filled with pectins in the form of a hydrogel,

The vast majority of angiosperms exhibit
Polygonum-type embryo sac development
The development of the female gametophyte, or em-
bryo sac, is more complex and more diverse than that
of the male gametophyte. According to one classifica-
tion scheme, there are more than 15 different patterns of
embryo sac development in angiosperms, with mature
embryo sacs that have from 4 to more than 12 cells. The
most common pattern was first described in the genus
Polygonum (“knotweed”) and is therefore called the Po-
lygonum type of embryo sac. Polygonum-type embryo
sacs have 8 cells at maturity (sometimes referred as
3+2+3). We discuss this type of embryo sac development
next; deviations from Polygonum-type development are
described in WEB TOPIC 21.4.

Functional megaspores undergo a series
of free nuclear mitotic divisions followed
by cellularization
The nucellus gives rise to the megaspore mother cell, the
cell that undergoes meiosis to produce the megaspores.
In the Polygonum type of embryo sac, meiosis of the dip-
loid megaspore mother cell produces four haploid me-
gaspores (Figure 21.8). Three of the megaspores, usually
those at the micropylar end of the nucellus, subsequently
undergo programmed cell death, leaving only one func-
tional megaspore. Functional megaspores then under-
go three rounds of free nuclear mitotic divisions (i.e.,

mitoses without cytokinesis) to produce a syncytium—a
multinucleate cell formed by nuclear divisions. The re-
sult is an eight-nucleate, immature embryo sac. Four of
the nuclei then migrate to the chalazal pole, and the other
four migrate to the micropylar pole. Three of the nuclei
at each pole undergo cellularization without the forma-
tion of a cell wall. The three cells at the chalazal pole
are termed the antipodal cells, while the three cells at
the micropylar end of the embryo sac are called the egg
apparatus, composed of an egg cell (the female gamete
that combines with a sperm cell to form the zygote) in
the middle, flanked by two synergid cells. The remain-
ing two nuclei, called polar nuclei, migrate toward the
central region of the embryo sac, which also contains a
large vacuole. The cytoplasm and the two polar nuclei
generate a plasma membrane around them, giving rise
to a large binucleate central cell. Note that the two polar
nuclei may either remain separate until the discharge of
the male gametes into the embryo sac, or they may fuse
before fertilization to form a dipolid (2N) secondary nu-
cleus. The fully cellularized embryo sac, consisting of
seven cells and either seven or eight nuclei, represents
the mature female gametophyte or embryo sac.

There are many variations on the Polygonum-type
embryo sac. For example, antipodal cells are absent in
the order Nymphaeales, which includes the water lilies,
as well as in members of the evening primrose family
(Onagraceae). As a result, these two plant groups have
only four-nucleate embryo sacs at maturity. In many
other species, including Arabidopsis, the antipodal cells
degenerate before fertilization, whereas in members of
the grass family (Poaceae) the antipodal cells can prolif-
erate. Given the variable presence of antipodal cells in
mature embryo sacs, their function(s) during embryo sac
development remain undetermined.

Flowering Plants

Pollination in angiosperms is the process of transfer-
ring pollen grains from the anther of the stamen (the
microspore-producing structure of the flower) to the
stigma of the carpel (the megaspore-producing structure

of the flower) (see Figure 21.1). In some species, such
as Arabidopsis thaliana and rice, reproduction typical-
ly occurs via self-pollination, or selfing—that is, the
pollen and the stigma arise from the same individual.
In other species, cross-pollination, or outcrossing, is
the norm—the male parent and the female parent are
separate  sporophytes.  Many  species  can  reproduce
by either self- or cross-pollination; other species (dis-
cussed later) have various mechanisms for promoting
cross-pollination, and may even be incapable of repro-
ducing by self-pollination.

During cross-pollination, pollen may travel great
distances before landing on a suitable stigma. Typically
produced in great excess, pollen grains are dispersed by
wind, insects, birds, and mammals. The evolution of pol-
len grains as vehicles for transporting sperm cells made
the reproduction of seed plants (gymnosperms and an-
giosperms) independent of a water medium. Because pol-
lination benefits both plants and their pollinators, many
animals coevolved with plants to facilitate the process.

Successful pollination depends on several factors,
including ambient temperature, timing, and the recep-
tivity of the stigma of a compatible flower. Many pollen
grains can tolerate desiccation and high temperatures
during their journey to the stigma. However, some pol-
len grains, such as those of tomato, are damaged by heat.
Understanding how some pollen grains tolerate periods
of high temperature will help ensure our food supply as
the global climate changes.

The progamic phase includes everything
from pollen landing and tube growth to the
fusion of sperm and egg
The female gametes are well protected from the environ-
ment by ovary tissues. Consequently, to reach an unfer-
tilized egg, sperm cells must be delivered by a pollen
tube that grows from the stigma through the style and
into the ovule. The developmental stage from pollen
deposition on the stigma to the delivery of the sperm
cells to the embryo sac is called the progamic phase of
reproduction. In the Arabidopsis pistil (or gynoecium),
which is similar to those of other angiosperms, this pro-
cess can be divided into six steps (Figure 21.10).

Note that in Arabidopsis, as in many angiosperms,
the style is solid and the pollen tubes grow intrusively
through the apoplast of the transmitting tissue. Flowers
with hollow styles (e.g., Lilium longiflorum, Medicago sa-
tiva, orchids) have what are termed stylar canals. In these
species the transmitting tissue lines the inner surface of
the stylar canal and the pollen tubes grow along the
canal surface to the ovary.

Pollen tubes grow by tip growth
Following germination on the stigma, Arabidopsis pol-
len tubes penetrate through gaps in the waxy cuticle
of the papillae cell and enter the papillae cell wall (see
Figure 21.11C). Throughout their transit to the ovules,
pollen tubes of all species grow by tip growth facilitat-
ed by the exocytosis of cell wall precursors exclusively
at the tip (see Chapter 1). As noted earlier (see Figure
21.10C), the growth rate of pollen tubes in some flower-
ing plants (e.g., Tradescantia, daylily, maize) is extremely
rapid, reaching rates of about 6 µm per second in vivo.
(For comparison, tip growth in root hairs is only about
10 to 40 nm per second.) These rates make pollen tubes
among the fastest growing cells in nature. Remarkably,
an individual tube can reach up to 40 cm in length, as
occurs when a maize pollen tube grows through a strand
of silk, which is the style of maize carpels. Such rapid
rates of pollen tube growth are all the more impressive
considering that many angiosperms have solid styles,
and that pollen tube growth proceeds by the invasive
disruption of the cell wall inside the transmitting tract
(see Figure 21.6).

Growing pollen tubes restrict the cytoplasm, the two
sperm nuclei, and the vegetative nucleus to the region
located a few hundred micrometers behind the grow-
ing apical region by forming large vacuoles and callose
plugs that seal off the rear portion of the tube, which
eventually dies (Figure 21.12). At the apical end of the
pollen tube is an area known as the clear zone, from
which most large organelles, such as amyloplasts, nu-
clei, and most endoplasmic reticulum and mitochondria,
are excluded (Figure 21.13A). In the region closest to the
tip, this size exclusion phenomenon is even more strict,
and only small secretory vesicles that carry the cell wall
precursors are present. These vesicles accumulate just
beneath the tip, where exo- and endocytosis occur, and
are frequently organized in an inverted cone shape, or a
triangle when seen in section (Figure 21.13B).

The molecular basis for the formation of the clear zone
is unknown, but it has been associated with the disrup-
tion or reorganization of the actin cables at the tip. Actin
microfilament dynamics (see Chapter 1) drive cytoplas-
mic streaming following a “reverse fountain” flow (Fig-
ure 21.13C). Organelles move along actin fibers that run
along the pollen tube shank, but these fibers break up
into small, short-lived filaments in the clear zone. Conse-
quently, streaming is disrupted in the clear zone, and the
movements of secretory vesicles and small organelles in
this region resemble disorganized, Brownian-like motion.

Two major models have been proposed to explain
how pollen tubes grow toward an ovule: the mechan-
ical hypothesis and the chemotropic hypothesis. In the
mechanical hypothesis, pistil architecture dictates the
path of the tube, which follows a narrow transmitting
tract leading to the ovule (see Figure 21.10A). The trans-
mitting tract of the stigma and style spatially constrains
pollen tube growth to the correct path, and in the case
of Arabidopsis, limits the number of pollen tubes that
can take that path.

Spatial constraints of this kind could also be mediat-
ed by secreted molecules. For example, during growth
toward the ovule, pollen tubes are in intimate contact
with the components of the extracellular matrix of the
transmitting tract (or in the case of species with hollow
styles, such as lily and orchids, with secretions emanat-
ing from the transmitting tissue cells). The extracellular
matrix supplies not only nutrients that could support
the tube’s metabolic activity, but also other molecules
that are thought to have adhesive properties that help
define the spatial constraints for pollen tube growth. In
lily, for example, a small, secreted protein—stigma/style
cysteine-rich adhesin (SCA), a lipid transfer protein—is
secreted by the transmitting tract epidermis that lines
the hollow style and is involved in growth and adhe-
sion of the tube along the tract. Although experimental
evidence favors the mechanical hypothesis as the ex-
planation for pollen tube movements through most of
the style, it doesn’t explain the sharp turns pollen tubes
must make to approach and enter the micropyle.

According to the chemotropic hypothesis, pollen
tubes are guided toward the ovule by following a gra-
dient of molecular cues called chemoattractants. In all spe-
cies of animals and in early land plants (e.g., mosses),
sperm cells swim toward the egg cell by a process named
chemotaxis: The sperm are able to decode a concentra-
tion gradient of a chemoattractant and follow it to its
most concentrated source. For example, the sperm cells
of ferns are thought to be attracted to the egg-containing
archegonia by a concentration gradient of malate. A sim-
ilar mechanism is known to guide pollen tube growth,

but since pollen tubes grow instead of swim, the process
is referred to as chemotropism, a phenomenon also ob-
served in fungal hyphae and neuronal growth. Several
types of molecules have been identified that function in
pollen tube guidance, mostly from in vitro and semi–in
vivo assays (see the next subsection).

It is important to note that the mechanical and chemo-
tropic mechanisms are not mutually exclusive and can
work either by complementarity, in which chemotro-
pism is involved only in regions along the path where
the mechanical mechanism is absent, or by redundancy,
in which the two mechanisms work in tandem to ensure
successful fertilization. In addition, while the early stag-
es of pollen tube growth are regulated by sporophytic
cells in the transmitting tract, genetic analyses of Ara-
bidopsis and in vitro guidance experiments in Torenia
fournieri (discussed in the next subsection) support the
idea that chemical signals from the female gametophyte
also play critical roles during the final stages of pollen
tube growth toward the micropyle.

grow toward the embryo sac

 21.5 Selfing versus Outcrossing

Most angiosperms—more than 85%—are hermaphroditic,
and of these about half are capable of self-fertilization,
or selfing. According to one hypothesis, species capa-
ble of selfing are more likely to become established (i.e.,
“naturalized”) after long-distance dispersal. Consistent
with this rule, a positive correlation was found between
selfing ability and the extent of global naturalization.
However, selfing is also associated with inbreeding de-
pression, which is the likely explanation why so many
flowering plants have evolved mechanisms that promote
outcrossing. Outcrossing increases genetic diversity and
thereby the ability to adapt to different environmental
conditions. The most common mechanism used by an-
giosperms to prevent selfing is pollen self-incompatibility,
which we discuss later in the section. Certain features
of floral morphology or developmental timing also pro-
mote outcrossing, as when the stamens and pistils of a
bisexual flower or monoecious plant mature at different
times. Finally, the production of male-sterile (function-
ally female) individuals prevents selfing and promotes
outcrossing in plants.

Hermaphroditic and monoecious
species have evolved floral features
to ensure outcrossing
Once sex in plants was discovered, botanists initially
assumed  that  hermaphroditic  flowers  must  all  be
self-pollinating. It thus came as a surprise when at the
end of the eighteenth century it was demonstrated that,
in the majority of cases, floral morphology was optimized
for attracting insect pollinators, and that these pollina-
tors facilitated outcrossing rather than self-pollination.
Temporal and spatial features of flower morphology
were identified that prevented self-pollination in both
hermaphroditic and monoecious species. In dichogamy,
the stamens and pistils mature at different times. There
are two types of dichogamy: protandry and protogyny.
In protandrous flowers the stamens mature before the
pistils, while in protogynous flowers the pistils mature
before the stamens (Figure 21.18A). A protandrous flow-
er thus functions as a “male flower” early in its devel-
opment and as a “female flower” later in development;
in a protogynous flower the pattern is reversed. Since
individuals in a wild population are at different devel-
opmental stages at any given time, there will always be
pollen available for every pistil, and vice versa.

Another flower feature that promotes outcrossing is
heterostyly. In heterostylous species, two or three mor-
phological types of flowers, termed morphs, exist in the
same population. The flower morphs differ in the lengths
of their pistils and stamens. In one morph the stamens are
short and the pistils are long, while in the second morph it
is the reverse (Figure 21.18B). The lengths of the stamens
and pistils in the two morphs are adapted for pollination
by different pollinators or by different body parts of the
same pollinator, thus promoting outcrossing. The morph
phenotype is also genetically linked to a unique type of
self-incompatibility, such that pollen from one morph
cannot fertilize another flower on the same morph.

the wild and is of great utility in agriculture

in a wide variety of crops, where it has been exploited
in breeding programs.

drial genome rearrangements can result in fusions be-

seeds. Without male sterility, the pollen recipient has to
be manually emasculated to prevent self-pollination, a
laborious procedure. Cell ablation has been widely used
to generate male-sterile varieties of various crops (e.g.,
canola). In this technique, gene promoters specific for
anthers or tissues that pollen depends on to develop
(e.g., tapetum) are used to drive the expression of cyto-
toxic proteins, such as RNAses. These cytotoxic proteins
specifically kill the targeted cells or tissues, rendering
the adult plants male-sterile. Sown in lines alternating
with pollen producing plants, these varieties generate
hybrid seeds with the desired phenotype.

Self-incompatibility (SI) is the primary
mechanism that enforces outcrossing
in angiosperms
Floral morphology and cytoplasmic male sterility pro-
mote outcrossing in some species. However, in most her-
maphroditic species in which selfing never occurs, out-
crossing is strictly enforced by a self/nonself recognition
mechanism termed self-incompatibility (SI). SI systems
have evolved independently several times in flowering
plants, leading to a diverse array of mechanisms. SI cre-
ates a biochemical barrier that prevents self-pollination,
usually by killing pollen grains or tubes generated by
the same individual, while allowing pollination by an-
other individual of the same species.

The ability to discriminate between self and nonself is
a ubiquitous and essential function of both multicellular
and microbial species. In vertebrates, for example, recog-
nition of nonself is a major function of immune systems,
which depend on a group of genes called the major his-
tocompatibility complex (MHC), in which allelic vari-
ability, or polymorphisms, at the MHC loci facilitates
self/nonself discrimination. In plants, self/nonself rec-
ognition during sexual reproduction is mediated by the
self-incompatibility locus, S (for Self-incompatibility),
which directs the recognition and rejection of self-pollen.
This functional analogy between the MHC and the S locus
in determining the discrimination of self from nonself
has long inspired proposals regarding evolutionary ho-
mologies between animals and plants. Yet the discovery
of the first molecular mechanisms of self-incompatibility

Two distinct genetic mechanisms govern
self-incompatibility
During pollination of the stigma, and pollen tube growth
within the style and ovary, the proteins expressed by the
alleles of the SI genes determine whether the pollen or
pollen tube will be perceived as self or nonself by the
cells of the pistil. If the cells of the pollen grain (or tube)
carry the same alleles as the S haplotype of the pistil,
the pollen is perceived as self and an incompatible reac-
tion occurs that aborts tube development. If, however,
the pollen grain (or tube) expresses S haplotype alleles
that are different from those of the pistil, the pollen is
perceived as nonself, allowing pollination, tube growth,
and fertilization to proceed.

Microscopy of SI processes across many species led to
an early grouping of SI mechanisms into two main types
based on the stage at which fertilization is arrested. In
one set of families (e.g., Brassicaceae, Poaceae), pollen
grains are blocked at a very early stage, sometimes even
before hydration, and fail to germinate. In the second set
of families (e.g., Solanaceae, Rosaceae), pollen grains ger-
minate normally on the stigma, but the pollen tubes die
on their way to the ovule. These two types of SI reflect
two very different genetic mechanisms (Figure 21.19).

In sporophytic self-incompatibility (SSI), the pollen
grain’s incompatibility phenotype is determined by the
diploid genome of the pollen parent, or more precisely,
the sporophytic tissue that supports pollen formation—
the tapetum of the anther. The diploid tapetum depos-
its proteins produced by both copies of its S haplotypes
onto the surface of the pollen of SSI species. Consequent-
ly, even though each haploid pollen grain carries only
one S haplotype in its genome, its incompatibility phe-
notype reflects both S haplotypes carried by its parent.
The stigma is also sporophytic and therefore diploid,
and also contains proteins from two different S haplo-
types. If either of the S haplotypes of the pollen parent
matches either of the S haplotypes on the stigma of the
recipient plant, then rejection will occur. Since the SSI
proteins react immediately after contact on the stigma,
the SSI reactions typically block pollen grains prior to
hydration or germination. However, if the ungerminat-
ed SSI pollen is removed from the incompatible stigma
and placed on a compatible stigma, it will recover.

the SI system in Brassicaceae is regarded as sporophytic.
The pistil S-determinant is a serine/threonine receptor
kinase, called the S-locus receptor kinase (SRK), which
is located in the plasma membrane of stigma cells. SRKs
are highly variable among different S haplotypes, as ex-
pected for proteins involved in self-recognition.

During microgametogenesis, the diploid tapetum

one  from  each S  haplotype  of  the  diploid  genome
—which are incorporated into the exine, the outer layer

diffuse onto the stigma surface and penetrate the papil-
lae cell wall until they reach the plasma membrane.

membrane also contains two types of SRKs, one for
each S haplotype in the stigma. Each SRK recognizes
and binds only to its cognate SCR encoded by the same
S-locus haplotype. When this occurs, binding of SCR to
SRK causes autophosphorylation of the receptor. Phos-
phorylation of the SRK receptor then initiates a signal-
ing cascade that rapidly inhibits functions that would
normally facilitate pollen hydration and germination.
For example, pollen activation compounds or proteins
needed for secretion may be targeted for degradation.
The SSI reaction occurs even if only one of the two S
haplotypes represented in the pollen coat (as SCRs) is
present in the genome of the stigma.

 21.6  Apomixis: Asexual

Reproduction by Seed

In some species the embryo is not produced as a result
of meiosis and fertilization, but from a somatic diploid
cell in the ovule that differentiates directly into a zygote
and is therefore genetically identical to the female par-
ent. This type of asexual or clonal reproduction by seed
is known as apomixis, and the plants produced in this
way are known as apomicts. Apomixis is known to occur
in approximately 0.1% of angiosperms, including both
monocots and eudicots in more than 40 angiosperm fam-
ilies. Common examples include citrus species, mango,
dandelion, blackberry, crabapple, and the fodder grass
Panicum. The various types of apomixis are described in
WEB TOPIC 21.10.

Apomixis is not an evolutionary dead end
Because of its clonal nature, apomixis was once regarded
as an evolutionary blind alley, genetically distinct from
sexual reproduction. This hypothesis was based on the
assumption that apomixis represented an irreversible
phylogenetic branch point that would inevitably lead
to the extinction of the lineage. This view has now been

superseded by phylogenetic analyses that have shown
that apomixis is not only widely distributed in both
early- and late-branching lineages, but that it is revers-
ible as well. That is, lineages that were once apomictic
sometimes revert to obligate sexual reproduction. In a
few cases, environmental stress has been shown to in-
duce a switch from sexual reproduction to apomixis.

The genetic control of apomixis is based on the altered
expression of the same genes that control the normal
development of the nucellus and megagametophyte.
Because the vast majority of apomictic genotypes are
polyploid, it has been suggested that the evolution of
apomixis may have contributed to the fitness of at least
some polyploid species.

Elucidating the mechanism of apomixis could po-
tentially provide plant breeders with an important new
tool for improving crops. Many of our most productive
crops, such as maize, are hybrids that have been devel-
oped to take advantage of the phenomenon of heterosis.
As noted earlier, hybrid plants do not breed true and
cannot be propagated by seed. Therefore, hybrid seeds
must be generated each season by repeating the original
cross. But if apomixis were to be introduced into the F1
hybrid, the hybrid plant could produce seeds clonally,
thus avoiding the problem of the loss of heterosis in the
F2 generation. The development of methods to induce
apomixis in F1 hybrids by transforming them with the
appropriate genes offers the exciting possibility of greatly
accelerating progress in crop improvement in the future.

maternal or paternal genes
Animal breeders have known about parent-of-origin ef-
fects for thousands of years. For example, crossing don-
keys with horses produces hinnies when the horse is
the male parent and mules when the horse is the female
parent. Parent-of-origin effects are defined as pheno-
types that depend on the sex of the parent from which
the trait is inherited.

A  subset  of parent-of-origin  effects  is  caused  by
imprinted gene expression. Such genetic imprinting
may have evolved in response to selective pressure in
both plants and animals (see WEB TOPIC 21.12). Among
the first demonstrations of the imprinting of individual
genes was a study of the inheritance of maize kernel col-
oration. It was shown that whether or not a maize kernel
was a solid color or exhibited purple mottling depended
on whether the R alleles controlling mottling came from
the maternal or paternal parent. These and subsequent
studies demonstrated that the two sets of chromosomes
from the female and male parents, while containing the
same genes, differed in their developmental effects. Ge-
netic imprinting is thus the process by which alleles on
the maternal and paternal chromosomes become differ-
entially “marked” to ensure parent-of-origin-specific
expression after fertilization.

transmission from gametes to offspring of epigenetic in-
formation (“marks”) in the form of chemical modification

Whereas seeds of many species store reserves as proteins
and oils, the endosperm of cereals stores large quantities
of starch. The starchy endosperm is a unique tissue, ac-
counting for the bulk of the endosperm in cereal grains
(see Figure 21.23). The starchy endosperm of cereals also
contains storage proteins, which are deposited in protein
storage vacuoles.

Endoreduplication—that is, chromosomal replica-
tion without mitosis, resulting in high DNA content and
polyploidy (see Chapter 1)—appears to play an import-
ant role in starchy endosperm development. In maize,
for example, up to five rounds of endoreduplication may
lead to a DNA content per nucleus of 96C—that is, 96
times the amount (content) present in the haploid nucle-
us. Endoreduplication begins during reserve deposition,
and the accumulation of DNA prevents further nuclear
or cell division. The typical size of cells seems to be lim-
ited by the amount of DNA in the nucleus. Therefore,
endoreduplication enables certain cells of plants and
other organisms to expand to larger than normal vol-
umes, maximizing their capacity to store food reserves

(e.g., endosperm, up to 96C) or secretory compounds
(e.g., leaf trichomes, up to 16C).

The starchy endosperm of cereals is dead at maturity
due to programmed cell death, an event linked to the
ethylene signaling pathway. In the maize mutant shrunk-
en2, which overproduces ethylene, endosperm cell death
is accelerated.

As discussed in Chapter 17, the aleurone layer (the
outermost layer[s] of the endosperm) functions during
early seedling growth by mobilizing starch and stor-
age protein reserves in the starchy endosperm through
the production of an α-amylase, protease, and other
hydrolases in response to gibberellins produced by the
embryo. Maize and wheat have one layer of aleurone
cells, rice has one to several layers, and barley has three
layers. In cereal grains, the aleurone layer is the only
part of the endosperm that may become pigmented.
Genetic studies have identified two genes that play a
central role in regulating aleurone layer differentiation
(see WEB TOPIC 21.14).

 21.8 Seed Coat Development

By serving as the outer protective layer of the seed, the
seed coat is the first line of defense for the embryonic
plant against adverse environmental factors, both abi-
otic and biotic. However, as discussed in Chapter 17,
seed coats can also regulate dormancy, ensuring that
external conditions are favorable for growth before ger-
mination starts. Following fertilization, the seed coat
differentiates from the cells of the maternally derived
ovule integuments over a 2- to 3-week period (Figure
21.28). Cells in both layers of the outer integument and
all three layers of the inner integument undergo a dra-
matic period of growth in the first few days after fertil-
ization through both cell division and expansion. The
resulting five cell layers undergo one of four distinct
fates. Cells of the innermost layer, derived from the
ovule endothelium, synthesize proanthocyanidin fla-
vonoid compounds, also known as condensed tannins
(see WEB APPENDIX 4), which accumulate in the cen-
tral vacuole of the endothelial cells during the first
week after fertilization and later become oxidized, im-
parting a brown color to the differentiated cells (known
as the pigmented cell layer) and the whole seed coat. By
contrast, cells of the other two inner integument layers
do not appear to differentiate further, undergo early
programmed cell death, and are crushed as the seed
develops (see Figure 21.28C).

During the acquisition of desiccation
tolerance, the cells of the embryo acquire
a glassy state
Desiccation can severely damage membranes and other
cellular constituents (see Chapter 24). Mature seeds have
as little as 0.1 g water g–1 dry weight, with water poten-
tials between –350 and –50 MPa. As seeds begin to de-
hydrate, embryos accumulate sugars and a specific set
of proteins. These groups of molecules are thought to
interact to produce a glassy state. In general, a glass is de-
fined as an amorphous, metastable state that resembles a
solid, brittle material but retains the disorder and physical
properties of the liquid state. Biological glasses are highly
viscous liquids with very low molecular diffusion rates,
and therefore can participate only in limited chemical
reactions. Because nonreducing sugars such as sucrose,
raffinose, and stachyose accumulate during the late stag-
es of seed maturation, it was initially assumed that they
were primarily responsible for cellular glass formation.
However, the physical properties of sugar glasses are
significantly different from those of desiccated embryos,
which led to the hypothesis that proteins, specifically LEA
proteins, are required for glass formation in seeds.

LEA proteins and nonreducing sugars
have been implicated in seed
desiccation tolerance
Late-embryogenesis-abundant  (LEA)  proteins  are
small, hydrophilic, largely disordered, and thermo-
stable proteins that are synthesized in orthodox seeds
during mid- to late maturation and in vegetative tissues
in response to osmotic stress. They are thought to have
a range of protective functions against desiccation with
different efficiencies, including ion binding, antioxidant
activity, hydration buffering, and membrane and protein
stabilization. Since LEA proteins were first described in
the early 1980s in cotton seeds, related proteins have
been identified in seeds and pollen grains of other plant
species, as well as in bacteria, cyanobacteria, and some
invertebrates, and LEA proteins have been shown to

increase osmotolerance in transgenic yeast. The ability
of “resurrection plants” (e.g., Craterostigma plantagineum)
to survive extreme desiccation has been linked to the
accumulation of LEA proteins in the vegetative organs.
In addition, LEA proteins may play a role in the response
to freezing and salinity stress, both of which involve cel-
lular dehydration (see Chapter 15).

Most LEA proteins show a biased amino acid com-
position, resulting in high hydrophilicity, and they are
related to a group of proteins called the dehydrins. A key
feature of LEA proteins is their ability to form hydrogen
bonds with sucrose. Since sugars accumulate during
seed maturation, it is thought that LEA proteins interact
with sucrose and other disaccharides and oligosaccha-
rides to form the glassy state required for desiccation
tolerance (see WEB TOPIC 21.15).

Abscisic acid plays a key role
in seed maturation
Seeds of ABA-insensitive mutants of M. truncatula fail
to develop desiccation tolerance and are therefore recal-
citrant. The synthesis of LEA proteins, storage proteins,
and lipids is promoted by ABA, as shown by physiolog-
ical and genetic studies of cultured embryos of many
species. ABA-deficient mutants fail to accumulate these
proteins. Furthermore, synthesis of some LEA proteins,
or of related family members, can be induced in vege-
tative tissues by ABA treatment. These results suggest
that the synthesis of many LEA proteins is under ABA
control during seed maturation.

As we discussed in Chapter 4, ABA induces changes
in cellular metabolism by activating, either directly or
indirectly, a network of transcription factors. In particu-
lar, ABSCISIC ACID INSENSITIVE3 (ABI3) induces the
synthesis of storage proteins and LEA proteins through
interactions with bZIP transcription factors such as
ABI5. An analysis of the gene regulatory network in M.
truncatula seeds has shown that ABI5 genes occupy a
central position in the regulatory network and are highly
connected to LEA and desiccation tolerance genes. Thus,
ABI3 and ABI5, along with several other genes, are the
core components of the seed-specific ABA signaling
pathway that regulates survival in the dry state.

Tomato is an important model system for
studying fleshy fruit development
The pericarp of fleshy fruits is typically divided into
three layers: exocarp (skin), mesocarp (intermediate
layer), and endocarp (innermost layer) (Figure 21.34).
The soft edible part of the fruit is often the mesocarp. In
peach, pistachio, and other drupe fruits, the hardened
endocarp is referred to as the stone or pit. The stone pro-
vides a physical barrier around the seed to protect seeds
from pathogens and herbivores. Analyses of gene ex-
pression during stone development have revealed that
similar regulatory genes are induced during lignification
of the stone in peach and the pericarp in Arabidopsis.

Much of what we know about the development of
fleshy fruits has come from work on tomato (Solanum
lycopersicum), a member of the nightshade family (Sola-
naceae) (Figure 21.35A). In tomato, as in Arabidopsis, the
fruit is derived from the fusion of carpels, and the seeds
are attached to the placenta. However, unlike Arabidopsis
fruits, tomato fruits are indehiscent and the carpels remain
completely fused. In fleshy fruits, cell division is usually
followed by massive cell expansion (Figure 21.35B). In
some varieties of tomato, pericarp cell diameters may
reach 0.5 mm. About 30 quantitative trait loci (QTLs) have
been shown to control fruit size in tomato, and several
of the genes for these QTLs have been cloned. A specific
locus (Fw2) encodes a plant-specific and fruit-specific pro-
tein that regulates cell division in the fruit and therefore
affects fruit size. Two other loci, FASCIATED and LOCULE
NUMBER, tomato homologs of CLV3 and WUS respec-
tively, control locule number and fruit size.

Fleshy fruits undergo ripening
Ripening in fleshy fruits refers to the changes that make
them attractive (to humans and other animals) and ready
to eat. Such changes typically include color develop-
ment, softening, starch hydrolysis, sugar accumulation,
production of aroma compounds, and the disappear-
ance of organic acids and phenolic compounds, includ-
ing tannins. Dry fruits do not undergo a true ripening
process, many of the same families of genes that control
dehiscence in dry fruits seem to have been recruited to
new functions in ripening in fleshy fruits. Because of
the importance of fruits in agriculture and their health
benefits, the vast majority of studies on fruit ripening
have focused on edible fruits. Tomato is the established
model for studying fruit ripening, as it has proved high-
ly amenable to biochemical, molecular, and genetic stud-
ies on the mechanism of ripening.

Ripening involves changes in the color of fruit
Fruits ripen from green to a range of colors, including
red, orange, yellow, purple, and blue. The pigments in-
volved affect not only the visual appeal of the fruit but
also its taste and aroma, and are known to have health
benefits for humans. Fruits typically contain a mixture
of pigments, including the green chlorophylls; yellow,
orange, and red carotenoids; red, blue, and violet an-
thocyanins; and yellow flavonoids. The loss of the green

pigment at the onset of ripening is caused by the degra-
dation of chlorophyll and the conversion of chloroplasts
to chromoplasts, which act as the site for the accumula-
tion of carotenoids (see Chapter 1).

Carotenoids are responsible for the red color of tomato
fruits. During ripening in tomato, the concentration of ca-
rotenoids increases between 10- and 14-fold, mainly due
to the accumulation of the deep red pigment lycopene.
Fruit ripening involves the active biosynthesis of carot-
enoids, the chemical precursors for which are synthesized
in the plastids. The first committed step is the formation
of the colorless molecule phytoene by the enzyme phy-
toene synthase. In tomato, phytoene is then converted
to the red pigment lycopene through a series of further
reactions. Experiments with transgenic tomatoes have
demonstrated that silencing the gene for phytoene syn-
thase prevents the formation of lycopene (Figure 21.36).
Anthocyanins are the pigments responsible for the blue
and purple color in some berries and red in strawberry
(Figure 21.37). Anthocyanins are made through the phen-
ylpropanoid pathway; that is, they are derived from the
amino acid phenylalanine. Phenylpropanoids constitute
some of the most important sets of secondary metabolites
in plants. They contribute not only to the characteristic
color and flavor of fruits but also to unfavorable traits,
such as browning of fruit tissues via enzymatic oxidation
of phenolic compounds by polyphenol oxidases.

The causal link between ethylene and
ripening was demonstrated in transgenic
and mutant tomatoes
Ethylene has long been recognized as the hormone
that can accelerate the ripening of many edible fruits.
However, the definitive demonstration that ethylene is
required for fruit ripening was provided by experiments
in which ethylene biosynthesis was blocked by inhibit-
ing expression of either ACC synthase (ACS) or ACC
oxidase (ACO). ACS and ACO are the enzymes involved
in the second-to-last and last steps in ethylene synthe-
sis, respectively (see Chapter 4). These two steps in the
pathway are normally tightly regulated. Silencing the
genes encoding either of these enzymes using antisense
RNA constructs inhibits ripening in transgenic tomatoes
(Figure 21.38). Exogenous ethylene restores normal rip-
ening in the transgenic tomato fruits.

Further demonstration of the requirement for eth-
ylene  in  fruit  ripening  came  from  analysis  of  the
Never-ripe mutation in tomato. As the name implies, this
mutation completely blocks the ripening of tomato fruit.

Molecular analysis has revealed that the Never-ripe phe-
notype is caused by a mutation in an ethylene receptor
that renders the receptor unable to bind ethylene. These
results, together with the demonstration that inhibiting
ethylene biosynthesis blocks ripening, provided un-
equivocal proof of the role of ethylene in fruit ripening.
The role of plant hormones other than ethylene in
controlling ripening is much less well understood, al-
though auxin, ABA, and gibberellins are known to have
an effect on this important developmental process.

Climacteric and non-climacteric fruit differ
in their ethylene responses
Fleshy fruits have traditionally been  placed in two
groups as defined by the presence or absence of a char-
acteristic respiratory rise called a climacteric at the onset
of ripening. Climacteric fruits show this respiratory in-
crease, and also a spike of ethylene production imme-
diately before, or coincident with, the respiratory rise
(Figure 21.39). Apple, banana, avocado, and tomato are
examples of climacteric fruits. In contrast, fruits such as
citrus fruits, strawberry, and grape do not exhibit such
large changes in respiration and ethylene production
and are termed non-climacteric fruits.

Studying the molecular mechanism of
ripening can have commercial applications
Elucidation of the role of ethylene in the ripening of cli-
macteric fruits has resulted in many practical applica-
tions aimed at either uniform ripening or the delay of
ripening. For example, banana bunches are picked when
they are still green and hard, which helps them survive
the journey from the fields of Central and South America

to their final destinations around the world. The clus-
ters of unripe fruits are cut from the bunch, treated with
fungicide, packed in cartons, and shipped overseas. On
arrival at their destination, the bananas are placed in
temperature-controlled rooms and treated with small
amounts of ethylene gas to initiate ripening. This mir-
rors the natural ripening process but ensures that fruits
at different stages of maturity will initiate ripening at the
same time, making them easier to market.

In the case of fruits such as apples, ripening can be
delayed using controlled atmosphere (i.e., ~2.5% oxygen
and 2.5% carbon dioxide) storage and refrigeration, thus
extending the marketable period for the crop. In elite va-
rieties of tomato, the rin mutation is widely used in the
heterozygous form to slow the rate of ripening and ex-
tend shelf life. A major disadvantage of using rin mutant
is that it delays all aspects of ripening so that the fruits
often have suboptimal levels of flavor, aroma, and other
quality-associated compounds. A more effective approach
would be to target individual ripening processes, for in-
stance slowing softening to extend shelf life, without det-
rimental effects on color and flavor. This goal is becoming
a reality as access to the tomato genome sequence is mak-
ing it easier to identify the genes underlying the complex
traits controlling individual aspects of fruit quality.

It is also possible to manipulate the nutritional quality
of fruit. For example, anthocyanins, like carotenoids, are
thought to protect against heart disease and certain can-
cers, as they are strong antioxidants that can scavenge
excessive damaging ROS. Anthocyanin levels in fruits
can be manipulated by transgenic approaches, even to
the extent of introducing high levels of these compounds
in  tomato  flesh,  where  they  do  not  normally  occur
(Figure 21.42). A greater understanding of the molecu-
lar determinants of other aspects of fruit development,
such as the production of volatiles, will presumably offer
other opportunities to improve fruit quality.

 Summary

Plants exhibit alternation of generations, where diploids
tend to be dominant but haploids produce the gametes.
Genetic diversity is encouraged through outcrossing,
which is enabled by vectors such as wind or insects,
while inbreeding is minimized by active preventive mech-
anisms in the plant. The new diploid generation develops
in the seed or fruit, which ripens and becomes attractive
to vectors that disperse seeds.

21.1  Development of the Male and Female

Gametophyte Generations

 ■ Plants undergo both a diploid and a haploid gen-
eration in order to make gametes and reproduce
(Figure 21.1).

 ■ Diploidy allows individuals to mask deleterious

recessive alleles and allows populations to exhibit
greater genetic diversity.

21.2  Formation of Male Gametophytes

in the Stamen

 ■ Pollen forms in two stages: first microsporogenesis,

then microgametogenesis (Figure 21.3).

 ■ In the locule, diploid pollen mother cells divide mei-
otically to produce the microspores, surrounded by
a nutritive tapetum (Figures 21.2, 21.3).

 ■ Pollen cell walls are complex, with multiple layers

for nutrient storage and for pollen dispersal
(Figures 21.4, 21.5).

21.3  Female Gametophyte Development

in the Ovule

 ■ Eggs are formed in the female gametophyte

(embryo sac) first by megasporogenesis and then
by megagametogenesis (Figures 21.7, 21.8).

 ■ Most angiosperms exhibit Polygonum-type

megagametophyte development, wherein meiosis
of a diploid mother cell produces four immature
haploid megagametophytes, only one of which
undergoes megagametogenesis.

 ■ Megagametogenesis begins with three mitotic

divisions without cytokinesis, followed by cellular-
ization (Figure 21.9).

21.4  Pollination and Fertilization

in Flowering Plants

 ■ Once pollen has been delivered to the stigma, sperm
cells travel to the female gametophyte through a
newly grown pollen tube (Figures 20.10, 21.11).

 ■ A pollen tube will not form unless there is recogni-

tion between pollen and stigma (Figure 21.11).

 ■ Pollen tubes grow by tip growth (Figures

21.12–21.14).

 ■ Tip-focused ion concentration gradients and a

GTPase switch are essential for controlling the polar-
ity of cell expansion of the pollen tube (Figure 21.15).

 ■ The path of pollen tube growth is determined by

physical and chemical cues from the pistil and the
megagametophyte.

 ■ Once the pollen tube has reached the ovule, two
sperm are released to fertilize the egg and the
central cell (Figure 21.17).

21.5 Selfing versus Outcrossing
 ■ Outcrossing is ensured in hermaphroditic and

monoecious species by dichogamy and heterostyly
(Figure 21.18).

 ■ Self-pollination is reduced by cytoplasmic male

sterility.

 ■ Self-incompatibility (SI) biochemically prevents
self-pollination in angiosperms (Figure 21.19).

 ■ Sporophytic SI reactions require expression of two
highly variable S-locus genes, while gametophytic
SI is mediated by cytotoxic S-RNases and F-box
proteins (Figures 21.20, 21.21).

21.6  Apomixis: Asexual Reproduction

by Seed

 ■ Apomixis, or clonal reproduction by a diploid cell,
may contribute to the fitness of polyploid species.

 ■ Being able to induce apomixis would reduce loss of

hybrid vigor in agricultural crops.

21.7 Endosperm Development
 ■ After fertilization, the diploid endosperm, which will
provide nutrition to the embryo, becomes multinu-
cleate, making it a coenocyte (Figures 21.24, 21.25).

 ■ Cellularization of the coenocytic endosperm in

Arabidopsis proceeds from the micropylar to the
chalazal region, while cellularization of cereal
endosperms proceeds centripetally (Figures 21.25,
21.26).

 ■ Endosperm development is controlled primarily

by maternally expressed genes (MEGs), not by the
embryo.

21.8 Seed Coat Development
 ■ The seed coat arises from maternal integuments,

but its development is regulated by the endosperm
(Figure 21.28).

21.9  Seed Maturation and
Desiccation Tolerance

 ■ Seed filling and the acquisition of dessication

tolerance overlap in most species (Figure 21.29).

 ■ The acquisition of dessication tolerance is aided
by LEA proteins, which form hydrogen bonds
with nonreducing sugars, allowing embryo cells to
acquire a glassy state that makes them more stable
than cells that are simply dehydrated.

 ■ Impermeable seed coats and low temperatures
can increase seed longevity, which otherwise is
highly variable across species.

21.10 Fruit Development and Ripening
 ■ Fruits are seed-dispersal units that either arise from

the pistil and contain the seed(s) or are derived
from accessory floral tissues (Figure 21.30).

 ■ Fruit initiation is triggered by fertilization of the

ovules and pollination of the stigma. This develop-
mental switch, also called fruit set, can be induced
by auxin and GA (Figure 21.31).

 ■ Induction of parthenocarpy—the development
of fruit without fertilization—can improve fruit
quality and yield (Figure 21.32).

 ■ Dehiscent fruits release their seeds through pod
shattering. Proper morphogenesis and differenti-
ation of the dehiscence zone rely on a network of
transcription factors and miRNAs (Figure 21.33).

 ■ Fleshy fruits undergo ripening, which involves

color changes, highly coordinated fruit softening,
and other changes (Figures 21.34–21.37).

 ■ Acids, sugars, and volatiles determine the flavor

of ripe and unripe fleshy fruits.

 ■ Ethylene accelerates ripening, particularly in

climacteric fruits (Figures 21.38, 21.39).

 ■ A mechanistic understanding of the ripening

process has commercial applications (Figure 21.42).
