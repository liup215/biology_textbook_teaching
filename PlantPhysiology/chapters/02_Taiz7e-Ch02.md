Cell Walls: Structure, Formation,
and Expansion

Plant cells, unlike animal cells, are surrounded by a mechan-

ically strong cell wall. This physical structure has arisen in
many organisms as a result of independent evolutionary

events. As a result of their discrete origins, the cell walls of
prokaryotes, fungi, heterokonts (e.g., diatoms and brown algae),
algae, and members of the plant kingdom differ from each other
in chemical composition and molecular structure. However, they
all share three common functions: regulation of cell volume, de-
termination of cell shape, and mechanical protection of the deli-
cate protoplast against biochemical and physical assaults.

Much of the carbon assimilated by photosynthesis in plants
(see Chapter 10) is channeled into the polysaccharides that make
up the cell wall. The wall, in its simplest conception, comprises a
scaffold of cellulose fibrils embedded in a matrix of polysaccha-
rides and proteins produced by the cell. The matrix components
and cellulose fibrils assemble into a network linked by a mixture
of covalent and noncovalent bonds. The function of the plant cell
wall is intimately linked to its composition and structure, which we
explore throughout this chapter.

Although our focus here is on the intrinsic organismal functions

of the plant cell wall, it is important to note that cell walls also
provide the raw material for many products important to humans
and our society. Plant cell walls are the material basis of paper,
textiles (such as cotton, flax, and linen), wood-based structural
products, synthetic fibers, plastics, films, coatings, adhesives,
gels, thickeners, and other engineered materials. Globally, aggre-
gated cell walls, described as cellulosic biomass, are widely used
for energy generation, heat, and fuel. As one of the most abundant
reservoirs of organic carbon in nature and a major sink for
photosynthetically captured carbon, the plant cell wall also
plays an integral role in carbon flow through ecosystems.

We begin this chapter with a description of some general
functions of plant cell walls, followed by an overview of their
composition, biosynthesis, and assembly. We then turn to the role
of the primary cell wall in cell expansion and cell shape, arguably
its most essential physiological function. We end by examining
how secondary cell walls are produced, via lignification, and
their importance for the physiology of water transport and
plant structure.

 2.1  Overview of Plant Cell Wall
Functions and Structures

It is impossible to know what plants would be like had
cell walls not evolved in their ancestors; however, we do
know that if we remove their cells walls through enzy-
matic digestion, we are left with vulnerable, spherical,
membrane-bound protoplasts that have no organized
supracellular structure or function. The cell wall is an
essential part of any plant. Indeed, the cell wall is critical
for many essential processes in plant growth, develop-
ment, and physiology:

 ● Cells walls connecting adjacent cells prevent cell

sliding, slippage, and motility. Cell adhesion is thus
essential for tissue integrity and defense. The selec-
tive release of cell adhesion is equally important for
growth, the development of intercellular air spaces
for gas exchange, and cell separation during
leaf abscission.

 ● As a mechanically strong layer encapsulating the cell,
the wall acts as a cellular “exoskeleton” that controls
cell shape and allows high turgor pressures to devel-
op. Without a cell wall to resist the forces generated
by turgor pressure, plant water relations would be
profoundly different (see Chapter 3). Because of this,
cell walls determine the mechanical strength of plant
structures that allow plants to grow to great heights.

 ● Plant morphogenesis ultimately depends on the

control of cell wall properties, because the physical en-
largement of plant cells is limited principally by when
and how the cell wall yields to the force of turgor.

 ● The cell wall acts as a diffusion barrier that limits
the size and kinds of molecules that can reach the
plasma membrane, both through sieving effects
and through ionic and hydrophobic interactions.
Fixed negative charges in the walls profoundly
influence the distribution of ions and charged
macromolecules.

 ● Numerous sensory proteins are partly anchored in the
cell wall and form a bridge to the plasma membrane,
providing a mechanism for sensing cell integrity.

 ● Cell walls present a major structural and chemi-

cal barrier to invasion and spread of pathogens, to
entry and proliferation of parasites, and to tissue
consumption by herbivores. Moreover, oligosaccha-
rides released from the cell wall by the action of lytic
enzymes from invading microbes act as important
signaling molecules that elicit defense responses
against pathogens.

 ● Transpirational water flow in the xylem requires a
mechanically strong wall that resists collapse in re-
sponse to the negative pressure in the xylem. Defects
in cell wall formation often result in xylem collapse.

The functional diversity and varying roles of the cell
wall require diverse cell wall compositions and struc-
tures. We begin this section with a brief description
of the morphology and basic architecture of plant cell
walls. Then we discuss the organization, composition,
and synthesis of the wall in some of its diverse forms.

Polysaccharides  may  be  linear  unbranched
chains of sugar residues (units), or they may con-
tain side branches attached to the backbone. For
branched polysaccharides, the backbone of the
polysaccharide is usually indicated by the last
part of the name. For example, xyloglucan has a
glucan backbone (a linear chain of glucose resi-
dues) with xylose sugars attached as side chains.
Arabinoxylan has a xylan backbone (a chain of
xylose residues) with arabinose side chains. The
names can get lengthy. For example, glucurono-
arabinoxylan (GAX) is an arabinoxylan decorat-
ed with a low frequency of glucuronic acid units.
However, a compound name does not necessarily
imply a branched structure. For example, rham-
nogalacturonan  I  is  the  name  given  to  a  poly-

Thus, the name is based on the major sugars in the
polymer but does not indicate its structural details.

The specific linkages between sugar rings, including
the specific carbons that are linked together and the
configuration of the linkage (see WEB TOPIC 2.1), are
important for the properties of the polysaccharide. For
instance, cellulose is a glucan made of β(1,4)-linked glu-
cose, while callose is made of β(1,3)-linked glucose; both
are glucans, but they have very different functions and
mechanical properties.

Cell wall polysaccharides are classified into three
groups. Cellulose is the major fibrillar component of the
cell wall and is composed of an array of β(1,4)-linked glu-
cans coalesced to form a microfibril with well-ordered
and less-ordered regions. It is insoluble in water and
has high tensile strength (see Section 2.2). Pectin is the
name for a complex and diverse group of hydrophilic,
gel-forming polysaccharides rich in acidic sugar resi-
dues. The third group of wall polysaccharides are collec-
tively called hemicelluloses. Chemically hemicelluloses
have been defined as polysaccharides with β(1,4)-linked
backbones linked in an equatorial configuration (mean-
ing the link between residues is in line with the plane
of the ring). Pectins and hemicelluloses are also called
matrix polysaccharides because they help form the ma-
trix within which cellulose is embedded.

celluloses, whereas secondary cell walls are high in cellu-
lose and a different form of hemicellulose, with varying
amounts of lignin (described in Section 2.4), a polymer

Cellulose is the most abundant polysaccharide on earth.
Its function in plants and utility for humans is based on
its packaging into microfibrils. The simplest cellulose
microfibrils are narrow structures, approximately 3 nm
wide, that strengthen the cell wall, sometimes reinforc-
ing more in one direction than in another, depending
on how the microfibrils are deposited in the wall (i.e.,
they give structural bias; see Figure 2.3B). Each micro-
fibril comprises an estimated 18 to 24 (usually 18) par-
allel chains of (1,4)-linked β-d-glucose tightly packed
together to form a highly ordered (crystalline) core
with extensive hydrogen bonding within and between
glucan chains (Figure 2.7). The chains surrounding the
core are more flexible, and their positions are influenced
by interactions with water and matrix polysaccharides
at the surface. Moreover, there is evidence of periodic
disorder along the microfibril—that is, short segments
where crystalline order is interrupted at intervals of 150
to 300 nm. These areas of disorder are thought to rep-
resent sites where pectins or hemicelluloses are woven
into the cellulose microfibril.

Native cellulose in plants is found in two variant crys-
talline forms, called allomorphs Iα and Iβ, which differ
slightly in the way the parallel glucan chains are packed.
Cellulose Iβ is the dominant allomorph in land plants.
The biological significance of these two crystalline forms
is unclear at present. Microfibrils have hydrophilic sur-
faces, populated by polar—OH groups extending from
the sides of stacked glucose chains, and hydrophobic
surfaces, populated by nonpolar C—H groups populat-
ing the plane of the sugar rings (see Figure 2.7E). These
surfaces bind water and matrix polymers differently,
and as a result the microfibril shape is an important fac-
tor for wall construction. It is also important for enzy-
matic attack by microbial cellulases, which dock onto the
hydrophobic surface and remove one glucan chain at a
time. A major barrier to enzymatic attack of cellulose is
the energetic cost of stripping an individual glucan from
this crystalline microfibril.

Hemicelluloses are matrix polysaccharides
that bind to cellulose
Hemicelluloses comprise a heterogeneous group of
polysaccharides (Figure 2.10) that are usually tightly
bound in the wall, as just introduced. Hemicelluloses
typically have a pronounced ability to bind to cellulose
in vitro and likely play an important role in the assembly
of cellulose microfibrils to form a coherent cell wall in
vivo (e.g., through altering cellulose crystallinity)

The dominant hemicellulose in primary cell walls
of most land plants is xyloglucan, which consists of a
(1,4)-β-d-glucan decorated with (1,6)-linked β-d-xylosyl
residues (see Figure 2.10A). Xyloglucan structure shows
some variability among species. In most eudicots, 30 to
40% of the xylose residues are appended with a galac-
tose residue, which in turn may bear a terminal fucose
residue. A concise nomenclature has been developed to
refer to the branching pattern of xyloglucan (see Figure
2.10B): For example, G is used for an unsubstituted glu-
cose residue; X means glucose is substituted solely with
xylose; L is used for a xylose–galactose side chain; and F
denotes a xylose–galactose–fucose side chain.

Xyloglucan has a repeating substructure in which one
of every four glucose residues in the backbone is unsub-
stituted (does not carry a sugar side chain). Endogluca-
nase digestion of xyloglucan from most eudicot sources
yields three major oligosaccharides with four glucose
residues in the backbone, designated XXXG, XXFG, and
XLFG. Plants in the Solanaceae, such as tomato, use an
arabinose residue in place of galactose, which appears
to be functionally equivalent for cell wall mechanics.
Glycosidases are able to remove the side chain sugars,
resulting in xyloglucans with a lower degree of substi-
tution, which bind more tightly to cellulose.

Unlike in most other land plants, the dominant hemi-
cellulose in the primary cell wall of grasses (Poaceae)
is arabinoxylan (also called glucuronoarabinoxylan or
GAX; see Figure 2.10C). The small amount of xylgoclu-
cans present are mostly made up of XXGG, XXGGG,
and XXGGGG repeat units. Pectins are also present in
grass cell walls but are greatly reduced in abundance.

GAX has a (1,4)-β-d-xylan backbone substituted with
(1,3)-α-l-arabinose residues; approximately 1 residue in
50 is substituted with (1,2)-α-d-glucuronic acid. The de-
gree of arabinose substitution varies widely, from more
than 80% to less than 10%. Unlike most hemicelluloses,
highly substituted GAX is not tightly bound to the cell
wall, does not bind cellulose in vitro, and is readily sol-
ubilized from the cell wall under mild conditions used
to extract pectin. Some of the arabinose residues bear
ferulate groups attached by an ester linkage. Oxidative
coupling of ferulate groups results in cross-links be-
tween GAX; such cross-linking reduces the digestibility
of grasses (i.e., for feeding cows and sheep) and may
reduce cell wall extensibility. Ferulates also function as
nucleation sites for lignin polymerization in grass walls.
In addition to GAX, primary cell walls of grasses
also contain mixed-linkage  (1,3;1,4)-β-D-glucan. The
mixed-linkage glucan is thought to bind tightly to the
surface of cellulose, reducing cellulose–cellulose in-
teractions, while less-substituted GAX may serve a
cross-linking function.

Secondary walls of woody tissues contain little xylo-
glucan or pectin; instead, the matrix polysaccharides are
primarily xylans and glucomannans with a low degree of
side chain substitution. These hemicelluloses bind tightly
to cellulose and require strong alkali to be solubilized
from the wall. The major hemicellulose of secondary
walls varies by source: In secondary walls of eudicots the
dominant hemicellulose is glucuronoxylan, with lesser
amounts of glucomannans. Glucuronoxylan is similar to
GAX (see Figure 2.10C), but without the arabinose side
chains and the glucuronic acid is 4-O-methyl substituted.
Glucomannan has a backbone consisting of β(1,4)-linked
glucose and mannose residues, with infrequent galactose
side chains (see Figure 2.10D). In gymnosperm wood
the major hemicellulose is glucomannan, with smaller
amounts of arabinoxylan substituted with 4-O-meth-
ylglucuronyl residues. GAX with low degree of substi-
tution is the major hemicellulose of secondary walls in
grasses. The low frequency of side chains in these hemi-
celluloses enables them to bind more tightly to cellulose
and to pack more tightly in the cell wall.

Pectins are hydrophilic gel-forming
components of the primary cell wall
Pectins are the most abundant component of most pri-
mary cell walls, forming a hydrated gel phase in which
cellulose and hemicelluloses are embedded. They act as
hydrophilic filler to prevent aggregation and collapse of
the cellulose network, and they also determine the po-
rosity of the cell wall to macromolecules. They are often
the sole component of the middle lamella (the oldest
part of the wall). Release of oligosaccharides from pec-
tins during fungal invasion of plant tissue elicits defense
responses that limit pathogen invasion (see Chapter 24).

 2.2 The Dynamic Primary Cell Wall

Early in the life of a plant cell, its primary cell wall is
extensible and able to incorporate new structural ma-
terial as the cell grows. As discussed in Section 2.1, the
primary cell wall is generally simple, comprising thin
layers made of long cellulose microfibrils embedded in
a hydrated matrix of noncellulosic polysaccharides and
small amounts of proteins (see Figure 2.3). This structure
imparts an ideal combination of flexibility and strength
to the growing cell wall, which must be both extensible
and strong at the same time.

Primary cell walls are continually assembled
during cell growth
As mentioned in Section 2.1, primary walls originate de
novo during the final stages of cell division when the
newly formed cell plate separates the two daughter cells
and solidifies into a stable wall that is capable of bearing
the physical stresses generated by turgor pressure. You
may recall that the middle lamella forms in parallel with
the cell plate (see Figure 2.2). As each daughter cell grows,
new components are continually added to the primary
cell wall. As such, the “life” of an individual polymer that

also guide wall assembly, by facilitating the breaking
and forming of covalent bonds. A prime candidate for
enzyme-mediated wall assembly is xyloglucan endo-
transglucosylase (XET). This enzyme, which belongs to
a large family of enzymes named xyloglucan endotrans-
glucosylase/hydrolases (XTHs), facilitates the severing
of the backbone of a xyloglucan and re-joining one end
of the cut xyloglucan with the free end of an acceptor xy-
loglucan (Figure 2.13). This transfer reaction integrates
newly synthesized xyloglucans into the cell wall, po-
tentially strengthening it. Transglycosylases with other
substrate specificities have recently been detected in
plant cell walls, but their biological functions have not
yet been assessed.

Other wall enzymes that might aid in wall assembly
include glycosidases, pectin methyl esterases, pectin ac-
yltransferases, and various oxidases. Some glycosidases
remove the side chains of hemicelluloses, increasing
the tendency of hemicelluloses to adhere to each other
and to the surface of cellulose microfibrils. As we de-
scribed in Section 2.1, pectin methyl esterase removes
methyl esters that block acidic groups of HG, thereby
enhancing HG’s ability to form a Ca2+-bridged gel net-
work; in this case there is likely to be interplay between
enzymatic activity and self-assembly. Oxidases such
as peroxidase catalyze cross-linking between phenolic
groups (tyrosine, phenylalanine, ferulic acid) in wall
proteins, pectins, and other wall polymers. Such oxida-
tive cross-linking is also the basis of lignin formation,
which we discuss in Section 2.4.

Microfibril orientation influences growth
directionality of cells with diffuse growth
During growth, the loosened cell wall is extended by
physical forces generated from cell turgor pressure. Tur-
gor pressure creates an outward-directed force, equal in
all directions. While turgor pressure has no directional-
ity, the cell wall may yield more in one direction than
another due to its structural characteristics (more detail
later in this section). The directionality of growth is de-
termined in large part by the orientation of cellulose mi-
crofibrils within the wall.

When cells first form at meristems, they are isodia-
metric; that is, they have equal diameters in all direc-
tions. If the orientation of cellulose microfibrils in the
primary cell wall is randomly arranged, the cells grow
isotropically (equally in all directions), expanding radi-
ally to generate a sphere (Figure 2.15A). In most plant
cell walls, however, cellulose microfibrils are aligned in
a predominant direction. The alignment of cellulose fi-
bers allows the cell wall to hold more stress (generated
by turgor) in the direction parallel to the predominant
fiber alignment and yield more in the direction perpen-
dicular to the fiber alignment. The result is anisotropic
growth (as in stems, where cells increase much more in
length than in width).

In the lateral walls of elongating cells, such as cor-
tical and vascular cells of some stems and roots, or the
giant internode cells of the filamentous green alga Nitel-
la, cellulose microfibrils are deposited circumferentially
(transversely), at right angles to the long axis of the cell.
The circumferential arrangement of cellulose micro-
fibrils restricts growth in girth and promotes growth

Cortical microtubules influence the
orientation of newly deposited microfibrils
Newly  deposited  cellulose  microfibrils  usually  are
coaligned with microtubule arrays in the cytoplasm, close
to the plasma membrane (Figure 2.16). A striking example
occurs in xylem vessel elements, where bands of cortical
microtubules mark the sites of secondary wall thickenings
and also the sites of CESA localization. Moreover, exper-

drugs or by genetic defects often lead to disorganized wall
structure and disorganized growth. For example, several
drugs bind to tubulin, the subunit protein of microtubules,
causing them to depolymerize. When growing roots are
treated with a microtubule-depolymerizing drug, such as

disrupted growth is due to the isotropic expansion of the
cells; that is, they enlarge like a sphere instead of in a di-
rectional manner. The drug-induced destruction of micro-
tubules in the growing cells interferes with the transverse
deposition of cellulose. Cellulose microfibrils continue to
be synthesized in the absence of microtubules, but they
are deposited randomly and have altered crystallinity, and
consequently the cells expand equally in all directions.

pothesis that microtubules serve as tracks that guide or
direct the movement of CESA complexes as they synthe-
size microfibrils (see WEB ESSAY 2.1). The movement

a fusion of CESA with a fluorescent protein. Tagged
CESA units were observed to move within the plasma

microtubules guide cellulose microfibril direction.

Many factors influence the extent and rate
of cell growth
Plant cells typically expand 10- to 1000-fold in volume
before reaching maturity; this is an important and dis-
tinguishing feature of plant growth, whereby the plant
body’s volume is built by massive increases in cell vol-
ume rather than cell number. In extreme cases—for ex-
ample, xylem vessel elements—cells may enlarge more
than 10,000-fold in volume compared with their meriste-
matic initials. The cell wall permits this massive expan-
sion without losing its mechanical integrity as a whole
or becoming dramatically thinner. Thus, as we described
in Section 2.1, newly synthesized polymers are integrat-
ed into the wall without destabilizing it.

This integration process may be particularly critical
for cells that display extremely rapid expansion, such as
tip-growing root hairs and pollen tubes. In rapidly grow-
ing cells with tip growth, the growing wall at the tip dou-
bles its surface area and is displaced to the nonexpanding
part of the cell within minutes. This is a much greater
rate of wall expansion than is typically found in cells with
diffuse growth, where growth rates are approximately 1
to 10% per hour. Because of their fast expansion rates,
tip-growing cells are highly susceptible to wall thinning
and bursting. Mechanical and cytological models of tip
growth in pollen tubes have given hints about how ex-
pansion and the addition of wall components need to
be coordinated for stable tip growth. Although diffuse
growth and tip growth appear to be different growth
patterns, both types of wall expansion must have anal-
ogous, if not identical, processes of polymer integration,
wall stress relaxation, and wall polymer slippage.

There are indications that the cell wall matrix may
regulate cell expansion rates. Transgenic alteration of
pectin to increase or decrease HG methylation results in
concomitant changes in hypocotyl cell elongation rate
in Arabidopsis. There are also tissue- and cell-level pat-
terns of HG methylation that are coincident with vari-
ations in growth rate. For example, in anisotropically
growing hypocotyl epidermal cells, the slower growing
end walls have more Ca2+-cross-linked pectin. It is still
unclear how changing matrix properties influence cell
elongation rate, but it may be through both direct and
indirect effects. Directly, changes to the matrix mechan-
ics, perhaps through changes in matrix hydration, could
affect the ability of cellulose fibrils to move. Indirectly,
changes in pectin methylation would probably alter the
porosity of the cell wall and charge distribution, both of
which could influence the mobility of factors important
for cellulose fibril modification.

Many intrinsic and extrinsic factors influence the rate
of cell wall expansion. Cell type and age are important de-
velopmental factors. So, too, are hormones such as auxin,
gibberellin, and brassinosteroids, which signal when and
where cells should grow. Environmental conditions such

as light and water availability may likewise modulate cell
expansion. These intrinsic and extrinsic factors are most
likely to modify cell expansion by altering the way in
which the cell wall is loosened, so that it yields (stretches
irreversibly) differently. In this context we speak of the
yielding properties of the cell wall. However, these
factors might also alter turgor pressure, which would
similarly influence expansion rate. The wall’s yielding
properties and turgor pressure are probably both regu-
lated during growth, perhaps to different degrees de-
pending on the exact context being considered.

Stress relaxation of the cell wall drives water
uptake and cell expansion
Because the cell wall is the major mechanical restraint
that limits cell expansion, much attention has been given
to its physical properties. As a hydrated polymeric ma-
terial, the plant cell wall has physical properties that
are intermediate between those of a solid and those of a
liquid. We call these viscoelastic, or rheological (flow),
properties. Walls of cells that are growing are general-
ly less rigid and exhibit less flow than those of mature
cells, and under appropriate conditions they exhibit a
long-term irreversible stretching, or yielding, that is ab-
sent or nearly absent in mature walls. This irreversible
stretching may also be referred to as plastic deformation.
Stress relaxation is a crucial concept for understand-
ing how cell walls enlarge. The term stress is used here
in the mechanical sense, as force per unit area. As we
hinted at earlier, wall stresses arise as an inevitable con-
sequence of cell turgor. The turgor pressure in growing
plant cells is typically between 0.3 and 1.0 megapascals
(MPa); for contrast, a typical car tire is inflated to about
0.2 MPa. Turgor pressure stretches the cell wall and gen-
erates a counterbalancing physical stress or tension in
the wall. Because plant cells enclose large pressurized
volumes within relatively thin cell walls, the tensile
stress generated within the wall is equivalent to 10 to
100 MPa, which is 10- to 100-fold greater than turgor—a
very large stress indeed.

This simple fact has important consequences for the
mechanics of cell enlargement. To change shape, plant
cells must control the direction and rate of wall expan-
sion. The directionality of wall expansion is determined
by depositing cellulose in a biased orientation. The rate
of expansion is determined by controlled loosening of
bonds between microfibrils and between other matrix
polysaccharides. This biochemical loosening enables
movement or slippage of cellulose microfibrils and their
associated matrix polysaccharides, thereby increasing
the wall surface area. At the same time, such loosening
reduces the physical stress in the wall.

because it decreases turgor pressure and thus water po-

Acid-induced growth and wall stress
relaxation are mediated by expansins
A common characteristic of growing cell walls is that
they extend much faster at acidic pH than at neutral pH.
This phenomenon is called acid growth. In living cells,
acid growth is evident when growing cells are treated
with acid buffers or with the fungal toxin fusicoccin,
which induces acidification of the cell wall space by ac-
tivating an H+-ATPase in the plasma membrane. These
changes in cell wall acidification can be directly visual-
ized with secreted ratiometric fluorescent protein report-
ers. These reporters use a red fluorescent protein that
is relatively unaffected by pH as a normalizing control
and a fusion (pHusion) of a yellow fluorescent protein
whose fluorescence is quenched (reduced) by low pH to
monitor local changes in pH.

An example of acid-induced growth can be found in
the initiation of the root hair, where the local wall pH
drops to a value of 4.5 at the time when the epidermal
cell begins to bulge outward. Wall acidification also

occurs during auxin-induced growth, but is probably
not sufficient to account for all the growth induced by
this hormone, and other wall-loosening processes may
also  be  involved.  Nevertheless,  this pH-dependent
mechanism of wall extension appears to be an evolu-
tionarily conserved process common to all land plants
and is involved in a variety of growth processes.

Acid growth may also be observed in isolated frozen
and thawed or heat-treated cell wall segments, which
lack normal cellular, metabolic, and synthetic process-
es. Such an observation entails using an extensometer
to place the walls in tension and to measure long-term
wall extension, or “creep” (Figure 2.18). The term creep
refers to a time-dependent irreversible extension under a
fixed force or load, typically the result of slippage of wall
polymers relative to one another. When growing walls

extend briefly when tension is applied, but extension
soon ceases. When transferred to an acidic buffer of pH
5 or less (0 min in Figure 2.18, the wall begins to extend
rapidly, in some instances continuing for many hours.

growing cells, but it is not observed in mature (nongrow-

they do not exhibit lytic or other enzymatic activities.
Expansins exhibit structural similarities with another

tivity cannot be demonstrated.

nomes, we now know that expansins belong to a large

this makes sense given the general differences in hemi-

plant tissues. Evolutionary analysis indicates that the
bacterial expansins probably arose from one or more

horizontal gene transfers from a plant to a bacterium,
followed by additional horizontal gene transfers among
various bacterial species that colonize the vascular sys-
tem of plants.

The molecular basis for expansin action on wall rhe-
ology is still uncertain, but most evidence indicates that
expansins cause wall creep by loosening noncovalent
adhesion between wall polysaccharides. Structural and
binding studies suggest that expansins act at sites in the
cell wall where cellulose microfibrils stick to one anoth-
er. These hypothesized “hot spots” appear to be short,
tightly connected junctions that may also be sites of pec-
tin and hemicellulose interactions with cellulose.

Cell wall models are hypotheses about how
molecular components fit together to make
a functional wall
To understand how plant cells grow, it is essential to un-
derstand how the cell wall polymers are connected to
produce a structure with enough tensile strength to re-
sist turgor pressure, yet at the same time flexible enough
to allow for irreversible expansion of the wall fabric and
incorporation of new polymers to strengthen the wall.
The complexity of the plant cell wall makes structural
elucidation quite difficult. Structural models of cell walls
are revised as new structural details are discovered.

The earliest molecular model of primary cell wall ar-
chitecture envisioned a covalently linked matrix of hemi-
cellulose (xyloglucans), pectins, and structural proteins
that were noncovalently bonded to cellulose microfibrils.
This model was later replaced by an alternative model in
which xyloglucans fully coated the surfaces of cellulose
microfibrils and directly tethered them into a load-bearing
network, with pectins and glycoproteins forming an inde-
pendent, interpenetrating matrix (Figure 2.20A).

and physical interactions among cell wall polymers.

tissues is synthesized by a set of three CESAs different
from the three CESAs used to synthesize primary cell

structure is unclear at this time, but possibly it has an
impact on how cellulose synthase complexes function,
either separately or in clusters to make macrofibrils (as-

of defects in SCW cellulose synthesis is xylem vessel

their hemicelluloses have xylan and (gluco-) mannan

impact on hemicellulose properties, such as conforma-

has a substantial effect on packing of cellulose microfi-

Secondary cell walls are rich in cellulose
and hemicellulose and often have a
hierarchical organization
The temporal history of the SCW, as reflected in its
layered structure, is even more obvious than that of
the primary wall. The best-studied SCWs consist of se-
quentially formed concentric layers named, from oldest
to youngest, S1, S2, S3, and so on; the number of lay-
ers varies with cell type (see Figure 2.21B). SCWs with
two or three layers are common in wood and fibers.
Cellulose orientation is distinct for each layer, with the
first-deposited layer (S1) oriented in a shallow, nearly
transverse helix, whereas cellulose in the thicker S2 layer
is oriented more longitudinally. In fiber cells (see Chap-
ter 1), there is often a special type of SCW deposited in
a final step and forming a “gelatinous” layer. This wall
section is rich in cellulose and pectins (typically rham-
nogalacturonans) that make it strong but also flexible.
This structure allows for contraction when needed to
balance increasing external forces, as are observed in

tension wood. There is some debate about whether this
gelatinous layer should be considered a tertiary wall.

MACROFIBRIL FORMATION, STRUCTURE, AND
ADHESION SCW lamellae contain highly aligned cel-
lulose microfibrils that are assembled into tightly packed
macrofibrils, which in turn coalign with each other
and are separated by hemicellulose and lignin (Figure
2.21C). The concept of the macrofibril as an aggregate
of numerous individual microfibrils is based largely on
high-resolution electron microscopy of de-lignified and
partially deconstructed cell walls. The appearance of
macrofibrils in the wall suggests that their formation is
well organized and begins at the earliest stages of cellu-
lose microfibril formation. One possibility is that clusters
of cellulose synthesis complexes—one for each elemen-
tary microfibril—coordinately produce microfibrils that
align and coalesce immediately to form a macrofibril,
with hemicellulose interaction occurring afterward. This
process may be mediated by auxiliary proteins in the
COBRA and KORRIGAN families, as their mutant phe-
notypes include reduced cell wall organization.

Molecular models of SCWs are much less developed
than those developed for primary cell walls. One hy-
pothetical model by Terashima and coworkers (Figure
2.21D) illustrates some basic concepts of macrofibril
construction at the nanoscale. The model proposes a
structured arrangement of matrix polymers, with glu-
comannans  coating  the  macrofibril  surface,  xylans
positioned in the next layer, and lignin linking xylans
and filling the remaining space between macrofibrils.
Other models suggest that lignin is intercalated and in-
tertwined among hemicellulose chains. The details of
macrofibril structure may also differ among species with
different hemicellulose composition.

Physical and computational studies indicate that mac-
rofibrils are prevented from fusing into one massive cel-
lulose crystal by water trapped between the constituent
microfibrils. Misalignment and twisting of individual
microfibrils may also help prevent such crystallization.
Macrofibril diameter also varies with cell type and wall
lamella in a manner that correlates with lignin content.

Although lignification is associated with
wall strengthening, the physical basis for
this effect is not clear. Lignin was previous-

native lignin (or “protolignin”) is smaller

of lignin cross-linking in other cell walls, but
extensive cross-linking does not appear to
be essential for wood formation in poplar.

In support of lignin’s proposed function in plant cell
walls, addition of lignin to composite hydrogel mate-

strength, deformation recoverability, and fracture force.
As the SCW becomes lignified, water is removed and is
replaced by the hydrophobic lignin molecules. This tends
to enhance noncovalant interactions between lignin and

polysaccharides, perhaps accounting for some of the
wall strengthening. There is also evidence for extensive
covalent linking between lignin and wall polysaccha-

linkages are largely via ferulate groups attached to arab-

 Summary

The architecture, mechanics, and function of plants de-
pend on the structure of the cell wall. The wall is secret-
ed and assembled as a complex structure that varies in
form and composition as the cell differentiates.

2.1  Overview of Plant Cell Wall Functions

and Structures

 ■ Cell walls vary greatly in form and composition,
depending on cell type and species (Figures 2.1,
2.3, 2.4).

 ■ Primary cell walls are synthesized in actively grow-
ing cells, whereas secondary cell walls are depos-
ited in certain cells, such as xylem vessel elements
and sclerenchyma fibers, after cell expansion
ceases (Figures 2.2–2.4).

 ■ The middle lamella is a pectin-rich layer between
the primary walls of adjoining cells that is formed
during cell division (Figure 2.2)

 ■ The primary cell wall is a network of cellulose

microfibrils embedded in a matrix of hemicellulo-
ses, pectins, and structural proteins (Figures 2.5,
2.6; Table 2.1).

 ■ Cellulose microfibrils are highly ordered arrays of
glucan chains synthesized at the surface of the
cell by protein complexes called cellulose syn-
thase complexes. (Figures 2.7, 2.8).

 ■ Matrix polysaccharides are synthesized in the
Golgi apparatus and secreted via vesicles
(Figure 2.9).

 ■ Hemicelluloses bind microfibrils together, and
pectins form hydrophilic gels that can become
cross-linked by calcium ions (Figures 2.10–2.12).

 ■ Secondary walls in woody tissues typically contain
xylans and glucomannans instead of xyloglucan
and pectin.

2.2 The Dynamic Primary Cell Wall
 ■ Wall assembly occurs partly by spontaneous
self-assembly but may also be mediated by
enzymes. Xyloglucan endotransglucosylase (XET)
has the ability to carry out transglycosylation
reactions that integrate newly synthesized xylo-
glucans into the wall (Figure 2.13).

2.3 Mechanisms of Cell Expansion
 ■ Wall expansion may be highly localized (tip

growth) or more dispersed over the wall surface
(diffuse growth) (Figure 2.14).

 ■ In diffuse-growing cells, the orientation of cell

growth is determined by the orientation of cellulose
microfibrils, which is determined by the orientation
of microtubules in the cytoplasm (Figures 2.15–2.17).

 ■ The actions of hormones (such as auxin, gibber-
ellin, and brassinosteroids) and environmental
conditions (such as light and water availability)
modulate cell expansion by altering wall extensi-
bility, or the yield properties of the wall.

 ■ Biochemical loosening of the cell wall leads to wall
stress relaxation, which dynamically links water
uptake with cell wall expansion in the growing cell.

 ■ Complicated cell growth patterns, like the “jigsaw”

pattern seen in leaf epidermal pavement cells,
involve localized pressure points generated by
cellular expansion and interactions with
cytoskeletal elements.

 ■ Acid-induced cell wall extension is characteristic

of walls in growing cells and is mediated by
proteins called expansins, which loosen the non-
covalent adhesions between wall polysaccharides
(Figures 2.18, 2.19).

 ■ Cessation of cell growth during cell maturation

involves multiple mechanisms of cell wall
cross-linking and rigidification.

2.4  Secondary Cell Wall Structure

and Function

 ■ Secondary cell walls are typically thick layers

deposited between the plasma membrane and the
primary cell wall. They add strength and compres-
sion resistance to stems and other organs.

 ■ The secondary cell walls of woody tissues comprise
two or more layers containing cellulose, hemicel-
lulose, and lignin.

 ■ Lignin is formed in the wall by oxidative coupling
of monolignols into a random polymer of pheno-
lic subunits. This results in crosslinking of lignin,
which locks the secondary cell wall into a hydro-
phobic material that is resistant to enzymatic
deconstruction (Figures 2.21, 2.22).
