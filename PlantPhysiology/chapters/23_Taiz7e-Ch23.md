Plant Senescence and
Developmental Cell Death

E very autumn, people living in temperate climates enjoy

the spectacular color changes that can precede the falling
of leaves from deciduous trees. Autumn leaves turn yellow,
orange, or red and fall from their branches in response to shorter
day lengths and cooler temperatures, which trigger two related
developmental processes: senescence and abscission (Figure 23.1).
Senescence is the last developmental stage, ultimately leading to
the death of targeted tissues, and it can be distinguished from the
related term necrosis in several ways. Senescence is the age-
dependent disassembly and degeneration process of cells, organs,
or organisms that is controlled by the interaction of environmental
factors with genetically regulated developmental programs.
Necrosis, despite having some overlap with senescence, is usually
defined as non-programmed cell death that is directly caused by
physical damage, poisons (such as herbicides or toxins released
by pathogens), or nonphysiological conditions, such as tempera-
ture or anoxia. Abscission refers to the separation of cell layers
that occurs at the bases of leaves, floral parts, and fruits, which
allows them to be shed easily without damaging the plant.

In plants, senescence at the cellular level involves genetically
encoded and actively controlled cellular suicide, otherwise known
as programmed cell death (PCD). PCD is induced as an intrinsic
part of regular development in specific cell types or tissues, and
can also be triggered by abiotic and biotic stresses (Figure 23.2).
Different forms of PCD play crucial roles in development, immunity,
and response to the environment.

Organ senescence (the senescence of whole leaves, branches,

flowers, or fruits) occurs at various stages of vegetative and
reproductive development and typically includes abscission of the
senescing organ. The importance of organ senescence to plant
fitness is easily seen in leaf senescence. In annual plants, leaf
senescence serves as a means to disassemble and release the
nutrients accumulated during the growth phase and to relocate
them to developing seeds for the next generation. In trees, the
nutrients disassembled during leaf senescence are relocated
and stored in stems or roots in preparation for the next season.

Whole-plant senescence, when the entire
plant senesces and dies, differs from aging in an-
imals and is much more variable. For example,
individual plant life spans may range from a few
weeks for some desert annuals to as long as 4600
years for bristlecone pine. Clonal perennial plants
can live even longer. Because of the presence of
continuously dividing apical meristems, plants
could potentially live forever, yet all apical meri-
stems eventually fail and the plant dies.

We  begin  this  chapter  with  a  brief  over-
view  of  the  features  of  and  mechanisms  re-
sponsible for cell death in plants. We then in-
troduce the various forms of PCD, including
senescence-associated cell death, and discuss
how a key process, autophagy, is involved in
plant cell death. Next we turn to senescence
at the organ level, focusing on leaf senescence
and abscission and describing the mechanism
of orderly degeneration during leaf senescence
and its regulation by developmental and en-
vironmental signals. Finally, we discuss the
factors that govern the two different types of
whole-plant senescence: monocarpic and poly-
carpic senescence.

 23.1  Programmed Cell Death

All eukaryotic organisms have evolved mecha-
nisms of programmed cell death. In multicellular
plants and animals, the organized destruction
of cells is required for normal growth and de-
velopment and for the removal of unwanted,
damaged, or infected cells. PCD can be initiated
by specific developmental signals or by poten-
tially lethal events, such as pathogen attacks or
errors in DNA replication during cell division. It
involves the expression of a characteristic set of
genes that orchestrates the dismantling of cellu-
lar components and ultimately causes cell death.
PCD, sometimes referred to as  apoptosis
(Greek for “falling off”) or autolysis, involves the
controlled activities of endonucleases, ribonu-
cleases, bifunctional nucleases, and proteases in
the cell undergoing the process. Endonucleases
digest the chromosomes, producing fragments
that form a “ladder” when the DNA is size-
separated by gel electrophoresis. A wide variety
of proteases, including caspase-like cysteine

Distinct types of PCD occur in plants
Various forms of PCD have been shown to be an intrinsic
part of normal plant development, as well as responses
to biotic and abiotic stresses. Developmental PCD func-
tions to eliminate senescing or no-longer-required cells,
or to generate tissues composed of modified cell “corps-
es” that retain structural or storage functions when dead.
This process occurs during gametophyte maturation,
degeneration of embryo suspensor cells, formation of
xylem tracheary elements, root development, and senes-
cence (see Figure 23.2). Although senescence-associated
cell death is a form of developmental PCD, it has many
distinct features. During senescence, PCD occurs over
broad areas of plant organs and at a relatively slow rate,
whereas other types of developmental PCD involve
rapid and localized cell death. The slow progression
of PCD in senescing leaf cells appears to be required to
allow sufficient time for remobilization of nutrients and
their translocation to other parts of the plant. Optimal
reallocation of nutrients accumulated during the grow-
ing season is critical for plant fitness and requires precise
control of senescence-associated cell death.

The  hypersensitive  response  and  other  forms  of
pathogen-triggered PCD play prominent roles in plant
biotic defense responses and are distinct from develop-
mental PCD. The types of PCD involved in responses
to abiotic and biotic stresses are discussed in detail in
Chapters 15 and 24, respectively.

Developmental PCD and pathogen-triggered
PCD involve distinct processes
The two broad types of PCD in plants differ not only in
function but in the associated molecular and cytolog-
ical processes. Plant cells are fundamentally different
from animal cells in that they are surrounded by rigid
cell walls. Because of the presence of the cell wall, and
thus the absence of phagocytes (i.e., animal cells spe-
cialized for engulfing and digesting cells targeted for
destruction), the cytological changes that occur during
apoptosis in animals seldom, if ever, occur in plants. In-
stead, ultrastructural studies have led to the identifica-
tion of two plant-specific cytological pathways of PCD:
the vacuolar type associated with developmental PCD
and the hypersensitive response-type associated with
pathogen-triggered PCD (Figure 23.3).

Vacuolar-type PCD occurs during normal develop-
ment and reflects the fact that the large central vacuole
is the main repository of proteases, nucleases, and other
lytic enzymes. The cytological changes associated with
tracheary element differentiation, during which cells

undergo vacuolar-type PCD, are illustrated in Figure
23.3A. Before PCD commences, however, hormones
such  as  methyl  jasmonate  (MeJA),  ethylene,  auxin,
and strigolactones help prime the cells for PCD. Hor-
mone signaling directs transcriptional reprogramming,
leading to the activation of developmental PCD genes
(e.g., genes encoding proteases and nucleases). For ex-
ample, in Arabidopsis a member of the NAC transcrip-
tion factor family (TF), a regulator of leaf senescence,
induces the enzyme BIFUNCTIONAL NUCLEASE 1
(BFN1), which degrades DNA and RNA. During devel-
opmental PCD, the vacuolar membrane, or tonoplast,
ruptures and releases the vacuolar contents, including
several proteases. For example, the Arabidopsis cyste-
ine proteases XYLEM CYSTEINE PEPTIDASE 1 (XCP1),
XCP2, and METACASPASE 9 (MC9) have been shown
to promote cytoplasmic clearance in differentiating
tracheary elements.

After hormones have primed cells for developmen-
tal PCD, the process may be initiated by various second
messengers, including a rise in the Ca2+ concentration,
the accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), or
a drop in intracellular pH. During vacuolar-type PCD,
the vacuole swells and either permeabilizes or rup-
tures, releasing hydrolases into the cytosol and causing
large-scale degradation. The cytosol and all its organ-
elles, as well as the plasma membrane, are completely
broken down, and in many cases the cell wall is either
partially or completely digested as well, as in endo-
sperm tissue. Cell wall degradation does not occur in
cells that have acquired lignified secondary cell walls
during the process, such as tracheary elements and
fibers.  (Senescence-associated  cell  death  involving
vacuolar-type PCD is described in WEB TOPIC 23.2.)

Hypersensitive response-type PCD is induced by a
range of abiotic stresses or by detection of a pathogen
during the hypersensitive response (see Chapter 24).
Cytological hallmarks that distinguish hypersensitive
response-type PCD from vacuolar-type PCD include
vacuolar  water  loss  and  cell  shrinkage  (see Figure
23.3B). Because the contents of the vacuoles are not re-
leased into the cytoplasm during this type of PCD, the
corpses of necrotic cells remain largely unprocessed.
Hypersensitive response-type PCD will be discussed
in greater detail in Chapter 24.

In autophagy, the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) first
gives rise to a cup-shaped, membranous cisterna called
the phagophore (Figure 23.4). In animals, the pha-
gophore has been shown to form at a specialized site
on the ER. The early phagophore then acquires addi-
tional membrane lipids, expands, and pinches off from
the ER. The expansion and fusion of the phagophore
enable it to engulf cytoplasmic components targeted

for destruction, which includes misfolded proteins, ri-
bosomes, ER, and mitochondria. The phagophore be-
comes spherical, and the inner and outer phospholipid
bilayers fuse to form the completed autophagosome,
which is surrounded by a double membrane. In plants,
the outer membrane of the autophagosome fuses with
the tonoplast. In the process, a single-membrane vesi-
cle called the autophagic body enters the vacuole and
is degraded (see Figure 23.4). The monomers (amino
acids, sugars, nucleosides, etc.) generated by hydrolytic
breakdown of the autophagic body are returned to the
cytosol for reuse, either as an energy source or as build-
ing blocks for new cellular structures.

During embryogenesis, autophagy may be
required for the normal differentiation of some
types of cells. The role of autophagy in develop-
mental PCD has been studied in somatic embryos
of Norway spruce. Suppression of ATG5 or ATG6
did not inhibit cell death, but it impaired the formation
of a morphologically normal suspensor. The impaired
suspensor phenotype is caused by an alteration in the
way the cells die, causing a switch from vacuolar cell
death, which is associated with gradual but complete
cell clearance, to rapid cell death with typical signs of
necrosis. The changes in the mode of cell death in atg
mutants imply that autophagy plays an ongoing role in
developmental PCD, rather than simply acting as an ini-
tiator. Cell disassembly during vacuolar PCD is highly
ordered and regulated, which requires the maintenance
of cellular activity to prevent necrosis. Autophagy is like-
ly to be involved by allowing growth of the vacuole as
well as by providing terminally differentiated cells with
substrates for energy metabolism during developmental
PCD. This would account for the fact that suppression of
autophagy during somatic embryo development leads
to necrosis.

Autophagy is required for nutrient recycling
during plant senescence
In non-senescing cells, autophagy serves as a homeostat-
ic mechanism that maintains the metabolic and struc-
tural integrity of the cell. The inhibitory role of autoph-
agy on plant senescence was demonstrated by means of
genetic analysis. As shown in Figure 23.6, Arabidopsis
mutants with defective autophagy exhibit accelerated
leaf senescence. Further molecular and biochemical
studies revealed that during leaf senescence, enhance-
ment  of  autophagy  facilitates  the  degradation  and
clearance of toxic and damaged cellular components.
The recycled cell material is then used to remobilize
nutrients throughout the plant. Therefore, as in other
types of PCD, autophagy appears to play both pro-death
and pro-survival roles during plant senescence. These
antagonistic roles sustain the life of senescing cells by
maintaining cellular energy levels and promoting the
controlled degradation of cell constituents. The tight

 23.2 The Leaf Senescence Syndrome

All leaves, including those of evergreens, undergo senes-
cence—whether in response to age-dependent factors,
environmental cues, biotic stress, or abiotic stress. In
nature, the leaf senescence syndrome is typically ob-
served in the autumn leaves turning from green to yel-
low, orange, or red in trees and other perennial plants.
Annual plants such as grain crops also undergo leaf se-
nescence as part of whole-plant-level senescence at the
grain-filling stage.

Senescence in animal cells primarily refers to mitotic
(or replicative) senescence—that is, loss of the capacity
for further cell division upon aging. In contrast, leaf
senescence is postmitotic senescence, in which a de-
generative process occurs after cellular maturation or
differentiation and leads to cell death. Leaf senescence
is a specialized form of PCD that allows the efficient re-
mobilization of nutrients from the source leaf to growing
vegetative or reproductive sinks via the phloem. As is
the case for the other examples of PCD shown in Figure
23.2, leaf senescence is an evolutionarily selected process
that contributes to the overall fitness of the plant.

Leaves undergo massive structural and
biochemical changes during leaf senescence
During senescence, leaf cells are subject to a dramatic
transition in cellular metabolism and an orderly deg-
radation of cellular structures. Chloroplasts are the
first organelles to be targeted for breakdown (Figure
23.9). Chloroplast degeneration begins with chloro-
phyll breakdown, the first visible symptom of senes-
cence. Protein and RNA degradation parallels the loss
of photosynthetic activity, releasing nutrients, such as
nitrogen, phosphorus, metal ions, and minerals, that are
transferred out of the leaf.

In senescing leaves, anabolic activities such as protein
synthesis generally decline, which can be observed as
a decline in the number of polysomes and ribosomes
during the early stages of leaf senescence. Consistent
with the decrease in protein synthesis, the synthesis of
rRNAs and tRNAs also decreases during leaf senescence.
Concurrently, catabolic activities increase, including the
hydrolysis of macromolecules, such as proteins, lipids,
nucleic acids, and pigments. Chloroplasts contain about
70% of the total leaf protein, most of which consists of
Rubisco localized in the stroma, and light-harvesting
chlorophyll-binding protein II (LHCP II) associated
with the thylakoid membranes (see Chapters 9 and 10).
Senescence-associated stromal and vacuolar proteases
play key roles in chloroplast protein degradation. Ca-
tabolism and remobilization of chloroplast proteins are
thus the primary source of amino acids and nitrogen
for sink organs. A variety of lipid-degrading enzymes,
including phospholipase D, phosphatidic acid phospha-
tase, and lipoxygenase, are involved in hydrolysis and
metabolism of membrane lipids. The increased activity
of RNases during leaf senescence combined with the de-
cline in RNA biosynthesis results in a massive decrease
in total RNA levels.

Like the chloroplasts, other organelles, such as the
peroxisome, also undergo biochemical changes as senes-
cence proceeds. In contrast, the nucleus and mitochon-
dria remain intact until the later stages of senescence.
This reflects the need for leaf cells to maintain gene ex-
pression and energy production to properly complete
the senescence program. However, chromatin conden-
sation and DNA laddering, typical symptoms of PCD,
are eventually observed in senescing leaves. The leaf
cells finally undergo disintegration of the plasma and
vacuolar membranes, leading to disruption of cellular
homeostasis and death.

TRANSLATIONAL REGULATION During leaf se-
nescence, synthesis of the large and small subunits of
Rubisco is controlled at the translational level. The large
and small subunits are encoded in the plastid and nu-

stoichiometrically to form the Rubisco holoenzyme, and
thus their expression needs to be tightly coordinated.
However, mRNA levels for the small subunit decline
faster during leaf senescence than do those for the large
subunit, creating an imbalance. Remarkably, this differ-
ence in mRNA levels is compensated for by alterations in
their rates of translation. A knockdown mutation of the
Arabidopsis PLASTID RIBOSOMAL SMALL SUBUNIT
PROTEIN 17 (PRPS17) gene leads to reduced amounts
of PRPS17 protein and delayed leaf senescence pheno-
types, consistent with a regulatory role for the transla-
tional regulation of plastid genes in leaf senescence.

kinases in leaf senescence. The role of reversible protein
phosphorylation in leaf senescence is demonstrated by

senescence, thereby inhibiting leaf senescence.

the molecular components of the ubiquitination path-

type ubiquitin ligases are also involved in regulating
leaf senescence.

LIGHT  SIGNALING Light deprivation can induce
premature leaf senescence in plants. In Arabidopsis, a
high red:far red light ratio (high R:FR) inhibits leaf se-
nescence, while a low R:FR promotes it, suggesting that
light regulation of leaf senescence is mediated by one
of the phytochromes (see Chapter 16). Phytochrome B
(phyB) has been proposed to be the main photoreceptor
responsible for the high R:FR-mediated retardation of
senescence. Phytochromes that have absorbed red light
inactivate phytochrome-interacting a basic helix–loop–
helix (bHLH) TFs (PIFs), which inhibit light responses

REACTIVE  OXYGEN  SPECIES There  is  growing
evidence that reactive oxygen species (ROS), especial-
ly H2O2, play important roles as signals during leaf se-
nescence. ROS are toxic chemicals that cause oxidative
damage to DNA, proteins, and membrane lipids (see
WEB TOPIC 13.7 and Chapter 15). They are produced
primarily as by-products of normal metabolic processes,
such as respiration and photosynthesis, in chloroplasts,
mitochondria, and peroxisomes. They can also be pro-
duced on the plasma membrane. However, ROS do not
trigger senescence by causing physicochemical damage
to the cell, but rather act as signals that activate genet-
ically programmed pathways of gene expression that
lead to regulated cell death events.

In Arabidopsis, WRKY53 gene expression increases
in leaves at the time of bolting (rapid stem elongation
associated with flowering and leaf senescence). Leaf
H2O2 levels increase at the time of bolting as well. Treat-
ing leaves with H2O2 has been shown to induce the

expression of WRKY53. Thus, there is good circumstan-
tial evidence that H2O2 acts as a signal that triggers leaf
senescence in Arabidopsis. ROS signaling during leaf se-
nescence is coupled to the activity of the MAPK signal
transduction pathway.

SUGARS Besides serving as an energy source and as
building blocks for macromolecules, sugars may also act
as signaling molecules, regulating metabolic pathways
as well as developmental events. For example, trehalose
6-phosphate, which stimulates starch biosynthesis (see
Chapter 10), may serve as a signal that couples starch
biosynthesis to the carbon status of the cytosol in leaves.
Studies have shown that high concentrations of sugars
lower photosynthetic activity and may even trigger
leaf senescence when the sugar concentration exceeds
a certain threshold. Sugar-induced senescence is espe-
cially important under conditions of low nitrogen avail-
ability. It has recently been shown that both trehalose
6-phosphate and sugars accumulate in senescing Arabi-
dopsis leaves. This suggests that trehalose 6-phosphate
may play a role in the onset of leaf senescence under
conditions of high carbon availability.

 23.4 Abscission

The shedding of leaves, fruits, flowers, and unhealthy
structures is termed abscission (see WEB TOPICS 23.4
and 23.5). Abscission takes place within specific layers
of cells in the abscission zone. In leaves, the abscission
zone is located near the base of the petiole (Figure
23.19A–C). Prior to abscission, a separation layer forms
within the abscission zone. In flowers, the abscission
zone is positioned where petals and sepals are attached
to the receptacle. The abscission zone can often be mor-
phologically identified as one or more layers of isodi-
ametrically flattened cells and becomes differentiated
during organ development, many months before organ
separation actually takes place. The abscission zone con-
sists of two neighboring cell types: residuum cells in the
receptacle and secession cells in the abscising organs,
both of which have distinct cellular activities and struc-
tures (Figure 23.19D and E).

Organ abscission involves cell separation, which plays
a crucial role in many developmental processes in plants,
including seed germination, the formation of stomata,
pollen tube growth, and fruit dehiscence. Some of these
processes are particularly important for agriculture. For
example, crop domestication that led to the development
of cultivated rice and maize (corn; Zea mays) involves a
reduction in seed shattering—an important agricultural
trait. Because plant cells are embedded within cell walls,
cell separation during developmental processes requires
the controlled breakdown of the surrounding cell walls.
During abscission, the middle lamella binding the cell
walls of the two neighboring cell types in the abscission
zone is degraded. The dissolution of the walls between
the cells of the separation layer subsequently results
in the organ being shed from the plant. Uncontrolled
cell wall processing by cell wall–hydrolyzing enzymes
during organ abscission results in cell separation at ecto-
pic sites, interfering with precise abscission of the organs
(see subsequent sections for more information).

Molecular genetic studies of Arabidopsis and toma-
to have added to our current understanding of organ
abscission. The process of organ abscission can be di-
vided into four distinct developmental phases, during
which the cells of the abscission zone become competent
to respond to developmental and environmental cues,
including ethylene and auxin (Figure 23.21).

1. Abscission zone formation and abscission suppression

phase. Prior to the perception of any signal (internal
or external) that initiates the abscission process, an
abscission zone forms at the base of the separating
organs. Abscission can be induced only after the
abscission zone is formed and differentiated. Early
in the abscission zone formation and abscission
suppression phase of leaves, auxin from the leaf
blade prevents abscission by maintaining the cells
of the abscission zone in an ethylene-insensitive
state. It has long been known that removal of the
leaf blade (the site of auxin production) promotes
petiole abscission. Application of exogenous auxin
to petioles from which the leaf blade has been
removed delays the abscission process. It remains
unclear how and when abscission zone cell
identity is specified.

2. Abscission induction phase. A reduction or reversal

in the auxin gradient from the leaf blade, normally
associated with leaf senescence, causes the
abscission zone to become sensitive to ethylene.
As abscission approaches, the amount of auxin
from the leaf blade decreases and the ethylene level
rises. Treatments that accelerate leaf senescence
may promote abscission by interfering with auxin
synthesis or transport in the leaf. Ethylene appears
to decrease the activity of auxin by reducing
its synthesis and transport and increasing its
catabolism. The reduction in the concentration of
free auxin increases the response of specific target
cells in the abscission zone to ethylene.

The determinacy of shoot apical meristems
is developmentally regulated
Plants are often described as having indeterminate
growth due to the activities of the apical meristems, but
the determinacy of the apical meristems is also under
strict developmental control. For example, shoot apical
meristems may be continuously meristematic (indeter-
minate), or may cease activity (be determinate), either
by differentiating into a terminal organ such as a flower,
or by undergoing growth arrest or senescence. Indeed,
the growth habits, life cycles, and senescence profiles of
different plant species are intimately connected to their
patterns of apical meristem determinacy.

contrast, polycarpic perennials retain a population of in-

reproductive and determinate.

Nutrient redistribution may trigger
senescence in monocarpic plants
A diagnostic feature of monocarpic senescence is the
ability to delay senescence well beyond the plant’s nor-
mal life span by the removal of the reproductive struc-
tures. For example, repeated depodding enables soybean
plants to remain vegetative for many years under favor-
able growing conditions, leading to a treelike appear-
ance. What is the relationship between fruit develop-
ment (reproduction) and whole-plant senescence? One
of the earliest explanations for monocarpic senescence
was based on the redistribution of vital nutrients via the
phloem from vegetative sources to reproductive sinks.
Plant hormones are considered to play important
roles in regulating source–sink relationships. Many
studies have shown that alterations in the source–sink
relations of vegetative and reproductive tissues can af-
fect the course of senescence. As discussed earlier in rela-
tion to leaf senescence, cytokinins increase sink strength
in leaves and delay leaf senescence. During monocarpic
senescence in peas, high endogenous GA levels in the
vegetative buds are correlated with high sink strength,
vigorous vegetative growth, and delayed whole-plant
senescence. In contrast, high auxin levels in floral buds
are correlated with high sink strength of the reproduc-
tive structures and rapid reproductive development fol-
lowed by whole-plant senescence.

If developing seeds and fruits are such strong sinks
that they can trigger the senescence of the rest of the
plant, why do male plants of dioecious species such as
spinach (Spinacea oleracea), which produce neither seeds

nor fruits, senesce at the same age as female plants,
which produce copious seeds and fruits? Experiments
carried out in the late 1950s showed that removing the
tiny pollen-producing flowers from the male plants de-
layed senescence to the same extent that removing the
female flowers did. This result seemed to contradict
the resource redistribution model, since it was assumed
that the use of carbohydrate resources by staminate
spinach flowers would be negligible compared with
the carbohydrate use of pistillate flowers. However,
more recent studies have shown that the nutritional
demand of the staminate flowers actually exceeds the
nutritional demand of the pistillate flowers, especially
during early flower development. This could be a de-
termining factor in triggering monocarpic senescence,
even in male plants.

Although resource redistribution may well trigger
monocarpic senescence, the critical compound is not
carbohydrate, because many studies have now shown
that the carbohydrate content of leaves increases during
senescence. This observation is consistent with the abil-
ity of exogenous sugars to trigger leaf senescence. Rath-
er than carbohydrate loss, alterations in source–sink
relations caused by floral development may induce a
global shift in the hormonal or nutrient balance of the
vegetative organs. For example, a loss of nitrogen cou-
pled with a simultaneous accumulation of carbohydrate
would cause an increase in the C:N ratio, which has been
associated with vacuolar-type PCD in senescing leaves.

and organismal levels and is intricately regulated through

is critical for plants’ fitness.

reproduce multiple times before senescing
(Figures 23.23, 23.24).

 ■ Nutrient redistribution from vegetative structures
to reproductive sinks may trigger whole-plant
senescence in monocarpic plants.

 ■ While growth efficiency in trees declines with
increasing tree size, leaf mass increases as the
square of trunk diameter and can overcome this loss
in efficiency, until internal or external factors initiate
whole-tree senescence (Figures 23.25, 23.26).
