Vegetative Growth and
Organogenesis: Branching
and Secondary Growth

Seedlings and juvenile plants tend to allocate more photo-

synthate to vertical growth than to lateral growth because
it helps them compete more effectively for sunlight above-

ground, and for water belowground. As the plant increases its
biomass, indeterminate growth is sustained by the emergence
of additional shoot and root axes, a process called branching. In
shoots, branches arise superficially from buds located in the axils
of the leaves (axillary buds), while in roots branches are initiated
internally in a layer of cells called the pericycle, located on the
interior side of the endodermis (see Chapter 1).

Variations in the spatial arrangements, angles, and morph-
ologies of branches generate the architecture of the shoot and
root systems. Shoot architecture is a complex adaptive feature
that depends on the regulated production of determinate lateral
organs, such as leaves, as well as the regulated formation and out-
growth of indeterminate branch systems. The “Green Revolution”
of the mid-20th century, a period of dramatic yield increases in
global agriculture due to the introduction of high-yielding varieties,
was brought about in part by intentional modifications in the
shoot architecture of key cereal crops (see WEB TOPIC 19.1). Root
architecture has comparable levels of complexity that arise from
the regulated formation and outgrowth of indeterminate lateral
roots. These include lateral roots that branch from primary roots
as well as lateral roots that branch from previously formed lateral
roots. Some root systems also include adventitious roots, which
form in structures other than roots—for example, from the lower
underground stem nodes in both monocots and eudicots, and
from aboveground stems or branches in the case of epiphytes.
A defining feature of primary growth is the near absence
of lignified cell walls in primary tissues other than the xylem
conducting elements. The relative lack of lignin in herbaceous
plants limits their abilities to grow very tall or to support large
branches. For herbaceous annuals, biennials, and even some
perennial plants that are either small in stature or climbers that
rely on other plants for support, the vegetative growth phase of
development consists entirely of primary growth. In perennial
plants (trees and shrubs), however, primary growth is quickly

meristems, which greatly enhance the rate of increase

genetic control mechanisms.

with a description of some of the regulatory pathways
that underly the production of secondary xylem and

plant biomass by greatly extending the limits on height.

tree biomass continues to increase in the lateral direction
because of the activity of the vascular cambium, which
persists until the tree dies (see Chapter 23).

Integration of environmental and
hormonal branching signals is required
for plant fitness
In some cases, a plant can adjust its default shoot
branching pattern in response to environmental
conditions. Two classic examples are the shade
avoidance response and the nutrient deficiency
response. Both of these responses involve the
regulatory pathways described earlier.

Plants avoid shade through enhanced shoot
elongation and suppressed branching. Shade
avoidance involves phytochrome B signaling in
response to the decreased R:FR light ratio that
results when sunlight is filtered through green
leaves containing chlorophyll. Genetic studies
in Arabidopsis have shown that phytochrome B
requires both auxin and strigolactone signaling
pathways, as well as the bud-specific BRC1 and
BRC2 genes to inhibit axillary bud outgrowth
under shading conditions.

The response to nutrient deficiency is medi-
ated by strigolactones. Well-nourished plants
are  bushy,  whereas  plants  growing  under
nutrient-poor  conditions  tend  to  be  weakly
branched. The involvement of strigolactones
in this branching response presumably relates,
evolutionarily, to the role of these hormones
in enhancing nutrient acquisition. Mycorrhi-
zal plant species secrete strigolactones into the
rhizosphere to promote the mycorrhizal symbi-
osis and thereby enhance nutrient uptake. The
details vary among different species, but even
in non-mycorrhizal plant species, strigolactone
concentration in the shoot is elevated under low
nutrient conditions. The increase in strigolac-
tone concentration suppresses axillary bud out-
growth. Reduced branching in response to nu-
trient deficiency is adaptive because the plant is
able to focus its resources on development of the
main shoot and existing branches, rather than
on promoting the growth of additional branches
that cannot be supported by the nutrient sup-
ply. In high nitrogen conditions, which promote
branching, cytokinin synthesis in the root is
up-regulated. Plants in which multiple cytoki-
nin biosynthesis genes are mutated are unable to
increase branching in response to high nitrogen,
suggesting a functional connection.

 19.2  Root Branching
and Architecture

Most living vascular plants have root systems that an-
chor them to the soil and absorb water and nutrients
required for growth. Through natural selection, plant
root systems have evolved the ability to adapt to vari-
ations in soil composition, competition with other root
systems, abiotic stress, and biotic interactions in the
rhizosphere. Diversity and phenotypic plasticity are
therefore key features of root architecture and are es-
sential to the plant’s survival. Recent research on the
structure of root systems has been driven by advanc-
es in root system phenotyping (see WEB TOPIC 19.4).
These and other studies have shown that plants have
evolved complex control mechanisms that regulate root
system architecture. Root system architecture is the
spatial configuration of the entire root system in the soil.
More specifically, root system architecture refers to the
geometric arrangement of individual roots within the
plant’s root system in the three-dimensional soil space.
Root systems are composed of different root types, and
different plant species are able to control the types of
roots they produce as well as their growth rates, degree
of branching, and growth angles.

Postembryonic roots arising from existing roots are
termed lateral or branch roots, while roots arising from
non-root tissues are termed adventitious roots. In gen-
eral, monocots such as the grasses have fibrous root
systems consisting mainly of adventitious roots which
in turn produce abundant lateral roots. By contrast, eu-
dicots such as Arabidopsis typically produce a single
dominant primary root, or taproot, which gives rise to
all of the lateral roots and their branches.

In shoots, leaf and axillary bud primordia are period-
ically initiated on the flanks of apical meristems in spiral
arrangements or other precise patterns—a phenomenon
referred to as phyllotaxis (see Chapter 18). Lateral root
primoridia are initiated in the zone of differentiation
of the growing root (see Chapter 18). The arrangement
of lateral roots along the surface of a root is termed
rhizotaxis, and is determined by a combination of en-
dogenous and environmental factors.

During root growth, the developmental zones (meri-
stematic [cell division], elongation, and maturation [dif-
ferentiation]) move acropetally by the addition of new
cells at the growing tip (see Figure 19.9A). As a result,
the developing lateral roots are shifted in the shootward
direction through the maturation zone toward the fully
mature region of the root. (Note that the developing
lateral roots do not change their position relative to
the root base.) This process generates a developmental
gradient between the youngest lateral root primordia
near the growing tip and the oldest lateral roots near
the root base.

Lateral root primordia arise from the xylem
pole pericycle cells
In most plants, including Arabidopsis, lateral roots are
initiated exclusively in the pericycle, the tissue located
just inside the endodermis (see Chapter 1). The arrange-
ment of vascular tissue in the root is characterized as
diarch (as in Arabidopsis), triarch, tetrarch, and so on,
depending on the number of xylem arms, which cor-
responds to the number of protoxylem strands (Figure
19.8). The phloem pole pericycle cells are the pericycle
cells located adjacent to the phloem strands, while the
xylem pole pericycle cells are adjacent to the tips of the
xylem arms. In Arabidopsis and most other eudicots
and nongrass monocots, lateral root initiation occurs ex-
clusively in the xylem pole pericycle cells, while in the
grasses (e.g., maize and barley) lateral root initiation is
restricted to the phloem pole pericycle cells. This differ-
ence raises the important question of how the pericycle
cells of the xylem pole (in the case of most angiosperms)
or the phloem pole (in the case of some monocots) be-
come specified from the other pericycle cells. Because
most of the research in this area has been conducted on
Arabidopsis, we will focus on the development of lateral
roots from xylem pole pericycle cells.

Transcription factors regulate the
gravitropic setpoint angles of lateral
roots and shoots
As mentioned in Section 19.1, the various
components of shoot systems and root sys-
tems tend to grow at characteristic gravit-
ropic setpoint angles. By convention, a pri-
mary root growing vertically downward has
a gravitropic setpoint angle of 0˚, while that
of a primary shoot growing vertically upward
is 180˚. Lateral shoots and roots tend to have setpoint
angles that deviate from the vertical.

Immediately following emergence, the lateral roots
have  not  yet  developed  an  elongation  zone,  which
means they are incapable of gravitropic bending. As a
result, newly emerged lateral roots initially grow away
from the primary axis at an angle perpendicular to grav-
ity. This horizontal growth helps expand the root sys-
tem in the radial direction, increasing access to nutrients
and stabilizing the plant. Once lateral roots have formed
elongation zones, they bend at their predetermined
gravitropic setpoint angles, and continue growing at
this same angle for a variable period of time called the
plateau phase. In general, early lateral roots have longer
plateau phases than later lateral roots. Eventually, anoth-
er gravitropic response kicks in, which causes a second
bending with more pronounced downward growth, re-
sulting in an angle between 0˚ and 30˚.

In general, if a graviresponsive branch (root or shoot) is
mechanically displaced, upward or downward, from its
gravitropic setpoint angle, it will undergo tropic growth
to shift it back toward that gravitropic setpoint angle.

setpoint angles can be negatively gravitropic—that is,
grow against the gravity vector. Conversely, nonvertical
shoot branches can be positively gravitropic and grow
downward. This observation provides a simple demon-
stration that the mechanistic basis for the maintenance of
graviresponsive nonvertical growth cannot lie solely in

 19.3 Secondary Growth

All gymnosperms and most eudicots—including woody
shrubs and trees, as well as large herbaceous species—
develop  lateral  meristems  that  cause  radial  growth
(growth in width) in stems and roots. Growth in girth that
results from lateral meristems is called secondary growth
(Figure 19.14), and it contributes to the continual enlarge-
ment of woody plants, allowing them to become thicker
and more robust. It has been estimated that wood makes
up more than half the world’s biomass, and forest biomes
provide homes for about 80% of the world’s biodiversity.
The development of a vascular cambium responsible
for secondary growth has occurred repeatedly during
the evolution of vascular plants (see WEB TOPIC 19.6).
Wood is composed of secondary xylem tissues with
thickened secondary cell walls, and its formation is dy-
namic and continuous. Physiologically, wood serves
transport, storage, and mechanical functions, and the re-
sponse of wood to various environmental factors reflects
changes that facilitate these functions. (For a discussion
of adaptive secondary growth responses to environmen-
tal stresses, see WEB TOPIC 19.7.)

Secondary growth occurs in more mature parts of

also responsible for the quiescence of the organizer, but
the presence of these transcription factors is not sufficient
to establish cambium on its own. In contrast, a local peak
in auxin signaling within vascular tissues is sufficient to
respecify a new cambium organizer (see Figure 19.16B).

ganizers in other meristems—the cambium organizer is
dynamic. When the xylem cells undergo programmed
cell death to differentiate into xylem vessels, the previous
organizer is replaced by initial cells that acquire orga-
nizer identity, ensuring the maintenance of the vascular
cambium (see WEB TOPIC 19.9). The role of organizers
in phloem cell formation is still poorly understood.

The xylem cells acting as the organizer send mobile
signals (see Figure 19.16B, dashed arrow) to adjacent
cells, giving them stem cell capabilities. All the cells in
physical contact with the xylem (both xylem procambial
cells and xylem pole pericycle cells) can become initial
cells and generate vascular cambium. The requirement
for direct contact with the xylem indicates that the abil-

sition and the signals received from the organizer during
secondary development. Lineage tracing experiments
have confirmed that a single bifacial initial cell in each
radial cell file, marked by the expression of transcription
factor AINTEGUMENTA (ANT), is able to divide, alter-
nately producing both phloem and xylem precursors.

The gene networks that control secondary
meristems share similarities and differences
with those that control the apical meristems
The patterns of growth and development during sec-
ondary growth are governed by processes and genes
that are similar to those that regulate development of
the SAM and RAM (see Chapter 18). This similarity has
aided the molecular dissection of the mechanisms con-
trolling secondary growth.

As we saw in Chapter 18, the activities of initials
in both the SAM and the RAM depend on the activity
of WOXs: WUSCHEL (WUS) promotes the activity of
initials in the SAM (Figure 19.17A), while WOX5 con-
tributes to the function of initials in the RAM (Figure
19.17B). The vascular cambium appears to rely on a sim-
ilar mechanism, whereby maintenance of the cambium
depends on the activity of WOX4 (Figure 19.17C). WOX4
activation promotes procambial and cambial cell divi-
sions and is required for cambial meristematic activi-
ty (Figure 19.18A). In plants with extensive secondary
growth, such as poplar trees, plants with deficient WOX4
expression have narrower cambium and decreased stem
girth. Like WUS and WOX5, WOX4 is regulated via the
interaction of small peptides with a receptor kinase. In
this case the receptor kinase is PHLOEM INTERCALAT-
ED WITH XYLEM/TDIF RECEPTOR (PXY/TDR) (see
Figure 19.18A), which is preferentially expressed in the
procambium and vascular cambium, while the CLAVA-
TA3/Embryo Surrounding Region-Related (CLE) pep-
tides are expressed in the phloem. However, unlike in
the apical meristems, the expression of CLE peptides
promotes WOX4 activity instead of inhibiting it.

Initial cell maintenance, proliferation, and differenti-
ation also require spatial separation, which is achieved
through transcription factors that define the develop-
mental boundaries. In the SAM, one such class of tran-
scription factors with this function is the LATERAL
ORGAN BOUNDARIES (LBD) family. LBD genes help
establish a boundary between the undifferentiated cells
in the SAM and the differentiating tissues of the leaf pri-
mordium. Members of the LBD family play a similar role
in secondary growth by separating the cambium zone
from the differentiating secondary phloem and xylem.
One phenotype of pxy mutants is that phloem and xylem
layers are intercalated (i.e., a layer of phloem can become
inserted between two layers of xylem, and vice versa).
One of the genes regulated by PXY is LBD4, which is also
controlled by WOX14 and TARGET OF MONOPTEROS
6 (TMO6) and promotes the formation of phloem (see
Figure 19.18A). Expressed at the phloem–procambium
boundary, LBD4 is likely to be involved in defining this
boundary. Similarly, LBD1, which is expressed primarily
in phloem and cambium, has been found to promote the
formation of secondary phloem in Populus.

Several transcription factors, including WOX4, SHORT
VEGETATIVE PHASE (SVP), and PETAL LOSS (PTL),
have dual functions in regulating cambial cell prolifera-
tion and xylem differentiation. When overexpression of
the cambial activator WOX4 was combined with removal
of the PTL cambial inhibitor, the resulting double mutant
showed enhanced radial growth (Figure 19.18B).

cambial meristems, significant differences also exist.

lateral roots in some species (although lateral roots can
sometimes derive from other pericycle cells).

underlying periderm formation are now being uncov-

are also conserved.

Bark has diverse protective
and storage functions
The outer protective layer of trees is commonly referred
to as bark. This term, often applied incorrectly to the per-
iderm alone, actually consists of all the tissues outside
the vascular cambium, including the functional second-
ary phloem, crushed nonfunctional secondary phloem,
crushed primary phloem, and periderm. Therefore, bark
originates from two different meristems, the phellogen
and the vascular cambium. Bark tends to peel easily from
a tree because the vascular cambium, with its dividing cell
layers, is much more fragile than the secondary tissues on
either side. Despite the importance of this array of tissues
to safeguard phloem and protect woody plants from bi-
otic and abiotic threats, the composition of bark and the
genetic pathways controlling its formation are not well
understood, mainly because it is highly diverse among
different species. For instance, in some trees, such as
oaks, the bark is thick and cracked, while in others, such
as cherry trees and birch, it is smooth. Bark can also have
different colors, even among closely related tree species.
Bark also varies in the types and amounts of secondary
metabolites it contains. While many of these are produced
by the plant as a defense mechanism, humans have also
found them to be useful. Classic examples of bark-derived
drugs are the antiseptic and anti-inflammatory salicylic
acid (from willow), the antimalarial quinine (from cin-
chona), and the chemotherapy medication paclitaxel
(from Pacific yew). A recent study analyzed gene expres-
sion and metabolites in the barks of different species of
the Betulaceae family and found that betulin, the triter-
pene responsible for the white color of silver birch, was
much less abundant in other species of the family, such
as black alder. The study also highlighted the importance
of phellem for producing secondary metabolites for plant
defense, since many of the relevant metabolic pathways
function in the formation of this tissue.

Bark can also function as a storage organ. Growth
seasonality imposes a significant challenge for perennial
plants with respect to nutrient use, storage, and recycling.
Although all plant species have mechanisms to recycle,
store, and remobilize nutrients during the growing sea-
son, the seasonal cycling of nitrogen, the most abundant
macronutrient, is a hallmark of the perennial life habit.
For example, nitrogen from the senescing leaves is stored
in the form of bark storage proteins (BSPs) in small vac-
uoles of the phloem parenchyma (inner bark). These pro-
teins are synthesized early in the autumn but are rapidly
mobilized during the spring as growth is reinitiated.

Much of the diversity of plant form comes from their abil-
ity to initiate and pattern new organs such as branches
or lateral roots throughout their lifetime. The organs are
initiated from meristems that are established and main-
tained in similar ways as those associated with primary
development seen in Chapter 18. The trigger to produce a
new organ requires the integration of environmental and
internal signals through the action of mobile signals in a
way that is comparable to the mechanisms that pattern
the cell types, tissues, and organ shapes during primary
development. Specific signaling pathways have also been
repurposed repeatedly throughout evolution to control
different aspects of development. For example, meriste-
matic tissues in the root, shoot, and vascular cambium all
use similar receptor kinase–WOX feedback loops to reg-
ulate initial cell number and position, and the hormone
auxin patterns vasculature, positions lateral organs, and
regulates branch outgrowth, among many other functions.

19.2 Root Branching and Architecture
 ■ There are two types of postembryonic roots: lateral,

or branch, roots arising from existing roots, and
adventitious roots arising from non-root tissues.

 ■ Lateral root primordia arise from the xylem pole

pericycle cells (Figures 19.8, 19.9).

 ■ An auxin clock in the oscillation zone primes the
xylem pole pericycle cells to become lateral root
founder cells (Figure 19.10).

 ■ Shoot and root systems exhibit different architectures
based on branching patterns and gravitropic setpoint
angles, which vary with species and the environment.

 ■ Monocot roots systems are composed largely
of seminal and crown roots, while eudicot root
systems are derived largely from the primary root
(taproot) (Figures 19.11, 19.12).

 ■ Gravitropic setpoint angles are fundamental to
plant architecture and are controlled by IGT
transcription factors (Figure 19.13).

 ■ Root system architecture is highly sensitive to
heterogeneity in the aqueous environment.

 ■ Mycorrhizal relationships among root systems are
ubiquitous and involve nutrient transport along
source–sink gradients.

19.3 Secondary Growth
 ■ Growth in width is accomplished by the vascular cam-
bium and cork cambium, which are secondary bifacial
meristems that give rise to secondary vascular tissue
and the periderm, respectively (Figures 19.14, 19.15).

 ■ Mobile transcription factors establish the pre-

pattern of the vascular cambium (Figure 19.16).
