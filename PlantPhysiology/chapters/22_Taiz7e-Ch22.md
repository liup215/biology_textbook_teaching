Embryogenesis: The Origin
of Plant Architecture

L and plants comprise a monophyletic group called the

embryophytes—plants that make embryos. What sets them
apart from their aquatic, algal sisters is the ability to develop

a multicellular sporophyte following fertilization of the egg cell
by the sperm cell. The fate of the zygote (the fertilized egg cell)
differs greatly among plant groups. For example, in nonvascular
land plants (bryophytes) such as mosses, the zygote undergoes
mitotic divisions to produce a multicellular, diploid sporophyte
that is dependent on the gametophyte (to which it is attached)
for most of its nourishment (see Chapter 1 for overview). The cells
of the moss sporophyte undergo minimal differentiation and
development, producing a relatively simple stalk and capsule that
lack true vascular tissue. In vascular plants (tracheophytes), by
contrast, the zygote divides to produce a diploid sporophyte
that lives independently of the gametophytes that produced it.
Furthermore, cells of vascular plant sporophytes differentiate
into the three main tissue types—dermal, ground, and vascular
tissues—that become further specialized into a wide variety of
cell and tissue types comprising the major organ systems of the
vegetative and reproductive structures. In seed plants (spermato-
phytes, which include gymnosperms and angiosperms), embryo-
genesis terminates with the formation of a dormant (or quiescent)
seed. The analogous resting stage of bryophytes and non-seed
plants is the haploid spore, which represents the start of the
gametophyte phase of the life cycle. Given the primary research
focus on flowering plants, and the tremendous importance of
seed plants to food production, we know much more about
embryogenesis in seed plants than in non-seed plants. Accordingly,
this chapter focuses on the embryogenesis of seed plants, with an
emphasis on the angiosperms. However, it should be kept in mind
that seed plant embryogenesis, which evolved about 320 million
years ago, is derived from a much older process that has defined
land plants since their origin about 450 million years ago.

In seed plants, embryogenesis transforms the single-celled
zygote into a complex, multicellular individual contained within
a mature seed. As such, embryogenesis provides many examples
of developmental processes by which the basic architecture
of the plant is established, including the elaboration of forms
(morphogenesis), the associated formation of functionally

 22.1  Embryogenesis in Monocots

and Eudicots

We begin by comparing embryogenesis in the two main
groups of angiosperms, the monocots and eudicots,
represented by maize (corn; Zea mays) and Arabidop-
sis, respectively. Next we consider the signals that guide
complex patterns of growth and differentiation in the
embryo, including the importance of position-dependent
cues. We conclude our survey of plant embryogenesis
with a discussion of the molecular and genetic mecha-
nisms that translate position-dependent cues into orga-
nized patterns of growth. An overview of the history of
plant embryogenesis can be found in WEB ESSAY 18.1.

Embryogenesis differs between
monocots and eudicots, but also
shares common features
Anatomical comparisons have revealed differences in
the patterns of embryogenesis among different seed
plant groups, such as those between monocots and eu-
dicots and those between non-flowering gymnosperms
and flowering angiosperms. Angiosperm embryogenesis
tends to be more predictable in eudicot species, with two
cotyledons (embryonic leaves), than in monocot species,
with one cotyledon. Embryogenesis in gymnosperms is
more unpredictable than in either eudicots or monocots,
with embryos from the same gymnosperm species ca-
pable of exhibiting a range of cotyledon numbers. Im-
portantly, though, the central elements of embryogenesis
are common among nearly all seed plant species, proba-
bly reflecting shared developmental processes.

Among flowering plants, Arabidopsis (a eudicot) and
maize (a monocot) provide two examples of embryogen-
esis that differ in detail but share certain fundamental
features relating to the establishment of major growth
axes. In both species, developmental and physiological
events follow the same sequence. In both, tissues and or-
gans are established in similar arrangements. However,

there are also major differences between monocot and
eudicot embryogenesis. For example, the shoot apical
meristem is inactive during embryogenesis in Arabidop-
sis, whereas several small leaves are formed during em-
bryogenesis in maize. Furthermore, while Arabidopsis is
representative of a group of plants in which embryonic
cell divisions appear to be strictly controlled and are
nearly invariable among individuals, maize displays a
superficially less ordered pattern of cell divisions. Maize
and Arabidopsis therefore represent opposite ends of a
spectrum in embryological development, from the rel-
atively complex and less predictable to the relatively
simple and more predictable.

Here  we  describe  maize  embryogenesis  in  brief
and Arabidopsis embryogenesis, about which more is
known, in detail.

MAIZE EMBRYOGENESIS The large and nearly ubiq-
uitous grass family (Poaceae or Gramineae), of which
maize is a member, represents a specialized group of
monocots in which, according to one interpretation, the
single cotyledon appears to have become functionally
divided  into  two  structures,  the  scutellum  and  the
coleoptile. The scutellum serves as an absorptive organ
that takes up sugars from the endosperm during germi-
nation, while the coleoptile forms a tubular sheath that
protects the emerging primary leaves from mechanical
damage from the soil. The cell division patterns that give
rise to these specialized monocot structures are necessar-
ily more complex, yet predictable, than those required to
form the two cotyledons of most eudicots. Nevertheless,
despite a lack of obvious regularity in cell division pat-
terns, it is possible to describe embryogenesis in maize
in terms of six morphologically defined developmental
stages (Figure 22.1):

1. Zygotic stage. This stage begins with the fusion
of the haploid egg and sperm to form the zygote
(not shown in Figure 22.1) (see Chapter 21). The
zygote undergoes polarized growth followed by
asymmetric transverse divisions, giving rise to a
small apical cell and an elongated basal cell (see
Figure 22.1A).

2. Globular stage. Following the creation of the apical
and basal cells, a series of variable cell divisions
creates a multilayered globular embryo consisting
of the embryo proper and the larger multicellular
suspensor (see Figure 22.1B).

3. Transition stage. During the early transition stage,

the scutellum appears on the inner side of the
embryo (relative to the future seed coat). By the late
transition stage, the future shoot apical meristem
is evident on the outer side of the embryo, relative
to the future seed coat. (The transition stage is not
shown in Figure 22.1.)

 22.4  Auxin Signaling During
Embryogenesis

As described in Chapters 4 and 18, the hormone
auxin  orchestrates  many  growth  and  develop-
mental processes in plants and has profound ef-
fects during embryogenesis. The distribution of
auxin and auxin signaling activity have both been
mapped using fluorescent reporter constructs that
can report either the presence of active auxin (DII
domain–based reporters, e.g., DII-VENUS) or tran-
scriptional activation in response to auxin (DR5
promotor-based reporters). Using these tools, it is
clear that throughout embryogenesis, cells display
dynamic auxin accumulation and response patterns
(Figure 22.13). At the two-cell stage, the apical cell
shows a more active auxin response than the basal
cell, while at the globular stage the hypophysis and
cells in the lower domain of the embryo proper ex-
hibit higher levels of auxin activity. Indeed, many
stages of embryogenesis, such as the formation of
the radicle (embryonic root), the initiation of coty-
ledons, and the differentiation of vascular tissue, are
closely linked to an increase in local auxin activity.

Spatial patterns of auxin accumulation
regulate key developmental events
Genetic studies have confirmed that increases in
local auxin accumulation are causally related to the
developmental events with which they are associat-
ed. Gene mutations that inhibit auxin biosynthesis
(due to defective biosynthesis genes) or the auxin re-
sponse (due to defective receptors, non-degradable
Aux/IAA repressors, or defective ARFs) specifically
block those stages in embryonic development that
are associated with increases in local auxin activity.
The phenotypes of these mutants include the fail-
ure to initiate roots or cotyledons and the failure to
properly specify vascular and ground tissue. If the
spatial pattern of auxin accumulation provides the
template for patterning in the embryo, what pro-
cesses determine where auxin accumulates?

The spatial pattern of auxin accumulation aris-
es from a combination of local biosynthesis and
transport. The genes responsible for auxin biosyn-
thesis are expressed in specific cells, which become
auxin sources. These cells, however, are not the
only ones that show auxin signaling activity, and
this discrepancy can be explained by polar auxin
transport within the embryo. For example, pin1 mutants
of Arabidopsis, which are defective in polar transport of
auxin, often exhibit abnormalities in embryo develop-
ment such as fused cotyledons (Figure 22.14), consistent
with a role for auxin transport during embryo develop-
ment. The PIN proteins, which function as auxin efflux

The overall output of this mechanism is the activation
of a set of genes that trigger the establishment of the
various pattern elements or cell types. Molecular genetic
studies using mutants, combined with whole-genome
transcriptional analyses, have identified several such
target genes. Given the position dependence of auxin
action, it is to be expected that the genes that are ac-
tivated by this pathway will vary depending on the
context of the cell. These genes encompass a variety of
functions, including a transcription factor, TARGET OF
MONOPTEROS 7 (TMO7), that can move from cell to
cell and contributes to root formation, as well as a reg-
ulator of vascular tissue formation, TMO5, which we
discuss later in the chapter.

One obvious and singular aspect of the radial axis
of the embryo is provided by the protoderm, a tissue
that can be uniquely defined by its superficial position
and which eventually produces the epidermis, a critical
tissue that mediates communication between the plant
and the outside world. Originating early in embryo-
genesis, protodermal cells have a set of exposed walls
that could, in theory, facilitate the exchange of signals
with the external environment or, alternatively, act as a
boundary as signals move from cell to cell within the
embryo. In either case, the protoderm would exhibit
unique properties distinguishing it from the internal
cell layers, and thus provide potential cues for radial

patterning. For example, studies in Citrus have shown
the presence of a cuticle layer on the surface of the em-
bryo from the earliest zygotic stages through maturity,
suggesting that the walls of protodermal cells form a
communication boundary. Some studies also suggest
that the epidermis can act as a physical constraint to the
growth of more internal layers.

Genetic studies have helped us understand the pro-
cesses that contribute to the unique character of the
epidermis. For example, two genes, ARABIDOPSIS
THALIANA MERISTEM LAYER 1 (ATML1) and PRO-
TODERMAL FACTOR 2 (PDF2), have been identified as
having essential roles in promoting the epidermal iden-
tity of superficially positioned cells. Both genes encode
homeodomain transcription factors and are expressed
from early stages of embryogenesis in the outer cells
of the embryo. This expression appears necessary for
the establishment of normal epidermal identity, since
loss-of-function mutant plants have an abnormal epider-
mis in which cells display characteristics normally asso-
ciated with mesophyll cells (Figure 22.16). Conversely,
ectopic (in the wrong location) expression of ATML1
in internal tissues has been shown to induce abnormal
epidermal characteristics. Together, these results suggest
that ATML1 and the related PDF2 are likely to function
by promoting the activity of downstream genes that me-
diate the development of epidermal characters.

tissue, and thereby its composition and structure.

The differentiation of cortical and
endodermal cells involves the intercellular
movement of a transcription factor
The development of endodermal and cortical tissues
provides a classic example of how the radial pattern-
ing process can be regulated by gene activity communi-
cated between adjacent layers. Two Arabidopsis genes,
SCARECROW (SCR) and SHORT ROOT (SHR), are both
essential for the normal formation of cortical and endo-
dermal cell layers. The similar protein sequences encod-

transcription factors, whose name derives from the first
known members, GIBBERELLIN-INSENSITIVE (GAI),
REPRESSOR OF GA1–3 (RGA), and SCR.

discrete layers. This interpretation is supported by the
ability of fass to restore more normal growth patterns.
Much as it rescues wol, fass appears to compensate for
the division defect of scr, and thus provides separate
layers in which distinct endodermal and cortical traits
can be expressed.

 22.6  Formation of the Root and

Shoot Apical Meristems

The most important outcome of embryogenesis is argu-
ably the formation of apical meristems at either end of
the mature embryo. These shoot and root apical meri-
stems are home to the initial cells that sustain the vege-
tative growth of the shoot and root systems. Failure to
initiate either of the two apical meristems leads to the

arrest of seedling growth, indicative of the critical im-
portance of this step in plant development.

Root formation involves MONOPTEROS and
other auxin-regulated transcription factors
As we saw earlier in the chapter, mutants lacking an
embryonic  root  (e.g., monopteros  [ mp]  and bodenlos
[bdl]) have helped elucidate the root-specifying role
played by auxin. Analyses of the genes regulated by
MONOPTEROS led to the discovery of the mobile tran-
scription factor TMO7 and the vascular tissue regulator
TMO5 (discussed earlier in the chapter), but neither of
these alone is sufficient to specify a root. Indeed, genet-
ic analyses have identified two additional transcription
factors that are activated by auxin and which appear to
be responsible for embryonic root formation.

by the PLETHORA 1 (PLT1) and PLETHORA 2 (PLT2)

Another set of transcription factors is activated in the
upper half of the globular embryo, and it appears to
mediate either the establishment of the entire shoot
domain or the shoot apical meristem. For example,
members of a small family of related transcription fac-
tors belonging to the HD-ZIP III class are specifically
expressed in the upper half of the globular embryo.
Expression of these HD-ZIP III genes in the upper half
of the embryo is required for shoot apical meristem
formation in the embryo, as well as for the adaxial–ab-
axial polarity of leaves during vegetative growth (see
Chapter 18). When ectopically expressed in the lower
half of the embryo, one of these factors, PHABULOSA
(PHB), is able to trigger the formation of a second shoot
domain (Figure 22.22C). This is the opposite of what
happens when the PLT protein is artificially expressed
in the upper half of the embryo (Figure 22.22B), sug-
gesting that HD-ZIP III and PLT transcription factors
act antagonistically. Indeed, further experiments have
shown that HD-ZIP III factors inhibit the expression of
PLT genes (Figure 22.22A).

Mutations in the SHOOT MERISTEMLESS (STM) or
WUSCHEL (WUS) genes, which encode transcription
factors, cause a loss of the embryonic shoot apical mer-
istem. Although neither of these two transcription fac-
tors on its own can trigger the formation of new shoot
apical meristems when ectopically expressed in other
tissues, in combination they can. STM and WUS are
both expressed in the apical domain of young embryos,
and it would seem that both are necessary for meristem
formation. Because of this interaction, the position of
the embryonic shoot apical meristem can be precisely
defined by the domain where the expression of the two
genes overlaps.

Figure 22.22 Initiation of shoot and root
apical meristems in the Arabidopsis embryo.
(A) Model for how regulators control initiation of
the shoot and root apical meristems in the 16-cell
Arabidopsis embryo. A three-dimensional cross
section of an embryo is shown in which each cell
type is marked in a different color. The upper tier
of the embryo gives rise to the shoot domain and
the lower tier gives rise to the root domain. At the
heart of the model is a mutually inhibitory inter-
action of HD-Zip III (top tier) and PLT (lower tier)
transcription factors, which inhibit one another’s
expression. Additionally, PLT expression is con-
trolled by BODENLOS [BDL] and TOPLESS [TPL],
and the microRNA miR165/166 from the lower
tier restricts the accumulation of HD­Zip III mRNA
to the top tier. HD-ZIP III promotes shoot initia-
tion, and PLT promotes root initiation. (B) Left: A
seedling overexpressing PLT protein in the shoot
develops root meristems there instead of a shoot
meristem. Right: Higher magnification of the
boxed area in the left panel shows the presence of
statoliths, a typical root characteristic. (C) A seed-
ling overexpressing HD-Zip III in the lower tier of
the embryo develops a second shoot domain.
(A after J. Palovaara et al. 2016. Annu. Rev. Cell

Figure 22.23 Diverse sources of plant embryos.
In addition to the zygote, many different cells can give rise to
plant embryos. The examples shown here are somatic cells in
the ovule, suspensor cells, microspores, and cultured somatic
cells. (After T. Radoeva and D. Weijers. 2014. Trends Plant
Sci. 19: 709–716.)

often morphologically (nearly) indistinguishable from
sexual, zygote-derived embryos. Embryogenesis can
also be artificially induced. For example, artificial so-
matic embryogenesis, whereby embryos are induced to
form from callus tissue following hormone treatments,
is often used for plant propagation. Likewise, exposure
of microspores to a stress, such as heat, can cause cer-
tain plant species to form embryos. Finally, alternative
embryogenesis can occur within seeds. Suspensor cells,
descendants of the basal cell produced by the first cell
division of the zygote, normally become quiescent after
a few rounds of cell division. If the embryo is damaged,
however, suspensor cells can resume division and form
a second embryo. Thus, given the right triggers, plant
cells of many types can form embryos, attesting to the
tremendous developmental potential of plant cells.

Embryogenesis: The Origin of Plant Architecture 689

 Summary

The sporophyte generation of plants begins with the fer-
tilization events that initiate embryogenesis. Regulated
cell divisions produce the polar axis and bilateral sym-
metry of the embryo. Both mobile and positional sig-
nals function as morphogenic regulators. An extended
set of these regulatory mechanisms functions in the fur-
ther elaboration of plant organs during postembryonic
growth. Postembryonic plants retain meristems (initial
cell niches) that are sites of undifferentiated cell division
to provide for plastic or adaptive growth.

22.1  Embryogenesis in Monocots and

Eudicots

 ■ Embryogenesis differs between monocots and
eudicots but also shares common features.

 ■ Maize (a monocot) and Arabidopsis (a eudicot)

represent opposite ends of a spectrum in embry-
ological development, from the relatively complex
and less predictable to the relatively simple and
more predictable.

22.2 Establishment of Apical–Basal Polarity
 ■ Among seed plants, the apical–basal polarity

is established early in embryogenesis (Figures
22.1–22.5).

22.3 Mechanisms Guiding Embryogenesis
 ■ Position-dependent mechanisms for determining

cell fate guide embryogenesis (Figure 22.8).

 ■ Arabidopsis mutants demonstrate that some pro-
cess other than a fixed sequence of cell divisions
must guide radial pattern formation (Figure 22.9).

 ■ The potential for intercellular protein movement
changes during development (Figure 22.10).

 ■ Screens for embryo lethal and seedling defective

mutants reveal genes that are essential for normal
Arabidopsis embryogenesis (Figures 22.11, 22.12).

22.4 Auxin Signaling During Embryogenesis
 ■ Auxin may function as a mobile chemical signal
during embryogenesis (Figures 22.13, 22.14).

22.5 Radial Patterning During Embryogenesis
 ■ Radial patterning guides formation of tissue layers

(Figure 22.15).

 ■ Two Arabidopsis genes establish normal epidermal

identity (Figure 22.16).

 ■ Different genes establish internal tissues, including
the vasculature and cortex (Figures 22.17–22.20).

22.6  Formation of the Root and Shoot

Apical Meristems

 ■ During embryogenesis, initiation of shoot and root
apical meristems is controlled by a mutually inhibi-
tory interaction of HD-Zip III and PLT transcription
factors (Figures 22.21, 22.22).

 ■ Plant embryos can be derived from various types

of cells (Figure 22.23).
