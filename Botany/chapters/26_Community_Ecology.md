Whichever	aspect	of	community	diversity	we	might	be	study-

areas	are	more	diverse	than	smaller	ones	(FIGURE 26-10).

BOX 26-1  Plants	and	Animals	Are	Different	Kinds	of	Prey

In	 many	 models	 of	 predator–prey	 interactions,	 both	 the
predator	and	the	prey	are	considered	to	be	animals:	One	ani-
mal	eats	another	animal.	But	because	plants	are	the	primary
producers	in	terrestrial	communities,	most	of	the	prey	bio-
mass	and	the	greatest	number	of	prey	individuals	are	plants.
Why	do	models	and	equations	focus	more	on	animals	rather
than	plants?

Plants	 and	 animals	 respond	 differently	 as	 prey.	 Ani-
mals	can	fight	back,	flee,	and	hide,	whereas	plants	can	only
remain	 rooted	 to	 the	 ground,	 perhaps	 protected	 by	 spines
or	 poisons.	 But	 more	 importantly,	 when	 a	 prey	 animal	 is
attacked,	 it	 is	 usually	 killed	 and	 eaten.	 The	 consumer	 may
eat	all	the	prey	animal	or	may	leave	some	for	scavengers,	but
either	way,	the	prey	animal	is	dead	and	gone:	The	number
of	prey	individuals	decreases	by	one.	Even	if	the	prey	animal
somehow	survives	an	initial	attack	with	only	a	wound,	that
will	probably	weaken	it	to	the	point	that	it	will	be	killed	and
eaten	in	the	next	day	or	two.

But	plants	do	things	differently,	and	it	is	difficult	to	kill
a	plant.	If	you	spent	time	gardening	with	your	parents,	you
have	 heard	 “Be	 sure	 to	 pull	 out	 the	 root.”	 Few	 plants	 are
killed	by	ripping	off	some	leaves	or	branches;	even	breaking
off	the	shoot	and	leaving	the	root	in	the	soil	will	not	kill	a
plant	if,	at	the	top	of	the	root,	there	is	a	bit	of	stem	with	an

axillary	bud	that	can	produce	a	new	shoot.	Caterpillars	rou-
tinely	eat	every	leaf	off	a	tree	(they	defoliate	the	tree)	with-
out	really	harming	the	tree;	many	trees	survive	being	com-
pletely	 defoliated	 twice	 in	 the	 same	 year.	 Deer	 browse	 off
young	twigs	and	leaves,	cattle	and	bison	graze	all	the	leaves
off	grasses,	but	none	of	this	ever	kills	the	plant.	If	we	count
only	the	numbers	of	individuals,	thousands	of	herbivorous
consumers	can	prey	on	a	patch	of	plants	for	years	without
the	loss	of	even	a	single	plant.	This	is	very	different	from	one
bobcat	killing	hundreds	of	rodents	during	the	same	time.

To	handle	plants	as	prey,	it	is	usually	necessary	to	have
models	and	equations	based	on	kilograms	of	biomass	instead
of	numbers	of	individuals.	In	some	cases,	it	is	relatively	easy
to	measure	the	amount	of	plant	biomass	lost	during	preda-
tion:	A	control	patch	can	be	mowed	to	collect	leaves,	which
are	then	weighed	and	compared	to	the	experimental	patches.
But	it	is	more	difficult	to	measure	the	weight	or	amount	of
leaves	and	twigs	lost	from	bushes	and	trees,	or	the	number
or	weight	of	small	seeds	lost	to	granivores.	In	addition,	most
plants	are	attacked	by	multiple	predators,	many	of	which	are
easily	 overlooked—aphids,	 fungi,	 and	 nematodes,	 to	 name
just	a	few.	It	is	difficult	to	measure	the	effects	of	grazing	if	we
cannot	quantify	the	harmful	effects	of	other	organisms	that
are	simultaneously	attacking	the	plant.

able	to	persist	in	that	sparse	state	or	even	increase.	If	a	spe-
cies	can	increase	from	very	low	population	density	even	with
its	 competitor	 present,	 then	 that	 species	 can	 be	 invasive
(FIGURE 26-30).	If	its	seeds	or	spores	can	somehow	be	dis-
tributed	to	a	new	community	where	its	competitor	is	already
established,	those	few	seeds	or	spores	will	establish	an	initial
low	population	that	can	survive	and	increase	despite	inter-
specific	competition.

As	 several	 species	 compete	 for	 a	 resource,	 they	 use
that	 resource	 and	 it	 becomes	 more	 scarce.	 Even	 a	 popula-
tion	 of	 just	 one	 species	 growing	 without	 competition	 con-
sumes	resources	and	depletes	them.	One	definition	is	that	a
resource	is	any	substance	or	factor	that	can	lead	to	increased
growth	rates	as	its	availability	is	increased	and	that	is	con-
sumed	 by	 an	 organism.	 Examples	 of	 abiotic	 resources	 are
light,	water,	minerals,	and	space,	and	of	biotic	resources	are
the	attention	of	pollinators,	seed	dispersers,	and	mycorrhizal
fungi.	Factors	such	as	temperature	are	not	resources	because
they	are	not	consumed,	and	typically	no	plant	uses	so	much
carbon	 dioxide	 as	 to	 lower	 its	 atmospheric	 concentration
enough	to	affect	other	plants,	so	even	though	it	is	consumed,
it	is	not	an	item	of	competition.	FIGURE 26-31	shows	that	as

Apparent Competition

Plants	often	face	the	problem	that	many	herbivores	will	eat	a
variety	of	plants.	In	this	case	we	say	that	the	prey	species	share
a	predator.	If	all	prey	populations	are	low,	then	the	popula-
tion	of	the	predator	is	probably	low	also.	If	something	should
cause	the	population	of	one	of	the	prey	species	to	increase,
for	example	if	the	weather	changes	or	mineral	pollution	acts
as	a	fertilizer	in	a	stream,	that	may	cause	the	predator	popu-
lation	to	increase	also.	Even	if	the	predator	prefers	one	spe-
cies	and	consumes	other	plants	only	incidentally,	the	larger
predator	population	results	in	more	herbivory	on	many	plant
species.	Thus	an	increase	in	one	plant	species	is	associated
with	a	decrease	in	others,	and	they	appear	to	be	in	competi-
tion.	Because	the	plants	are	not	actually	competing	for	and
using	a	resource,	this	is	called	apparent competition.

	❚ Beneficial	Interactions

Between	Species

The	various	organisms	within	a	community	often	interact	in
ways	that	are	beneficial.	If	two	organisms	interact	such	that
both	benefit,	that	is	mutualism	or	a	mutualistic relation-
ship.	Familiar	examples	are	pollinators	and	the	plants	they
pollinate.	If	a	plant’s	flowers	absolutely	require	an	animal	to
carry	 out	 pollination,	 then	 that	 plant	 benefits	 greatly	 from
the	 presence	 of	 its	 pollinator	 whereas	 the	 pollinator	 also
benefits	 through	 the	 nectar	 or	 pollen	 it	 consumes	 during
pollination.	 These	 two	 organisms	 are	 not	 preying	 on	 each
other	nor	are	they	competing.	If	one	organism	helps	another
without	receiving	any	benefit,	that	is	a	facilitation,	the	first

organism	facilitates	the	presence	of	the	other.	The	beavers
mentioned	earlier	are	facilitators	by	building	dams	and	cre-
ating	 ponds;	 the	 ponds	 are	 of	 great	 benefit	 to	 many	 plants
and	animals,	but	none	of	those	really	help	the	beavers.

The	concepts	of	mutualism	and	facilitation	are	easy	to
understand	intuitively,	but	whereas	we	have	developed	good
mathematical	 models	 of	 negative	 interactions	 such	 as	 pre-
dation	 and	 competition,	 quantitative	 models	 of	 beneficial
interactions	are	less	well-developed.	As	a	first	step,	we	might
expect	that	the	Lotka-Volterra	model	could	be	changed	from
negative	interactions	to	beneficial	ones	simply	by	changing
certain	negative	signs	in	the	equations	to	positive	signs.	The
result	 is	 often	 that	 such	 equations	 then	 predict	 that	 both
species	 in	 a	 mutualistic	 relationship	 continue	 helping	 each
other	such	that	both	populations	grow	to	infinity.	Obviously,
that	never	happens.	Other	factors	that	are	not	present	in	the
equations	 prevent	 unending,	 infinite	 growth.	 The	 opposite
situation,	 in	 which	 competition	 or	 predation	 drives	 one	 or
both	species	populations	to	zero	occurs	everywhere;	that	is
the	 fundamental	 reason	 why	 a	 community	 may	 be	 rich	 in
species	diversity	but	it	is	never	infinitely	rich.	Competition
and	 predation	 in	 every	 community	 prevent	 thousands	 of
species	from	surviving	in	the	community,	otherwise	virtually
all	species	would	live	everywhere.

Even	 without	 mathematical	 models,	 basic	 concepts	 of
beneficial	relationships	can	be	analyzed.	Certain	mutualistic
interactions	 may	 have	 evolved	 from	 interactions	 that	 were
initially	predation.	Pollination	is	a	good	example.	The	ances-
tors	 of	 angiosperms	 were	 wind-pollinated,	 as	 are	 conifers
and	many	other	plants	even	today.	Insects	would	have	preyed
on	the	ancestral	flowers	by	collecting	and	eating	pollen.	In
many	cases	this	would	simply	be	predation	with	no	benefit
to	the	plant	at	all.	But	at	some	point,	some	insects	began	to
visit	the	ancestral	flowers	regularly	enough	that	insect	pol-
lination	was	as	effective	as	wind	pollination,	and	the	plants
began	 to	 benefit.	 At	 this	 point,	 evolution	 could	 favor	 the
development	 of	 a	 mutualistic	 relationship	 in	 which	 certain
plants	 became	 adapted	 to	 particular	 pollinators	 and	 vice
versa.	Other	mutualistic	relationships	also	may	have	begun
as	predator–prey	relationships,	for	example	nitrogen-fixing
bacteria	 may	 have	 simply	 been	 pathogenic	 bacteria	 before
root	nodules	evolved;	mycorrhizal	fungi	also	may	have	been
pathogens	before	the	mutualistic	exchange	of	phosphate	and
carbohydrates	evolved.

Although	mutualisms	are	beneficial	for	both	organisms,
both	organisms	each	incur	a	cost:	The	relationships	are	not
free.	Flowers	have	the	cost	of	either	producing	nectar	or	los-
ing	pollen	that	is	eaten;	similarly,	plants	must	supply	carbo-
hydrates	to	the	nitrogen-fixing	bacteria	or	to	the	mycorrhizal
fungi.	And	the	same	is	true	in	the	opposite	direction:	Polli-
nators	must	work	flowers	to	obtain	nectar	or	pollen;	bacteria
and	fungi	must	give	up	part	of	their	fixed	nitrogen	or	phos-
phate.	 Natural	 selection	 favors	 organisms	 that	 reduce	 their
costs,	and	in	the	case	of	mutualists,	this	means	that	cheating
can	evolve.	Cheating	here	refers	to	obtaining	benefits	with-
out	 paying	 costs.	 Certain	 nectar-robbing	 bees	 bite	 through
petals	 and	 easily	 obtain	 nectar	 without	 having	 to	 struggle

around	the	stamens	and	becoming	dusted	with	pollen.	The
nectar	robbers	fly	from	flower	to	flower	without	carrying	any
pollen,	 and	 the	 flowers	 lose	 their	 nectar	 without	 receiving
the	 benefit	 of	 pollination.	 The	 ultimate	 fate	 of	 this	 short-
circuited	mutualism	will	depend	on	other	species.	If	no	other
bees	 carry	 out	 pollination,	 then	 the	 flowers	 will	 be	 unable
to	reproduce	and	the	plant	species	will	die	out	in	the	area.
The	nectar-robbing	bees	will	then	be	without	a	food	supply
unless	they	can	switch	to	another	nectar	source.	If	another
species	of	bee	carries	out	enough	pollination	to	ensure	the
plants’	survival,	then	the	nectar-robbing	is	just	another	cost
of	living	for	the	plant,	but	we	might	expect	to	see	the	evo-
lution	of	thicker	petals	that	are	so	tough	the	nectar-robbers
cannot	bite	through	them.

The	 concept	 of	 minimizing	 cost	 is	 also	 demonstrated
in	several	cases	of	mycorrhizal	associations	and	of	symbio-
ses	 between	 root	 nodules	 and	 nitrogen-fixing	 bacteria.	 No
cases	of	cheating	are	known,	but	many	of	the	plant	partners
in	 these	 mutualisms	 are	 able	 to	 break	 off	 the	 relationship
if	the	soil	is	so	rich	that	it	is	less	expensive	for	the	roots	to
obtain	phosphate	or	nitrogenous	compounds	on	their	own.
An	 especially	 sophisticated	 example	 was	 studied	 in	 yellow
bush	 lupine	 (Lupinus  arboreus)	 and	 Rhizobium	 bacteria.
Some	strains	of	Rhizobium	fix	nitrogen	rapidly	and	transfer
relatively	 large	 amounts	 to	 the	 legume	 root;	 others	 fix	 and
transfer	 less.	 Scientists	 grew	 multiple	 plants	 of	 L.  arboreus
and	infected	some	roots	of	each	plant	with	a	high-producing
strain	of	Rhizobium;	other	roots	of	each	were	infected	with
a	 low-producing	 strain.	 Each	 L.  arboreus	 plant	 produced
larger	nodules	on	roots	infected	with	high-yielding	Rhizobia
and	 produced	 only	 smaller	 nodules	 on	 roots	 infected	 with
low-yielding	Rhizobia.	Each	plant	allocated	more	resources
(built	larger	nodules)	where	that	investment	was	gaining	the
greatest	reward.	Mutualistic	relationships	are	often	described
in	rather	human	terms	of	each	organism	helping	the	other,
but	natural	selection	favors	mutations	that	reduce	costs	and
maximize	benefits.	Mutualistic	relationships	will	be	selected
for	only	as	long	as	the	benefits	outweigh	the	costs.

The	concept	of	facilitation	is	a	bit	more	difficult	to	con-
ceptualize.	 It	 has	 been	 defined	 as	 an	 interaction	 in	 which
one	 species	 alters	 the	 environment	 in	 a	 way	 that	 enhances
the	 survival	 and	 reproduction	 of	 a	 second,	 neighboring
species.	Nurse plants	are	an	example;	these	are	plants	that
alter	 a	 small	 area	 of	 habitat	 immediately	 below	 themselves
such	that	it	is	more	favorable	to	the	survival	of	seedlings	of
other	 plants	 as	 compared	 to	 other	 nearby	 areas	 not	 below
the	nurse	plant.	An	example	that	is	frequently	cited	is	spiny
desert	shrubs	(FIGURES 26-33	and 26-34).	Seeds	that	ger-
minate	 beneath	 the	 nurse	 plant	 are	 more	 likely	 to	 survive
because	 the	 nurse	 plant	 provides	 shade	 as	 well	 as	 protec-
tion	 from	 herbivores	 that	 are	 deterred	 by	 the	 nurse	 plant’s
spines.	Seeds	that	germinate	just	a	meter	or	two	away	from	a
nurse	plant	may	be	located	in	full,	harsh	desert	sunlight	and
have	no	protection	from	herbivores.	After	a	seedling	under
a	nurse	plant	becomes	established,	it	may	grow	so	large	that
it	 emerges	 above	 the	 nurse	 plant,	 dwarfing	 it	 and	 perhaps

If	all	individuals	of	a	species	were	able	to	interact	freely,	we
would	 speak	 of	 just	 one	 population	 rather	 than	 of	 several.
The	 world	 is	 patchy	 and	 heterogeneous;	 it	 contains	 many
areas	suitable	for	any	particular	species,	but	those	areas	are
physically	and	geographically	separated	from	each	other.	For
example,	hardwood	forests	suitable	for	maples	are	widespread
but	separated	into	numerous	discrete	forests;	the	maples	in
one	constitute	a	population	distinct	from	the	populations	of
maples	in	other	forests.	If	several	local	populations	are	inter-
connected	by	migration	and	gene	flow	between	the	patches,
the	local	populations	constitute	a	metapopulation.	In	con-
servation	 biology,	 with	 the	 goal	 of	 preserving	 endangered
species,	the	concept	of	metapopulations	is	crucial.	We	must
consider	all	the	populations	of	a	species,	not	just	one	or	two
in	which	a	species	appears	to	be	doing	well.

A	 common	 model	 of	 metapopulations	 makes	 four

assumptions:

1.	 A	region	of	the	environment	is	composed	of	many	dis-

crete	patches	in	which	the	species	can	live.

2.	 Some	patches	are	occupied	by	the	species	whereas	other

The	simplest	communities	typically	have	at	least	three	tro-
phic	levels:	primary	producers	(plants,	algae,	cyanobacteria),
primary	consumers	(herbivores),	and	secondary	consumers
(carnivores).	For	example,	a	prairie	might	consist	of	several
species	of	grasses	and	herbs	that	are	fed	upon	by	mice	and
deer,	which	in	turn	are	prey	to	bobcats	or	coyotes.	If	environ-
mental	conditions	are	very	harsh,	as	in	deserts	or	high	alpine
areas,	 the	 populations	 of	 primary	 produces	 are	 sparse	 and
consequently	the	populations	of	herbivores	will	be	sparse	as
well.	With	so	few	herbivores,	only	a	single	species	or	two	of
carnivores	 can	 be	 supported,	 and	 the	 community	 has	 only
three	trophic	levels.	In	areas	with	better	conditions	and	more
luxuriant	plant	growth,	enough	energy	may	be	available	to
support	numerous	species	of	herbivores	and	omnivores,	and
in	 turn	 there	 may	 be	 high	 species	 diversity	 of	 carnivores,
some	of	which	prey	only	on	other	carnivores.	Such	a	com-
munity	has	more	than	three	trophic	levels.

We	 can	 study	 species	 interactions	 across	 trophic	 lev-
els	in	several	ways.	The	simplest	is	to	choose	one	predator,
then	 identify	 its	 main	 prey	 and	 the	 plant	 species	 that	 sup-
ports	them.	This	direct	line	of	consumption	would	be	a	food
chain.	But	most	carnivores	eat	a	variety	of	prey,	and	those
prey	may	be	herbivores,	or	omnivores,	or	other	carnivores.
Similarly,	 most	 prey	 species	 will	 be	 threatened	 by	 multiple
carnivores.	If	we	trace	all	the	prey	of	the	top	carnivores,	and
then	 trace	 the	 food	 sources	 of	 each	 of	 those	 prey	 species
and	so	on,	we	obtain	a	network	of	numerous	interrelation-
ships	called	a	food web.	At	the	first	stage	of	our	study	we
might	merely	attempt	to	make	a	map	of	who	feeds	on	whom.
But	each	of	the	organisms	have	favorite	food	sources,	so	we
might	 attempt	 to	 map	 out	 which	 herbivores	 consume	 the
most	energy,	and	in	turn	which	are	eaten	the	most,	etc.,	so
as	to	trace	how	energy	flows	through	the	community.	This
would	 be	 an	 energy flow web;	 it	 is	 extremely	 difficult	 to
construct	energy	flow	webs	for	real	communities.

It	is	common	to	see	diagrams	of	food	webs	and	energy
flow	 webs	 in	 textbooks	 of	 biology	 and	 ecology.	 But	 in	 fact
those	 are	 primarily	 just	 for	 illustrating	 relationships	 and
most	have	severe	problems.	The	majority	of	studies	of	food

webs	are	very	incomplete,	giving	their	primary	attention	to
large	familiar	animals	such	as	carnivorous	mammals,	birds,
fish,	and	reptiles;	less	attention	is	given	to	herbivorous	mam-
mals	 such	 as	 deer	 and	 mice,	 and	 little	 attention	 is	 paid	 to
plants.	However,	a	few	studies	have	shown	that	invertebrates
such	as	ticks,	lice,	flies,	mosquitoes,	and	caterpillars	make	up
as	much	as	50%	of	the	biomass	of	a	community.	But	typically
these	are	never	studied;	they	are	not	even	identified	to	genus
or	species.	Similarly,	studies	of	aquatic	food	webs	often	iden-
tify	the	primary	producers	merely	as	“algae”	with	no	effort
given	to	distinguishing	one	species	from	another.

It	 is	 difficult	 to	 trust	 food	 web	 studies	 knowing	 that
they	are	so	incomplete,	but	a	few	cautious	conclusions	can
be	drawn.	Most	communities	have	a	small	number	of	strong
food	chains	through	which	most	energy	flows	and	a	much
larger	 number	 of	 other	 food	 chains	 involving	 much	 less
energy.	However,	the	abundance	of	low	energy	interactions
among	 many	 organisms	 tends	 to	 stabilize	 communities.	 In
general,	 communities	 with	 high	 diversity	 seem	 to	 be	 more
stable	than	those	with	low	diversity.

The	 incompleteness	 of	 many	 food	 web	 studies	 appears
to	be	less	of	a	problem	than	we	would	expect	at	first.	Certain
species	can	be	removed	and	seem	to	have	little	effect	on	most
other	 members	 of	 the	 community;	 consequently,	 detailed
studies	 of	 most	 members	 of	 a	 food	 web	 would	 not	 provide
much	useful	information.	In	contrast,	the	presence	or	absence
of	 certain	 species,	 called	 keystone  species,	 dramatically
affects	the	structure	to	their	community.	A	keystone	species
has	an	impact	out	of	proportion	to	its	size	or	the	number	of
individuals	 present.	 For	 comparison,	 a	 dominant	 species,
such	as	a	particular	grass	of	a	prairie	or	a	tree	of	a	forest,	will
have	 a	 strong	 impact	 on	 the	 structure	 of	 a	 community,	 but
that	impact	is	related	to	its	abundance.	It	is	easy	to	recognize
the	dominant	species	of	a	community,	but	it	is	very	difficult
to	identify	a	keystone	species	without	actually	removing	the
species	to	examine	how	much	it	affects	community	structure.
A	 classic	 example	 of	 a	 community	 with	 a	 keystone
species	 is	 seen	 in	 the	 relationships	 between	 sea	 otters,	 sea
urchins,	 and	 kelp	 “forests”	 (FIGURES  26-40  and	 26-41).
Sea	otters	were	hunted	almost	to	extinction	in	the	northern
Pacific	Ocean,	and	were	finally	given	protection	from	hunt-
ing	in	1911.	Sea	urchins	are	a	favorite	food	of	sea	otters,	and
during	the	time	while	sea	otters	were	hunted	to	low	popula-
tions,	sea	urchins	thrived	due	to	low	predation.	They	would
scour	rocky	coasts,	eating	various	algae,	especially	sporelings
of	kelp	(Macrocystis).	During	a	trip	to	the	shore,	one	would
encounter	 numerous	 urchins	 in	 a	 barren,	 desolate	 waste-
land.	But	after	1911,	sea	 otter	numbers	 increased	and	they
reduced	the	populations	of	sea	urchins.	With	urchin	grazing
minimized,	kelp	spores	could	germinate	and	grow	into	giant
plants,	creating	extensive,	dense	underwater	forests.	A	kelp
forest,	much	like	a	terrestrial	forest,	is	a	complex	habitat;	fish
can	feed	and	hide	among	the	kelps,	epiphytic	algae	can	attach
to	kelp,	and	so	on.	Sea	otters	are	a	keystone	species:	By	pro-
tecting	them	and	letting	their	numbers	rise,	sea	urchins	were
reduced,	 and	 the	 coastal	 communities	 became	 much	 more
productive,	much	richer,	and	the	number	of	species	present

Treaties	to	protect	endangered	species	often	do	more	harm
than	good.	In	1975,	the	Convention	on	International	Trade
in	 Endangered	 Species	 (CITES)	 was	 proposed	 and	 ratified
by	 many	 nations.	 This	 treaty	 has	 a	 noble	 purpose:	 to	 con-
trol,	and	in	many	cases	to	completely	eliminate,	international
sales	of	endangered	species.	Until	1975,	there	was	an	almost
completely	open	market	on	animals	and	plants,	whether	they
were	as	abundant	as	bananas	or	as	rare	as	a	black	rhinoceros.
Live	animals	could	be	captured	and	then	sold	to	zoos	any-
where	in	the	world,	or	much	worse	and	much	more	profit-
able,	they	could	be	sold	to	private	“game	ranches.”	Hunters	on
these	ranches	could	pay	to	hunt	and	kill	elephants,	giraffes,
monkeys—anything.	An	even	larger	problem	is	that	various
animal	organs	are	highly	valued:	elephant	ivory,	rhinoceros
horn,	hides,	and	internal	organs.	Some	were	valued	for	their
beauty,	 others	 for	 reputed	 medicinal	 properties,	 and	 still
others	were	believed	to	be	either	aphrodisiacs	or	enhancers
of	 male	 virility.	 Unknown	 numbers	 of	 animals	 were	 killed,
butchered	for	parts	in	the	field,	and	the	corpses	left	to	rot.

The	goal	of	CITES	was	to	regulate	this	trade.	Some	ani-
mals	were	judged	to	be	sufficiently	plentiful	that	some	“har-
vesting”	 could	 be	 allowed;	 others	 were	 so	 endangered	 that
they	were	to	be	left	unharmed	and	the	harvesting	quota	was
set	at	zero.	Individual	countries	tried	to	control	this,	but	if
some	countries	still	allowed	these	to	be	purchased,	smugglers
would	find	a	means	of	obtaining	the	animals	from	their	hab-
itat.	With	CITES,	all	countries	agreed	to	control	trafficking
in	endangered	species,	so	if	illegal	products	are	discovered	in
cargo	anywhere,	they	can	be	seized.	This	makes	smuggling
much	more	difficult,	but	of	course	it	still	exists;	it	is	common
for	game	wardens	in	Africa	to	find	corpses	of	elephants	with-
out	tusks	and	rhinoceroses	with	their	horns	cut	off.

Unfortunately,	 the	 people	 who	 wrote	 CITES	 decided
to	protect	plants	but	without	taking	into	consideration	that
plants	are	different	from	animals.	CITES	controls	the	inter-
national	trade	in	whole	individuals	and	also	their	part	(hides,
horns,	tusks,	testicles)	because	harvesting	parts	usually	kills
the	animals.	But	plants	are	different:	Harvesting	a	portion	of
a	plant’s	seeds	does	not	harm	the	plant	or	the	seed	crop,	and
for	many	plants,	it	is	safe	to	take	cuttings	that	can	 later	be
rooted	and	grown	as	a	new	clone	of	the	original	plant.	In	fact,
by	 collecting	 a	 portion	 of	 the	 propagules,	 the	 endangered
species	 can	 be	 propagated	 in	 botanical	 gardens	 or	 planted
in	new	safe	areas,	making	the	species	a	bit	less	endangered.
Unfortunately,	 CITES	 does	 not	 recognize	 this:	 Both	 whole
individuals	and	their	parts	are	banned,	for	both	animals	and
plants.

CITES	 has	 greatly	 hindered	 the	 legitimate	 work	 and
research	of	zoologists	and	botanists.	Prior	to	CITES,	a	biolo-
gist	would	usually	work	in	cooperation	with	local	biologists
to	explore	remote,	poorly	known	parts	of	the	world.	Part	of
the	research	would	be	to	collect	specimens	for	propagation
in	university	research	gardens.	If	sufficiently	plentiful,	some
living	material	would	be	given	to	professional	propagators—
nurseryman—who	often	could	turn	a	few	seeds	or	bulbs	into
tens	 of	 thousands	 of	 plants	 that	 could	 be	 sold	 worldwide.
Many	people	could	enjoy	the	new,	exotic	species	without	any
harm	to	the	original	plants.

CITES	 has	 a	 provision	 for	 scientific	 collectors	 but	 it
is	 difficult	 to	 use.	 A	 person	 going	 from	 the	 United	 States
to	 another	 country	 to	 study	 biology,	 for	 example	 to	 Latin
America,	Africa,	or	Southeast	Asia,	must	apply	for	permits
both	from	the	U.S.	and	from	the	host	country.	The	applica-
tion	must	list	the	exact	number	of	individuals	of	each	spe-
cies	to	be	collected—a	difficult	task	when	exploring	for	new
undiscovered,	unnamed	species.	Until	recently	many	of	the
less-developed	 countries	 did	 not	 have	 an	 agency,	 not	 even
a	single	 agent,	 who	could	 approve	 such	 an	 application	 and
provide	the	needed	permit.

Another	 problem	 with	 CITES	 and	 plants	 is	 that	 bor-
der	 inspection	 agents	 can	 quickly	 learn	 to	 recognize	 an
elephant	 tusk,	 a	 rhinoceros	 horn,	 a	 leopard	 hide,	 but	 they
cannot	 quickly	 learn	 which	 of	 the	 hundreds	 of	 orchids	 are
endangered	and	which	are	plentiful,	so	all	orchid	species	are
treated	as	endangered	and	require	permits.	The	same	is	true
of	cacti	and	bromeliads.

Additional	trouble	has	been	caused	by	the	convention
on	biological	diversity	that	was	adopted	in	Rio	de	Janeiro	in
1992.	The	background	of	this	treaty	is	that	we	had	come	to
appreciate	the	value	of	medicinal	plants:	If	aspirin,	vinblas-
tine,	and	Taxol	could	be	extracted	from	plants,	what	other
life-saving	 drugs	 might	 exist	 in	 the	 untested,	 unknown
plants	 of	 the	 world?	 Also,	 animal	 venoms	 often	 kill	 by
interfering	with	nerve	metabolism,	but	in	the	proper	doses
they	 give	 us	 an	 amazing	 control	 over	 various	 nerve	 func-
tions.	 Teams	 of	 biologists	 were	 sent	 out	 across	 all	 parts	 of
the	world	to	obtain	plant	and	animal	samples	to	be	tested
for	effectiveness	against	many	diseases.	Obviously,	the	best
place	to	start	is	to	talk	to	the	local	healer	(curandero,	witch
doctor)	of	a	village.	Unfortunately,	this	exploration	came	at
a	time	when	many	countries	felt	they	were	being	treated	as
second-class	citizens	in	the	world:	Americans	and	Europe-
ans	 would	 arrive,	 collect	 samples,	 mine	 for	 minerals,	 drill
for	 oil,	 pay	 a	 small	 amount	 and	 then	 later	 sell	 expensive

accelerated.	 Millions	 of	 square	 kilometers	 of	 forests	 and

farming	or	for	housing	kills	all	the	organisms	that	had	been
present,	whereas	biological	exploration	and	collecting	would
have	saved	some	individuals.	Both	CITES	and	the	Rio	Con-

At the Next Level

1.	 Succession  and  pioneer  species.	 Until	 12,000	 years	 ago,
all	of	Canada	and	much	of	the	northern	United	States
was	covered	by	the	Laurentide	Ice	Sheet.	This	was	more
than	 a	 mile	 thick	 in	 many	 places,	 and	 actually	 caused
Earth’s	crust	under	Canada	to	sink	downward.	Since	the
beginning	 of	 the	 Holocene	 warming	 12,000	 years	 ago,
the	ice	sheet	retreated,	but	this	has	been	patchy	because
ice	retreated	at	different	times	in	different	places:	11,000
years	ago	in	the	lowlands,	more	recently	as	glaciers	con-
tinue	to	retreat	in	the	mountains.	Consequently	the	ages
of	 communities	 in	 Canada	 and	 the	 mountains	 of	 the
western	U.S.	vary	between	12,000	years	and	zero	years
(at	the	foot	of	a	retreating	glacier	today).	It	is	possible	to
see	older	stages	of	succession	as	one	walks	down	a	val-
ley	formerly	occupied	by	a	retreating	glacier.	Succession
in	pioneer	species	has	been	studied	extensively	in	these
areas,	and	you	should	find	many	good	summaries	and
original	research	papers.

2.	 Sky islands.	The	communities	located	on	cool	mountain
peaks	usually	differ	greatly	from	those	in	the	warm	low-
lands	between	mountains.	When	Earth	is	very	cool,	the
communities	typical	of	mountain	peaks	may	be	able	to
survive	in	the	lowlands,	allowing	communities	of	adja-
cent	mountain	peaks	to	interact	and	exchange	species.
Species	 normally	 confined	 to	 the	 cold	 high	 latitudes
(far	 north	 in	 the	 northern	 hemisphere)	 may	 migrate
far	toward	the	equator.	When	Earth	warms	again,	many
species	survive	by	occupying	higher	and	higher	altitudes
on	nearby	mountains.	As	result,	mountain	tops	near	the

will	the	density	of	other	granivores	increase	compensa-

remains	the	same)?	Or	will	changes	in	the	density	of	one

been	resolved,	and	may	not	be	the	same	in	all	communi-

4.	 Metapopulations and conservation biology.	This	chapter

SUMMARY

1.	 A	community	is	a	group	of	species	that	occur	together	at

the	same	time	and	place.

2.	 Many	communities	undergo	a	more-or-less	predictable

sequence	of	changes	called	succession.

3.	 A	 community	 that	 remains	 more	 or	 less	 the	 same	 for

many	years	is	a	climax	community.

4.	 We	 humans	 are	 members	 of	 many	 communities,	 and
we	contribute	to	habitat	loss,	habitat	fragmentation,	and
overharvesting,	 but	 we	 also	 contribute	 to	 community
restoration	and	resource	conservation.

5.	 When	studying	community	diversity,	the	size	of	the	area
being	studied,	called	its	scale,	is	important;	larger	areas
have	more	diversity	than	smaller	areas.

6.	 In	 most	 communities,	 plants	 are	 the	 most	 important
primary	 producers,	 and	 they	 are	 the	 prey	 for	 herbivo-
rous	and	omnivorous	predators.

7.	 The	paradox	of	enrichment	is	a	phenomenon	in	which
fertilizing	 or	 enriching	 a	 community	 may	 lead	 to	 the
loss	 of	 diversity	 rather	 than	 an	 increase	 in	 abundance
of	all	species.

8.	 Fixed	effort	harvesting	is	a	technique	that	automatically
decreases	harvests	whenever	a	resource	is	in	short	sup-
ply	or	is	endangered.

9.	 Multiple	 species	 can	 compete	 with	 each	 other	 for	 a
resource;	 if	 they	 actually	 consume	 the	 resource	 and
reduce	 it,	 it	 is	 exploitation	 competition.	 If	 one	 merely
interferes	with	access	to	the	resource	by	other	species,	it
is	interference	competition.

10.	 Species	of	a	community	may	interact	such	that	one	ben-
efits	(facilitation)	or	such	that	both	benefit	(mutualism).
11.	 Populations	typically	are	separated	by	regions	of	unsuit-
able	habitat;	if	species	can	migrate	between	the	various
populations,	they	all	constitute	a	metapopulation.
12.	 Metapopulation	 ecology	 is	 important	 when	 planning
the	 establishment	 of	 wildlife	 preserves;	 incorporating
migration	corridors	may	be	essential.

13.	 Food	webs	and	energy	flow	webs	are	the	predator–prey
relationships	within	 a	community,	 but	 studies	 of	these
webs	are	not	able	to	incorporate	or	measure	every	pos-
sible	relationship.
